¶1

Every expression of the life of the human spirit can be conceived as a kind of language, and this conception, in the manner of a true method, everywhere opens up new questions. One can speak of a language of music and of sculpture, of a language of justice that has nothing immediately to do with the languages in which German or English legal verdicts are drawn up, of a language of technology that is not the technicians' professional idiom. Language means, in such contexts, the principle, directed toward the imparting of quintessential contents, at work in the domains concerned: in technology, art, justice, or religion. In a word: every imparting of quintessential contents is language, imparting by the word being only a particular case, that of the human, and of what underlies it or is founded upon it (justice, poetry). But the existence of language extends not only over all the regions of the human spirit's expression, in which language in some sense always dwells; it extends to absolutely everything. There is no event or thing, in animate or inanimate nature alike, that does not in some way partake of language, for it is essential to each to impart its quintessential content. But the word "language," so used, is by no means a metaphor. For it is a cognition full of content that we can imagine nothing that does not impart its quintessence in expression; the greater or lesser degree of consciousness with which such imparting is apparently (or really) bound up can change nothing in this: that we can imagine the complete absence of language in nothing. An existence that stood wholly without relation to language is an idea; but this idea cannot be made fruitful even within the precinct of ideas whose circumference traces the idea of God.

Jede Äußerung menschlichen Geisteslebens kann als eine Art der Sprache aufgefaßt werden, und diese Auffassung erschließt nach Art einer wahrhaften Methode überall neue Fragestellungen. Man kann von einer Sprache der Musik und der Plastik reden, von einer Sprache der Justiz, die nichts mit denjenigen, in denen deutsche oder englische Rechtssprüche abgefaßt sind, unmittelbar zu tun hat, von einer Sprache der Technik, die nicht die Fachsprache der Techniker ist. Sprache bedeutet in solchem Zusammenhang das auf Mitteilung geistiger Inhalte gerichtete Prinzip in den betreffenden Gegenständen: in Technik, Kunst, Justiz oder Religion. Mit einem Wort: jede Mitteilung geistiger Inhalte ist Sprache, wobei die Mitteilung durch das Wort nur ein besonderer Fall, der der menschlichen, und der ihr zugrunde liegenden oder auf ihr fundierten (Justiz, Poesie), ist. Das Dasein der Sprache erstreckt sich aber nicht nur über alle Gebiete menschlicher Geistesäußerung, der in irgendeinem Sinn immer Sprache innewohnt, sondern es erstreckt sich auf schlechthin alles. Es gibt kein Geschehen oder Ding weder in der belebten noch in der unbelebten Natur, das nicht in gewisser Weise an der Sprache teilhätte, denn es ist jedem wesentlich, seinen geistigen Inhalt mitzuteilen. Eine Metapher aber ist das Wort »Sprache« in solchem Gebrauche durchaus nicht. Denn es ist eine volle inhaltliche Erkenntnis, daß wir uns nichts vorstellen können, das sein geistiges Wesen nicht im Ausdruck mitteilt; der größere oder geringere Bewußtseinsgrad, mit dem solche Mitteilung scheinbar (oder wirklich) verbunden ist, kann daran nichts ändern, daß wir uns völlige Abwesenheit der Sprache in nichts vorstellen können. Ein Dasein, welches ganz ohne Beziehung zur Sprache wäre, ist eine Idee; aber diese Idee läßt sich auch im Bezirk der Ideen, deren Umkreis diejenige Gottes bezeichnet, nicht fruchtbar machen.

¶2

Only this much is correct: that in this terminology every expression, insofar as it is an imparting of quintessential contents, is counted as language. And indeed expression, by its whole and innermost essence, is to be understood only as language; on the other hand, to understand a linguistic essence one must always ask of which quintessence it is the immediate expression. That is to say: the German language, for example, is by no means the expression of everything that we can, supposedly, express through it; rather it is the immediate expression of that which imparts itself in it. This "itself" is a quintessence. It is therefore self-evident at the outset that the quintessence which imparts itself in language is not language itself but something to be distinguished from it. The view that the quintessence of a thing consists precisely in its language — this view, understood as hypothesis, is the great abyss into which all theory of language threatens to fall,1 and to keep itself hovering over that abyss, precisely over it, is that theory's task. The distinction between the quintessence and the linguistic essence in which it imparts is the most primordial in any inquiry into the theory of language; and this distinction seems so undoubtable that it is, rather, the frequently asserted identity of quintessence and linguistic essence that constitutes a deep and incomprehensible paradox, whose expression has been found in the double sense of the word λόγος. Nevertheless this paradox, as a solution, has its place at the center of the theory of language, but remains a paradox, and insoluble, where it stands at the beginning.

Nur soviel ist richtig, daß in dieser Terminologie jeder Ausdruck, sofern er eine Mitteilung geistiger Inhalte ist, der Sprache beigezählt wird. Und allerdings ist der Ausdruck seinem ganzen und innersten Wesen nach nur als Sprache zu verstehen; andererseits muß man, um ein sprachliches Wesen zu verstehen, immer fragen, für welches geistige Wesen es denn der unmittelbare Ausdruck sei. Das heißt: die deutsche Sprache z. B. ist keineswegs der Ausdruck für alles, was wir durch sie – vermeintlich – ausdrücken können, sondern sie ist der unmittelbare Ausdruck dessen, was sich in ihr mitteilt. Dieses »Sich« ist ein geistiges Wesen. Damit ist es zunächst selbstverständlich, daß das geistige Wesen, das sich in der Sprache mitteilt, nicht die Sprache selbst, sondern etwas von ihr zu Unterscheidendes ist. Die Ansicht, daß das geistige Wesen eines Dinges eben in seiner Sprache besteht – diese Ansicht als Hypothesis verstanden, ist der große Abgrund, dem alle Sprachtheorie zu verfallen droht1, und über, gerade über ihm sich schwebend zu erhalten ist ihre Aufgabe. Die Unterscheidung zwischen dem geistigen Wesen und dem sprachlichen, in dem es mitteilt, ist die ursprünglichste in einer sprachtheoretischen Untersuchung, und es scheint dieser Unterschied so unzweifelhaft zu sein, daß vielmehr die oft behauptete Identität zwischen dem geistigen und sprachlichen Wesen eine tiefe und unbegreifliche Paradoxie bildet, deren Ausdruck man in dem Doppelsinn des Wortes Λόγος gefunden hat. Dennoch hat diese Paradoxie als Lösung ihre Stelle im Zentrum der Sprachtheorie, bleibt aber Paradoxie und da unlösbar, wo sie am Anfang steht.

¶3

What does language impart? It imparts the quintessence that corresponds to it. It is fundamental to know that this quintessence imparts itself in language and not through language. There is, then, no speaker of languages, if by that one means someone who imparts himself through these languages. Quintessence imparts itself in, and not through, a language — that is: it is not, from outside, equal to the linguistic essence. The quintessence is identical with the linguistic essence only insofar as it is impartable. What is impartable in a quintessence, that is its linguistic essence. Language therefore imparts the particular linguistic essence of things, but their quintessence only insofar as it lies immediately enclosed within the linguistic, insofar as it is impartable.

Was teilt die Sprache mit? Sie teilt das ihr entsprechende geistige Wesen mit. Es ist fundamental zu wissen, daß dieses geistige Wesen sich in der Sprache mitteilt und nicht durch die Sprache. Es gibt also keinen Sprecher der Sprachen, wenn man damit den meint, der durch diese Sprachen sich mitteilt. Das geistige Wesen teilt sich in und nicht durch eine Sprache mit – das heißt: es ist nicht von außen gleich dem sprachlichen Wesen. Das geistige Wesen ist mit dem sprachlichen identisch, nur sofern es mitteilbar ist. Was an einem geistigen Wesen mitteilbar ist, das ist sein sprachliches Wesen. Die Sprache teilt also das jeweilige sprachliche Wesen der Dinge mit, ihr geistiges aber nur, sofern es unmittelbar im sprachlichen beschlossen liegt, sofern es mitteilbar ist.

¶4

Language imparts the linguistic essence of things. Its clearest appearance, however, is language itself. The answer to the question, what does language impart, is therefore: every language imparts itself. The language of this lamp, for example, imparts not the lamp (for the quintessence of the lamp, insofar as it is impartable, is by no means the lamp itself) but: the language-lamp, the lamp in imparting, the lamp in expression. For in language the case is this: the linguistic essence of things is their language. The understanding of the theory of language depends on bringing this sentence to a clarity that annihilates in it even every semblance of tautology. This sentence is untautological, for it means: what is impartable in a quintessence is its language. On this "is" (equivalent to "is immediately") everything rests. — Not that what is impartable in a quintessence appears most clearly in its language, as was said just now by way of transition; rather, this impartable is immediately language itself. Or: the language of a quintessence is immediately that which is impartable in it. What is impartable in a quintessence, in this it imparts itself; that is to say: every language imparts itself. Or more precisely: every language imparts itself in itself; it is, in the purest sense, the "medium" of imparting. Mediality, which is the immediacy of all quintessential imparting, is the fundamental problem of the theory of language, and if one wishes to call this immediacy magical, then the primal problem of language is its magic. At the same time, the phrase "the magic of language" points to something else: to its infinity. This is conditioned by the immediacy. For precisely because nothing imparts itself through language, what imparts itself in language cannot be limited or measured from outside; and therefore each language is indwelt by its own incommensurable, uniquely constituted infinity. Its linguistic essence, not its verbal contents, marks its boundary.

Die Sprache teilt das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge mit. Dessen klarste Erscheinung ist aber die Sprache selbst. Die Antwort auf die Frage: was teilt die Sprache mit? lautet also: Jede Sprache teilt sich selbst mit. Die Sprache dieser Lampe z. B. teilt nicht die Lampe mit (denn das geistige Wesen der Lampe, sofern es mitteilbar ist, ist durchaus nicht die Lampe selbst), sondern: die Sprach-Lampe, die Lampe in der Mitteilung, die Lampe im Ausdruck. Denn in der Sprache verhält es sich so: Das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge ist ihre Sprache. Das Verständnis der Sprachtheorie hängt davon ab, diesen Satz zu einer Klarheit zu bringen, die auch jeden Schein einer Tautologie in ihm vernichtet. Dieser Satz ist untautologisch, denn er bedeutet: das, was an einem geistigen Wesen mitteilbar ist, ist seine Sprache. Auf diesem »ist« (gleich »ist unmittelbar«) beruht alles. – Nicht, was an einem geistigen Wesen mitteilbar ist, erscheint am klarsten in seiner Sprache, wie noch eben im Übergange gesagt wurde, sondern dieses Mitteilbare ist unmittelbar die Sprache selbst. Oder: die Sprache eines geistigen Wesens ist unmittelbar dasjenige, was an ihm mitteilbar ist. Was an einem geistigen Wesen mitteilbar ist, in dem teilt es sich mit; das heißt: jede Sprache teilt sich selbst mit. Oder genauer: jede Sprache teilt sich in sich selbst mit, sie ist im reinsten Sinne das »Medium« der Mitteilung. Das Mediale, das ist die Unmittelbarkeit aller geistigen Mitteilung, ist das Grundproblem der Sprachtheorie, und wenn man diese Unmittelbarkeit magisch nennen will, so ist das Urproblem der Sprache ihre Magie. Zugleich deutet das Wort von der Magie der Sprache auf ein anderes: auf ihre Unendlichkeit. Sie ist durch die Unmittelbarkeit bedingt. Denn gerade, weil durch die Sprache sich nichts mitteilt, kann, was in der Sprache sich mitteilt, nicht von außen beschränkt oder gemessen werden, und darum wohnt jeder Sprache ihre inkommensurable einziggeartete Unendlichkeit inne. Ihr sprachliches Wesen, nicht ihre verbalen Inhalte bezeichnen ihre Grenze.

¶5

The linguistic essence of things is their language; this sentence, applied to man, says: the linguistic essence of man is his language. That is: man imparts his own quintessence in his language. But the language of man speaks in words. Man therefore imparts his own quintessence (insofar as it is impartable) by naming all other things. But do we know other languages that name things? Let no one object that we know no language besides the language of man; that is untrue. Only, we know no naming language besides the human one; by identifying naming language with language as such, the theory of language robs itself of its deepest insights. — The linguistic essence of man, then, is this: that he names things.

Das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge ist ihre Sprache; dieser Satz auf den Menschen angewandt besagt: Das sprachliche Wesen des Menschen ist seine Sprache. Das heißt: Der Mensch teilt sein eignes geistiges Wesen in seiner Sprache mit. Die Sprache des Menschen spricht aber in Worten. Der Mensch teilt also sein eignes geistiges Wesen (sofern es mitteilbar ist) mit, indem er alle anderen Dinge benennt. Kennen wir aber noch andere Sprachen, welche die Dinge benennen? Man wende nicht ein, wir kennten keine Sprache außer der des Menschen, das ist unwahr. Nur keine benennende Sprache kennen wir außer der menschlichen; mit einer Identifizierung von benennender Sprache mit Sprache überhaupt beraubt sich die Sprachtheorie der tiefsten Einsichten. – Das sprachliche Wesen des Menschen ist also, daß er die Dinge benennt:

¶6

Why name? To whom does man impart himself? — But is this question, in man's case, other than in the case of other impartings (languages)? To whom does the lamp impart itself? The mountain range? The fox? — Here, however, the answer runs: to man. This is no anthropomorphism. The truth of this answer proves itself in knowledge, and perhaps also in art. Moreover: if lamp and mountains and the fox did not impart themselves to man, how then should he name them? But he does name them; he imparts himself by naming them. To whom does he impart himself?

Wozu benennt? Wem teilt der Mensch sich mit? – Aber ist diese Frage beim Menschen eine andere als bei anderen Mitteilungen (Sprachen)? Wem teilt die Lampe sich mit? Das Gebirge? Der Fuchs? – Hier aber lautet die Antwort: dem Menschen. Das ist kein Anthropomorphismus. Die Wahrheit dieser Antwort erweist sich in der Erkenntnis und vielleicht auch in der Kunst. Zudem: wenn Lampe und Gebirge und der Fuchs sich dem Menschen nicht mitteilen würden, wie sollte er sie dann benennen? Aber er benennt sie; er teilt sich mit, indem er sie benennt. Wem teilt er sich mit?

¶7

Before this question can be answered, it must once more be examined: how does man impart himself? A deep distinction is to be drawn, an alternative posed, before which the essentially false opinion of language unfailingly betrays itself. Does man impart his quintessence through the names that he gives to things? Or in them? In the paradox of this way of posing the question lies its answer. Whoever believes that man imparts his quintessence through names cannot in turn assume that it is his quintessence that he imparts — for that does not happen through names of things, that is, through the words by which he designates a thing. And he can in turn only assume that he imparts factual subject matter to other men, for that does happen through the word by which I designate a thing. This view is the bourgeois conception of language, whose untenability and emptiness shall become clear, with mounting distinctness, in what follows. It holds: the means of imparting is the word, its object the subject matter, its addressee a human being. The other conception, by contrast, knows no means, no object, and no addressee of imparting. It holds: in the name, the quintessence of man imparts itself to God.

Ehe diese Frage zu beantworten ist, gilt es noch einmal zu prüfen: Wie teilt der Mensch sich mit? Es ist ein tiefer Unterschied zu machen, eine Alternative zu stellen, vor der mit Sicherheit die wesentlich falsche Meinung von der Sprache sich verrät. Teilt der Mensch sein geistiges Wesen durch die Namen mit, die er den Dingen gibt? Oder in ihnen? In der Paradoxie dieser Fragestellung liegt ihre Beantwortung. Wer da glaubt, der Mensch teile sein geistiges Wesen durch die Namen mit, der kann wiederum nicht annehmen, daß es sein geistiges Wesen sei, das er mitteile, – denn das geschieht nicht durch Namen von Dingen, also durch Worte, durch die er ein Ding bezeichnet. Und er kann wiederum nur annehmen, er teile eine Sache anderen Menschen mit, denn das geschieht durch das Wort, durch das ich ein Ding bezeichne. Diese Ansicht ist die bürgerliche Auffassung der Sprache, deren Unhaltbarkeit und Leere sich mit steigender Deutlichkeit im folgenden ergeben soll. Sie besagt: Das Mittel der Mitteilung ist das Wort, ihr Gegenstand die Sache, ihr Adressat ein Mensch. Dagegen kennt die andere kein Mittel, keinen Gegenstand und keinen Adressaten der Mitteilung. Sie besagt: im Namen teilt das geistige Wesen des Menschen sich Gott mit.

¶8

The name, within the domain of language, has this sense alone and this incomparably high meaning: that it is the innermost essence of language itself. The name is that through which nothing further imparts itself, and in which language itself, and absolutely, imparts itself. In the name, the quintessence that imparts itself is language. Where the quintessence in its imparting is language itself in its absolute wholeness, there alone is the name, and there is the name alone. Name, as the inherited portion of human language, therefore vouches that language as such is the quintessence of man; and only for this reason, of all quintessences, is the quintessence of man alone impartable without remainder. Upon this is founded the difference between human language and the language of things. But because the quintessence of man is language itself, he cannot impart himself through it, but only in it. The epitome of this intensive totality of language as the quintessence of man is the name. Man is the namer; by this we know that out of him pure language speaks. All nature, insofar as it imparts itself, imparts itself in language, and so, in the end, in man. Hence he is the lord of nature and can name things. Only through the linguistic essence of things does he come, out of himself, to knowledge of them — in the name. God's creation completes itself when things receive their name from man, out of whom, in the name, language alone speaks. One may designate the name as the language of language (if the genitive designates the relation not of a means but of a medium), and in this sense man is indeed, because he speaks in names, the speaker of language, and for that very reason its only speaker. In designating man as the speaker (which is, however, according to the Bible for instance, evidently the name-giver: "as man would name all kinds of living creatures, so should they be called"), many languages enclose this metaphysical knowledge.

Der Name hat im Bereich der Sprache einzig diesen Sinn und diese unvergleichlich hohe Bedeutung: daß er das innerste Wesen der Sprache selbst ist. Der Name ist dasjenige, durch das sich nichts mehr, und in dem die Sprache selbst und absolut sich mitteilt. Im Namen ist das geistige Wesen, das sich mitteilt, die Sprache. Wo das geistige Wesen in seiner Mitteilung die Sprache selbst in ihrer absoluten Ganzheit ist, da allein gibt es den Namen, und da gibt es den Namen allein. Der Name als Erbteil der Menschensprache verbürgt also, daß die Sprache schlechthin das geistige Wesen des Menschen ist; und nur darum ist das geistige Wesen des Menschen allein unter allen Geisteswesen restlos mitteilbar. Das begründet den Unterschied der Menschensprache von der Sprache der Dinge. Weil das geistige Wesen des Menschen aber die Sprache selbst ist, darum kann er sich nicht durch sie, sondern nur in ihr mitteilen. Der Inbegriff dieser intensiven Totalität der Sprache als des geistigen Wesens des Menschen ist der Name. Der Mensch ist der Nennende, daran erkennen wir, daß aus ihm die reine Sprache spricht. Alle Natur, sofern sie sich mitteilt, teilt sich in der Sprache mit, also letzten Endes im Menschen. Darum ist er der Herr der Natur und kann die Dinge benennen. Nur durch das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge gelangt er aus sich selbst zu deren Erkenntnis – im Namen. Gottes Schöpfung vollendet sich, indem die Dinge ihren Namen vom Menschen erhalten, aus dem im Namen die Sprache allein spricht. Man kann den Namen als die Sprache der Sprache bezeichnen (wenn der Genetiv nicht das Verhältnis des Mittels, sondern des Mediums bezeichnet) und in diesem Sinne ist allerdings, weil er im Namen spricht, der Mensch der Sprecher der Sprache, eben darum auch ihr einziger. In der Bezeichnung des Menschen als des Sprechenden (das ist aber z. B. nach der Bibel offenbar der Namen-Gebende: »wie der Mensch allerlei lebendige Tiere nennen würde, so sollten sie heißen«) schließen viele Sprachen diese metaphysische Erkenntnis ein.

¶9

But name is not only the last outcry; it is also the authentic call of language. With this there appears in name the essential law of language, according to which speaking oneself out and speaking to all else are one and the same. Language — and in it a quintessence — speaks itself out purely only where it speaks in name, that is: in universal naming. So in name culminate the intensive totality of language, as the absolutely impartable quintessence, and the extensive totality of language, as the universally imparting (naming) essence. Language is, in its imparting essence, in its universality, imperfect wherever the quintessence that speaks out of it is not in its whole structure linguistic, that is, impartable. Man alone has the language that is perfect in universality and intensity.

Der Name ist aber nicht allein der letzte Ausruf, er ist auch der eigentliche Anruf der Sprache. Damit erscheint im Namen das Wesensgesetz der Sprache, nach dem sich selbst aussprechen und alles andere ansprechen dasselbe ist. Die Sprache – und in ihr ein geistiges Wesen – spricht sich nur da rein aus, wo sie im Namen spricht, das heißt: in der universellen Benennung. So gipfeln im Namen die intensive Totalität der Sprache als des absolut mitteilbaren geistigen Wesens und die extensive Totalität der Sprache als des universell mitteilenden (benennenden) Wesens. Die Sprache ist ihrem mitteilenden Wesen, ihrer Universalität nach, da unvollkommen, wo das geistige Wesen, das aus ihr spricht, nicht in seiner ganzen Struktur sprachliches, das heißt mitteilbares ist. Der Mensch allein hat die nach Universalität und Intensität vollkommene Sprache.

¶10

In view of this knowledge, a question now becomes possible without danger of confusion, a question which, though of the highest metaphysical importance, can at this point be raised in all clarity as, first of all, a terminological one: whether quintessence — not only of man (for that is necessary) but also of things, and thus quintessence as such — is, from the standpoint of the theory of language, to be designated as linguistic. If quintessence is identical with linguistic essence, then a thing is, by its quintessence, medium of imparting, and what imparts itself in it is — in accordance with the medial relation — precisely this medium (language) itself. Language is then the quintessence of things. Quintessence is thus posited from the outset as impartable, or rather is posited precisely within impartability; and the thesis that the linguistic essence of things is identical with their quintessence, insofar as the latter is impartable, becomes in its "insofar" a tautology. A content of language there is not; as imparting, language imparts a quintessence, that is, an impartability as such. The differences among languages are those of media, distinguished as it were according to their density, hence gradually; and this in the twofold respect of the density of the imparting (naming) and of the impartable (name) in the imparting. These two spheres, purely divided and yet united only in the name-language of man, naturally correspond to each other without cease.

Angesichts dieser Erkenntnis ist nun ohne Gefahr der Verwirrung eine Frage möglich, die zwar von höchster metaphysischer Wichtigkeit ist, aber an dieser Stelle in aller Klarheit zunächst als eine terminologische vorgebracht werden kann. Ob nämlich das geistige Wesen – nicht nur des Menschen (denn das ist notwendig) – sondern auch der Dinge und somit geistiges Wesen überhaupt in sprachtheoretischer Hinsicht als sprachliches zu bezeichnen ist. Wenn das geistige Wesen mit dem sprachlichen identisch ist, so ist das Ding seinem geistigen Wesen nach Medium der Mitteilung, und was sich in ihm mitteilt, ist – gemäß dem medialen Verhältnis – eben dies Medium (die Sprache) selbst. Sprache ist dann das geistige Wesen der Dinge. Es wird das geistige Wesen also von vornherein als mitteilbar gesetzt, oder vielmehr gerade in die Mitteilbarkeit gesetzt, und die Thesis: das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge ist mit ihrem geistigen, sofern letzteres mitteilbar ist, identisch, wird in ihrem »sofern« zu einer Tautologie. Einen Inhalt der Sprache gibt es nicht; als Mitteilung teilt die Sprache ein geistiges Wesen, d. i. eine Mitteilbarkeit schlechthin mit. Die Unterschiede der Sprachen sind solche von Medien, die sich gleichsam nach ihrer Dichte, also graduell, unterscheiden; und das in der zwiefachen Hinsicht nach der Dichte des Mitteilenden (Benennenden) und des Mitteilbaren (Namen) in der Mitteilung. Diese beiden Sphären, die rein geschieden und doch vereinigt nur in der Namensprache des Menschen, entsprechen sich natürlich ständig.

¶11

For the metaphysics of language, the equation of quintessence with linguistic essence, which knows only differences of degree, yields a gradation of all quintessential being by degrees. This gradation, which takes place within quintessence itself, can no longer be comprehended under any higher category, and it leads therefore to the grading of all quintessences as of all linguistic essences by degrees of existence or of being, as was already customary in Scholasticism with regard to the quintessential. But the equation of quintessence and linguistic essence is, for the theory of language, of such great metaphysical consequence because it leads to that concept which has again and again, as if of its own accord, risen to the center of the philosophy of language and constituted its most inward connection with the philosophy of religion. That is the concept of revelation. — Within all linguistic formation there reigns the conflict of the spoken-out and the speakable with the unspeakable and the unspoken. In considering this conflict one sees, in the perspective of the unspeakable, at the same time the last quintessence. Now it is clear that in the equation of quintessence and linguistic essence, this relation of inverse proportionality between the two is disputed. For here the thesis runs: the deeper, that is, the more existent and more actual the spirit, the more speakable and the more spoken; as indeed it lies in the sense of this equation to make the relation between spirit and language unambiguous through and through, so that the linguistically most existent, that is, the most fixed expression, the linguistically most pregnant and immovable, in a word: the most spoken-out, is at the same time the purely quintessential. Yet exactly this is what the concept of revelation means when it takes the untouchability of the word as the sole and sufficient condition and mark of the divinity of the quintessence that speaks itself out in it. The highest region of the spirit, that of religion, is (in the concept of revelation) at the same time the only one that does not know the unspeakable. For it is spoken to in the name, and speaks itself out as revelation. But herein announces itself that only the highest quintessence, as it appears in religion, rests purely on man and on the language in him; while all art, poetry not excepted, rests not on the ultimate epitome of the spirit of language, but on the thingly spirit of language, even if in its consummate beauty. "Language, the mother of reason and revelation, its Α and Ω," says Hamann.2

Für die Metaphysik der Sprache ergibt die Gleichsetzung des geistigen mit dem sprachlichen Wesen, welches nur graduelle Unterschiede kennt, eine Abstufung allen geistigen Seins in Gradstufen. Diese Abstufung, die im Inneren des geistigen Wesens selbst stattfindet, läßt sich unter keine obere Kategorie mehr fassen, sie führt daher auf die Abstufung aller geistigen wie sprachlichen Wesen nach Existenzgraden oder nach Seinsgraden, wie sie bezüglich der geistigen schon die Scholastik gewohnt war. Die Gleichsetzung des geistigen mit dem sprachlichen Wesen ist aber in sprachtheoretischer Hinsicht von so großer metaphysischer Tragweite, weil sie auf denjenigen Begriff hinführt, der sich immer wieder wie von selbst im Zentrum der Sprachphilosophie erhoben hat und ihre innigste Verbindung mit der Religionsphilosophie ausgemacht hat. Das ist der Begriff der Offenbarung. – Innerhalb aller sprachlichen Gestaltung waltet der Widerstreit des Ausgesprochenen und Aussprechlichen mit dem Unaussprechlichen und Unausgesprochenen. In der Betrachtung dieses Widerstreites sieht man in der Perspektive des Unaussprechlichen zugleich das letzte geistige Wesen. Nun ist es klar, daß in der Gleichsetzung des geistigen mit dem sprachlichen Wesen dieses Verhältnis der umgekehrten Proportionalität zwischen beiden bestritten wird. Denn hier lautet die Thesis: je tiefer, d. h. je existenter und wirklicher der Geist, desto aussprechlicher und ausgesprochener, wie es denn eben im Sinne dieser Gleichsetzung liegt, die Beziehung zwischen Geist und Sprache zur schlechthin eindeutigen zu machen, so daß der sprachlich existenteste, d. h. fixierteste Ausdruck, das sprachlich Prägnanteste und Unverrückbarste, mit einem Wort: das Ausgesprochenste zugleich das reine Geistige ist. Genau das meint aber der Begriff der Offenbarung, wenn er die Unantastbarkeit des Wortes für die einzige und hinreichende Bedingung und Kennzeichnung der Göttlichkeit des geistigen Wesens, das sich in ihm ausspricht, nimmt. Das höchste Geistesgebiet der Religion ist (im Begriff der Offenbarung) zugleich das einzige, welches das Unaussprechliche nicht kennt. Denn es wird angesprochen im Namen und spricht sich aus als Offenbarung. Hierin aber kündigt sich an, daß allein das höchste geistige Wesen, wie es in der Religion erscheint, rein auf dem Menschen und der Sprache in ihm beruht, während alle Kunst, die Poesie nicht ausgenommen, nicht auf dem allerletzten Inbegriff des Sprachgeistes, sondern auf dinglichem Sprachgeist, wenn auch in seiner vollendeten Schönheit, beruht. »Sprache, die Mutter der Vernunft und Offenbarung, ihr Α und Ω«, sagt Hamann.

¶12

Language itself is not perfectly spoken out in things themselves. This sentence has a double sense, according to its figurative and its literal meaning: the languages of things are imperfect, and they are mute. To things the pure formal principle of language — sound — is denied. They can impart themselves to one another only through a more or less material community. This community is immediate and infinite, like that of every linguistic imparting; it is magical (for there is also a magic of matter). What is incomparable in human language is that its magical community with things is immaterial and purely quintessential, and of this, sound is the symbol. This symbolic fact the Bible speaks out when it says that God breathed his breath into man: this is at once life and spirit and language. —

Die Sprache selbst ist in den Dingen selbst nicht vollkommen ausgesprochen. Dieser Satz hat einen doppelten Sinn nach der übertragenen und der sinnlichen Bedeutung: Die Sprachen der Dinge sind unvollkommen, und sie sind stumm. Den Dingen ist das reine sprachliche Formprinzip – der Laut – versagt. Sie können sich nur durch eine mehr oder minder stoffliche Gemeinschaft einander mitteilen. Diese Gemeinschaft ist unmittelbar und unendlich wie die jeder sprachlichen Mitteilung; sie ist magisch (denn es gibt auch Magie der Materie). Das Unvergleichliche der menschlichen Sprache ist, daß ihre magische Gemeinschaft mit den Dingen immateriell und rein geistig ist, und dafür ist der Laut das Symbol. Dieses symbolische Faktum spricht die Bibel aus, indem sie sagt, daß Gott dem Menschen den Odem einblies: das ist zugleich Leben und Geist und Sprache. –

¶13

If in what follows the essence of language is considered on the basis of the first chapters of Genesis, the aim is neither to pursue biblical interpretation nor, in this place, to lay the Bible objectively, as revealed truth, at the foundation of reflection; rather, what is to be discovered is what yields itself of its own accord out of the biblical text with regard to the nature of language; and the Bible is at first irreplaceable for this purpose only because these expositions follow it in principle in this: that in them language is presupposed as an ultimate reality, inexplicable and mystical, to be considered only in its unfolding. The Bible, in regarding itself as revelation, must of necessity develop the fundamental facts of language. — The second version of the story of creation, which tells of the breathing-in of the breath, reports at the same time that man was made from earth. This is, in the whole story of creation, the only place where a material of the creator is spoken of, in which he expresses his will, which is otherwise surely conceived as creating without mediation. In this second story of creation the making of man did not happen through the word: God spoke — and it came to pass —; rather, upon this man not created out of the word there is now bestowed the gift of language, and he is raised above nature.

Wenn im folgenden das Wesen der Sprache auf Grund der ersten Genesiskapitel betrachtet wird, so soll damit weder Bibelinterpretation als Zweck verfolgt noch auch die Bibel an dieser Stelle objektiv als offenbarte Wahrheit dem Nachdenken zugrunde gelegt werden, sondern das, was aus dem Bibeltext in Ansehung der Natur der Sprache selbst sich ergibt, soll aufgefunden werden; und die Bibel ist zunächst in dieser Absicht nur darum unersetzlich, weil diese Ausführungen im Prinzipiellen ihr darin folgen, daß in ihnen die Sprache als eine letzte, nur in ihrer Entfaltung zu betrachtende, unerklärliche und mystische Wirklichkeit vorausgesetzt wird. Die Bibel, indem sie sich selbst als Offenbarung betrachtet, muß notwendig die sprachlichen Grundtatsachen entwickeln. – Die zweite Fassung der Schöpfungsgeschichte, die vom Einblasen des Odems erzählt, berichtet zugleich, der Mensch sei aus Erde gemacht worden. Dies ist in der ganzen Schöpfungsgeschichte die einzige Stelle, an der von einem Material des Schöpfers die Rede ist, in welchem dieser seinen Willen, der sonst doch wohl unmittelbar schaffend gedacht ist, ausdrückt. Es ist in dieser zweiten Schöpfungsgeschichte die Erschaffung des Menschen nicht durch das Wort geschehen: Gott sprach – und es geschah –, sondern diesem nicht aus dem Worte geschaffenen Menschen wird nun die Gabe der Sprache beigelegt, und er wird über die Natur erhoben.

¶14

But this singular revolution of the act of creation, where it turns toward man, is no less clearly laid down in the first story of creation, and in an entirely different context it vouches, with equal certainty, for the special relation between man and language out of the act of creation. The manifold rhythm of the acts of creation in the first chapter nonetheless admits a kind of basic form, from which only the act that creates man significantly deviates. Nowhere here, to be sure, whether with man or with nature, is there any express reference to the material out of which they were created; and whether in the words "he made" a creating out of matter is intended must here be left undecided. But the rhythm according to which the creation of nature (in Genesis 1) accomplishes itself is: Let there be — He made (created) — He named. — In individual acts of creation (Genesis 1:3; 1:11) only the "Let there be" occurs. In this "Let there be" and in the "He named" at the beginning and at the end of the acts, there appears each time the deep and clear relation of the act of creation to language. It begins with the creating omnipotence of language, and at the close language, as it were, incorporates the created into itself: it names it. Language is therefore the creating, and the completing; it is word and name. In God the name is creative because it is word, and God's word is cognizing because it is name. "And he saw that it was good" — that is: he had known it through the name. The absolute relation of name to knowledge exists only in God; only there is the name, because it is inwardly identical with the creating word, the pure medium of knowledge. That means: God made things knowable in their names. Man, however, names them according to the measure of knowledge.

Diese eigentümliche Revolution des Schöpfungsaktes, wo er sich auf den Menschen richtet, ist aber nicht minder deutlich in der ersten Schöpfungsgeschichte niedergelegt, und in einem ganz anderen Zusammenhange verbürgt er mit gleicher Bestimmtheit den besonderen Zusammenhang zwischen Mensch und Sprache aus dem Akte der Schöpfung heraus. Die mannigfache Rhythmik der Schöpfungsakte des ersten Kapitels läßt doch eine Art Grundform zu, von der allein der den Menschen erschaffende Akt bedeutsam abweicht. Zwar handelt es sich hier nirgends weder bei Mensch noch Natur um eine ausdrückliche Beziehung auf das Material, aus dem sie geschaffen wurden; und ob jeweils in den Worten: »er machte« an ein Schaffen aus Materie etwa gedacht ist, muß hier dahingestellt bleiben. Aber die Rhythmik, nach der sich die Schöpfung der Natur (nach Genesis 1) vollzieht, ist: Es werde – Er machte (schuf) – Er nannte. – In einzelnen Schöpfungsakten (Genesis 1,3; 1,11) tritt allein das »Es werde« auf. In diesem »Es werde« und in dem »Er nannte« am Anfang und Ende der Akte erscheint jedesmal die tiefe deutliche Beziehung des Schöpfungsaktes auf die Sprache. Mit der schaffenden Allmacht der Sprache setzt er ein, und am Schluß einverleibt sich gleichsam die Sprache das Geschaffene, sie benennt es. Sie ist also das Schaffende, und das Vollendende, sie ist Wort und Name. In Gott ist der Name schöpferisch, weil er Wort ist, und Gottes Wort ist erkennend, weil es Name ist. »Und er sah, daß es gut war«, das ist: er hatte es erkannt durch den Namen. Das absolute Verhältnis des Namens zur Erkenntnis besteht allein in Gott, nur dort ist der Name, weil er im innersten mit dem schaffenden Wort identisch ist, das reine Medium der Erkenntnis. Das heißt: Gott machte die Dinge in ihren Namen erkennbar. Der Mensch aber benennt sie maßen der Erkenntnis.

¶15

In the creation of man, the threefold rhythm of the creation of nature has given way to an entirely different order. In it, therefore, language has another meaning; the threeness of the act is preserved here too, but precisely in the parallelism the distance announces itself all the more mightily: in the threefold "He created" of verse 1:27. God did not create man out of the word, and he did not name him. He did not wish to subject him to language; rather, in man, God released language, which had served him as medium of creation, freely out of himself. God rested when he left his creative power to itself in man. This creative power, divested of its divine actuality, became knowledge. Man is the knower of the same language in which God is creator. God created him in his image; he created the knower in the image of the creator. Therefore the sentence, that the quintessence of man is language, requires explanation. His quintessence is the language in which creation occurred. In the word creation occurred, and God's linguistic essence is the word. All human language is only reflection of the word in the name. The name reaches the word as little as knowledge reaches creation. The infinity of all human language remains always of a limited and analytic essence, in comparison with the absolute, unlimited, and creating infinity of the divine word.

In der Schöpfung des Menschen ist die dreifache Rhythmik der Naturschöpfung einer ganz anderen Ordnung gewichen. In ihr hat also die Sprache eine andere Bedeutung; die Dreiheit des Aktes ist auch hier erhalten, aber um so mächtiger bekundet sich eben im Parallelismus der Abstand: in dem dreifachen: »Er schuf« des Verses 1,27. Gott hat den Menschen nicht aus dem Wort geschaffen, und er hat ihn nicht benannt. Er wollte ihn nicht der Sprache unterstellen, sondern im Menschen entließ Gott die Sprache, die ihm als Medium der Schöpfung gedient hatte, frei aus sich. Gott ruhte, als er im Menschen sein Schöpferisches sich selbst überließ. Dieses Schöpferische, seiner göttlichen Aktualität entledigt, wurde Erkenntnis. Der Mensch ist der Erkennende derselben Sprache, in der Gott Schöpfer ist. Gott schuf ihn sich zum Bilde, er schuf den Erkennenden zum Bilde des Schaffenden. Daher bedarf der Satz: Das geistige Wesen des Menschen ist die Sprache, der Erklärung. Sein geistiges Wesen ist die Sprache, in der geschaffen wurde. Im Wort wurde geschaffen, und Gottes sprachliches Wesen ist das Wort. Alle menschliche Sprache ist nur Reflex des Wortes im Namen. Der Name erreicht so wenig das Wort wie die Erkenntnis die Schaffung. Die Unendlichkeit aller menschlichen Sprache bleibt immer eingeschränkten und analytischen Wesens im Vergleich mit der absoluten uneingeschränkten und schaffenden Unendlichkeit des Gotteswortes.

¶16

The deepest image of this divine word, and the point at which human language attains the most inward share in the divine infinity of the pure word, the point at which it can become neither finite word nor knowledge: that is the human name. The theory of the proper name is the theory of the boundary of finite language against infinite language. Of all beings, man is the only one who himself names his own kind, as he is the only one whom God did not name. It is perhaps bold, yet scarcely impossible, to cite the second part of verse 2:20 in this context: that man named all beings, "but for man there was found no helpmeet to be about him." As indeed Adam names his wife as soon as he has received her (Männin, she-man, in the second chapter; Eve in the third). With the giving of the name, parents consecrate their children to God; to the name they give here corresponds — understood metaphysically, not etymologically — no knowledge, since they name newborn children. In a strict spirit, moreover, no human being ought to correspond to the name (according to its etymological meaning), for the proper name is word of God in human sounds. By it, each man is vouched his creation by God, and in this sense he is himself creating, as mythological wisdom speaks it out in the intuition (which is surely not seldom borne out) that a man's name is his fate. The proper name is the community of man with the creative word of God. (It is not the only one; man knows a further linguistic community with God's word.) Through the word man is bound to the language of things. The human word is the name of things. Thus the notion can no longer arise, corresponding as it does to the bourgeois view of language, that the word relates to its object accidentally, that it is a sign of things (or of the knowledge of them) posited by some convention. Language never gives mere signs. But the rejection of the bourgeois theory of language by the mystical theory of language is likewise open to misunderstanding. For according to the latter, the word is simply the essence of the thing. That is incorrect, because the thing in itself has no word; created out of God's word, it is known in its name according to the human word. This knowledge of the thing, however, is not spontaneous creation; it does not occur out of language absolutely unrestrictedly and infinitely, as creation does; rather, the name that man gives to the thing rests upon how it imparts itself to him. In the name the word of God has not remained creating; it has become, in one part, conceiving, though conceiving of language. This conception is directed at the language of things themselves, out of which in turn, soundlessly, in the mute magic of nature, the word of God shines forth.

Das tiefste Abbild dieses göttlichen Wortes und der Punkt, an dem die Menschensprache den innigsten Anteil an der göttlichen Unendlichkeit des bloßen Wortes erlangt, der Punkt, an dem sie nicht endliches Wort und Erkenntnis nicht werden kann: das ist der menschliche Namen. Die Theorie des Eigennamens ist die Theorie von der Grenze der endlichen gegen die unendliche Sprache. Von allen Wesen ist der Mensch das einzige, das seinesgleichen selbst benennt, wie es denn das einzige ist, das Gott nicht benannt hat. Vielleicht ist es kühn, aber kaum unmöglich, den Vers 2,20 in seinem zweiten Teile in diesem Zusammenhang zu nennen: daß der Mensch alle Wesen benannte, »aber für den Menschen ward keine Gehilfin gefunden, die um ihn wäre«. Wie denn auch Adam sein Weib, alsobald er es bekommen hat, benennt. (Männin im zweiten Kapitel, Heva im dritten.) Mit der Gebung des Namens weihen die Eltern ihre Kinder Gott; dem Namen, den sie hier geben, entspricht – metaphysisch, nicht etymologisch verstanden – keine Erkenntnis, wie sie die Kinder ja auch neugeboren benennen. Es sollte im strengen Geist auch kein Mensch dem Namen (nach seiner etymologischen Bedeutung) entsprechen, denn der Eigenname ist Wort Gottes in menschlichen Lauten. Mit ihm wird jedem Menschen seine Erschaffung durch Gott verbürgt, und in diesem Sinne ist er selbst schaffend, wie die mythologische Weisheit es in der Anschauung ausspricht (die sich wohl nicht selten findet), daß sein Name des Menschen Schicksal sei. Der Eigenname ist die Gemeinschaft des Menschen mit dem schöpferischen Wort Gottes. (Es ist dies nicht die einzige, und der Mensch kennt noch eine andere Sprachgemeinschaft mit Gottes Wort.) Durch das Wort ist der Mensch mit der Sprache der Dinge verbunden. Das menschliche Wort ist der Name der Dinge. Damit kann die Vorstellung nicht mehr aufkommen, die der bürgerlichen Ansicht der Sprache entspricht, daß das Wort zur Sache sich zufällig verhalte, daß es ein durch irgendwelche Konvention gesetztes Zeichen der Dinge (oder ihrer Erkenntnis) sei. Die Sprache gibt niemals bloße Zeichen. Mißverständlich ist aber auch die Ablehnung der bürgerlichen durch die mystische Sprachtheorie. Nach ihr nämlich ist das Wort schlechthin das Wesen der Sache. Das ist unrichtig, weil die Sache an sich kein Wort hat, geschaffen ist sie aus Gottes Wort und erkannt in ihrem Namen nach dem Menschenwort. Diese Erkenntnis der Sache ist aber nicht spontane Schöpfung, sie geschieht nicht aus der Sprache absolut uneingeschränkt und unendlich wie diese; sondern es beruht der Name, den der Mensch der Sache gibt, darauf, wie sie ihm sich mitteilt. Im Namen ist das Wort Gottes nicht schaffend geblieben, es ist an einem Teil empfangend, wenn auch sprachempfangend, geworden. Auf die Sprache der Dinge selbst, aus denen wiederum lautlos und in der stummen Magie der Natur das Wort Gottes hervorstrahlt, ist diese Empfängnis gerichtet.

¶17

For conception and spontaneity at once, as they are found in this uniqueness of binding only in the linguistic domain, language has, however, its own word, and this word holds also of that conception of the nameless in the name. It is the translation of the language of things into that of man. It is necessary to ground the concept of translation in the deepest stratum of the theory of language, for it is much too far-reaching and too mighty to be treated, in any respect, as an afterthought, as is sometimes supposed. Its full meaning it gains in the insight that every higher language (with the exception of the word of God) can be regarded as translation of all the others. With the relation, mentioned above, of languages as of media of differing densities, the translatability of languages into one another is given. Translation is the carrying-over of one language into another through a continuum of transformations. Translation traverses continua of transformation, not abstract precincts of equality and likeness.

Für Empfängnis und Spontaneität zugleich, wie sie sich in dieser Einzigartigkeit der Bindung nur im sprachlichen Bereich finden, hat aber die Sprache ihr eigenes Wort, und dieses Wort gilt auch von jener Empfängnis des Namenlosen im Namen. Es ist die Übersetzung der Sprache der Dinge in die des Menschen. Es ist notwendig, den Begriff der Übersetzung in der tiefsten Schicht der Sprachtheorie zu begründen, denn er ist viel zu weittragend und gewaltig, um in irgendeiner Hinsicht nachträglich, wie bisweilen gemeint wird, abgehandelt werden zu können. Seine volle Bedeutung gewinnt er in der Einsicht, daß jede höhere Sprache (mit Ausnahme des Wortes Gottes) als Übersetzung aller anderen betrachtet werden kann. Mit dem erwähnten Verhältnis der Sprachen als dem von Medien verschiedener Dichte ist die Übersetzbarkeit der Sprachen ineinander gegeben. Die Übersetzung ist die Überführung der einen Sprache in die andere durch ein Kontinuum von Verwandlungen. Kontinua der Verwandlung, nicht abstrakte Gleichheits- und Ähnlichkeitsbezirke durchmißt die Übersetzung.

¶18

The translation of the language of things into that of man is not only translation of the mute into the sounding; it is the translation of the nameless into name. That is thus the translation of an imperfect language into a more perfect one; it cannot but add something to it, namely knowledge. But the objectivity of this translation is vouched in God. For God created things; the creating word in them is the germ of the cognizing name, just as God, too, in the end named each thing after it was created. But evidently this naming is only the expression of the identity of the creating word and the cognizing name in God, not the anticipated solution of that task which God expressly assigns to man himself: namely, to name things. By receiving the mute, nameless language of things and carrying it over into names, in sounds, man accomplishes this task. It would be insoluble were not the name-language of man and the nameless language of things akin in God, released out of the same creating word, which in things has become the imparting of matter in magical community, and in man the language of knowledge and of name in blissful spirit. Hamann says: "Everything that man heard in the beginning, saw with his eyes ... and his hands touched, was ... living word; for God was the word. With this word in his mouth and in his heart, the origin of language was as natural, as near and easy, as a child's game ...". The painter Müller, in his poem "Adam's First Awakening and First Blissful Nights,"3 lets God call man to the giving of names with these words: "Man of earth, draw near; in beholding grow more perfect, more perfect grow through the word!" In this binding of beholding and naming there is inwardly meant the imparting muteness of things (of animals), tending toward the word-language of man, which takes them up in name. In the same chapter of the poem, there speaks out of the poet the knowledge that only the word out of which things are created permits man to name them, imparting itself in the manifold languages of the animals, even if mutely, in the image: God gives each animal in turn a sign, whereupon they step before man to be named. In an almost sublime way, the language-community of mute creation with God is thus given in the image of the sign.

Die Übersetzung der Sprache der Dinge in die des Menschen ist nicht nur Übersetzung des Stummen in das Lauthafte, sie ist die Übersetzung des Namenlosen in den Namen. Das ist also die Übersetzung einer unvollkommenen Sprache in eine vollkommenere, sie kann nicht anders als etwas dazu tun, nämlich die Erkenntnis. Die Objektivität dieser Übersetzung ist aber in Gott verbürgt. Denn Gott hat die Dinge geschaffen, das schaffende Wort in ihnen ist der Keim des erkennenden Namens, wie Gott auch am Ende jedes Ding benannte, nachdem es geschaffen war. Aber offenbar ist diese Benennung nur der Ausdruck der Identität des schaffenden Wortes und des erkennenden Namens in Gott, nicht die vorhergenommene Lösung jener Aufgabe, die Gott ausdrücklich dem Menschen selbst zuschreibt: nämlich die Dinge zu benennen. Indem er die stumme namenlose Sprache der Dinge empfängt und sie in den Namen in Lauten überträgt, löst der Mensch diese Aufgabe. Unlösbar wäre sie, wäre nicht die Namensprache des Menschen und die namenlose der Dinge in Gott verwandt, entlassen aus demselben schaffenden Wort, das in den Dingen Mitteilung der Materie in magischer Gemeinschaft, im Menschen Sprache des Erkennens und Namens in seligem Geiste geworden wäre. Hamann sagt: »Alles, was der Mensch am Anfange hörte, mit Augen sah ... und seine Hände betasteten, war ... lebendiges Wort; denn Gott war das Wort. Mit diesem Worte im Mund und im Herzen war der Ursprung der Sprache so natürlich, so nahe und leicht, wie ein Kinderspiel ... «. Der Maler Müller in seiner Dichtung »Adams erstes Erwachen und erste selige Nächte« läßt Gott mit diesen Worten den Menschen zur Namengebung aufrufen: »Mann von Erde, tritt nahe, am Anschauen werde vollkommner, vollkommner werde durchs Wort!« In dieser Verbindung von Anschauung und Benennung ist innerlich die mitteilende Stummheit der Dinge (der Tiere) auf die Wortsprache des Menschen zu gemeint, die sie im Namen aufnimmt. In demselben Kapitel der Dichtung spricht aus dem Dichter die Erkenntnis, daß nur das Wort, aus dem die Dinge geschaffen sind, ihre Benennung dem Menschen erlaubt, indem es sich in den mannigfachen Sprachen der Tiere, wenn auch stumm, mitteilt in dem Bild: Gott gibt den Tieren der Reihe nach ein Zeichen, auf das hin sie vor den Menschen zur Benennung treten. Auf eine fast sublime Weise ist so die Sprachgemeinschaft der stummen Schöpfung mit Gott im Bilde des Zeichens gegeben.

¶19

As the mute word in the existence of things falls so infinitely far short of the naming word in the knowledge of man, and as this in turn falls short of the creating word of God, the ground is given for the plurality of human languages. The language of things can pass into the language of knowledge and of name only in translation — as many translations, so many languages, once, that is, man has fallen from the paradisiacal state that knew only one language. (According to the Bible, to be sure, this consequence of the expulsion from paradise sets in only later.) The paradisiacal language of man must have been the perfectly cognizing one; whereas later all knowledge differentiates itself once more, infinitely, in the manifold of language, and indeed had to differentiate itself, on a lower level, as creation in the name as such. That the language of paradise was the perfectly cognizing one, even the existence of the tree of knowledge cannot conceal. Its apples were to bestow the knowledge of what is good and what is evil. But God had already known on the seventh day, with the words of creation. And behold, it was very good. The knowledge to which the serpent seduces, the knowing of what is good and evil, is nameless. It is, in the deepest sense, void, and this knowing is itself the sole evil that the paradisiacal state knows. The knowing of good and evil forsakes the name; it is a knowledge from outside, the uncreative imitation of the creating word. The name steps outside itself in this knowledge: the Fall is the birth-hour of the human word, in which the name no longer lived unharmed, which stepped out of name-language, the cognizing language, out of what we may call its own immanent magic, in order to become magical expressly, from outside as it were. The word is now to impart something (other than itself). That is truly the Fall of the spirit of language. The word as outwardly imparting, as it were a parody, by the expressly mediate word, of the expressly immediate, the creating word of God; and the decay of the blissful spirit of language, the Adamic spirit, that stands between them. For between the word which, after the promise of the serpent, knows good and evil, and the outwardly imparting word, there is in truth an identity at ground. The knowledge of things rests in the name; the knowledge of good and evil, however, is, in the deep sense in which Kierkegaard grasps this word, "prattle," and knows only one purification and elevation, under which, accordingly, the prattling man, the sinner, was placed: judgment. To the judging word, to be sure, the knowledge of good and evil is immediate. Its magic is other than the magic of the name, but no less magic. This judging word expels the first human beings from paradise; they themselves excited it, according to an eternal law by which this judging word punishes — and awaits — the awakening of itself as the sole and deepest guilt. In the Fall, since the eternal purity of the name was touched, the sterner purity of the judging word arose, of judgment. For the essential context of language, the Fall has a threefold meaning (without mention here of its other meanings). In stepping out of the pure language of the name, man makes language into a means (namely, of a knowledge unsuited to him), and therewith also, in one part at least, into mere sign; and from this, later, the plurality of languages follows. The second meaning is that now, out of the Fall, in restitution for the immediacy of the name there injured, a new immediacy arises, the magic of judgment, which no longer rests blissfully in itself. The third meaning, whose conjecture may perhaps be ventured, would be that the origin of abstraction, too, as a capacity of the spirit of language, is to be sought in the Fall. For good and evil stand, as unnameable, as nameless, outside the language of names, which man forsakes precisely in the abyss of this question. Name, however, with respect to existing language, offers only the ground in which its concrete elements are rooted. But the abstract elements of language — so it may perhaps be conjectured — are rooted in the judging word, in judgment. The immediacy (which, however, is the linguistic root) of the impartability of abstraction lies in the judicial verdict. This immediacy in the imparting of abstraction set in as judging when, in the Fall, man forsook the immediacy in the imparting of the concrete, the name, and fell into the abyss of the mediacy of all imparting, of the word as means, of the vain word, into the abyss of prattle. For — let it be said once more — prattle was the question after good and evil in the world after creation. The tree of knowledge did not stand in God's garden for the sake of the disclosures about good and evil that it might have been able to give, but as the emblem of judgment over the one who asks. This immense irony is the mark of the mythic origin of law.

Wie das stumme Wort im Dasein der Dinge so unendlich weit unter dem benennenden Wort in der Erkenntnis des Menschen zurückbleibt, wie wiederum dieses wohl unter dem schaffenden Wort Gottes, so ist der Grund für die Vielheit menschlicher Sprachen gegeben. Die Sprache der Dinge kann in die Sprache der Erkenntnis und des Namens nur in der Übersetzung eingehen – soviel Übersetzungen, soviel Sprachen, sobald nämlich der Mensch einmal aus dem paradiesischen Zustand, der nur eine Sprache kannte, gefallen ist. (Nach der Bibel stellt diese Folge der Austreibung aus dem Paradiese allerdings erst später sich ein.) Die paradiesische Sprache des Menschen muß die vollkommen erkennende gewesen sein; während später noch einmal alle Erkenntnis in der Mannigfaltigkeit der Sprache sich unendlich differenziert, auf einer niederen Stufe als Schöpfung im Namen überhaupt sich differenzieren mußte. Daß nämlich die Sprache des Paradieses vollkommen erkennend gewesen sei, vermag auch das Dasein des Baumes der Erkenntnis nicht zu verhehlen. Seine Äpfel sollten die Erkenntnis verleihen, was gut und böse sei. Gott aber hatte schon am siebenten Tage mit den Worten der Schöpfung erkannt. Und siehe, es war sehr gut. Die Erkenntnis, zu der die Schlange verführt, das Wissen, was gut sei und böse, ist namenlos. Es ist im tiefsten Sinne nichtig, und dieses Wissen eben selbst das einzige Böse, das der paradiesische Zustand kennt. Das Wissen um gut und böse verläßt den Namen, es ist eine Erkenntnis von außen, die unschöpferische Nachahmung des schaffenden Wortes. Der Name tritt aus sich selbst in dieser Erkenntnis heraus: Der Sündenfall ist die Geburtsstunde des menschlichen Wortes, in dem der Name nicht mehr unverletzt lebte, das aus der Namensprache, der erkennenden, man darf sagen: der immanenten eigenen Magie heraustrat, um ausdrücklich, von außen gleichsam, magisch zu werden. Das Wort soll etwas mitteilen (außer sich selbst). Das ist wirklich der Sündenfall des Sprachgeistes. Das Wort als äußerlich mitteilendes, gleichsam eine Parodie des ausdrücklich mittelbaren Wortes auf das ausdrücklich unmittelbare, das schaffende Gotteswort, und der Verfall des seligen Sprachgeistes, des adamitischen, der zwischen ihnen steht. Es besteht nämlich in der Tat zwischen dem Worte, welches nach der Verheißung der Schlange das Gute und Böse erkennt, und zwischen dem äußerlich mitteilenden Worte im Grunde Identität. Die Erkenntnis der Dinge beruht im Namen, die des Guten und Bösen ist aber in dem tiefen Sinne, in dem Kierkegaard dieses Wort faßt, »Geschwätz« und kennt nur eine Reinigung und Erhöhung, unter die denn auch der geschwätzige Mensch, der Sündige, gestellt wurde: das Gericht. Dem richtenden Wort ist allerdings die Erkenntnis von gut und böse unmittelbar. Seine Magie ist eine andere als die des Namens, aber gleich sehr Magie. Dieses richtende Wort verstößt die ersten Menschen aus dem Paradies; sie selbst haben es exzitiert, zufolge einem ewigen Gesetz, nach welchem dieses richtende Wort die Erweckung seiner selbst als die einzige, tiefste Schuld bestraft – und erwartet. Im Sündenfall, da die ewige Reinheit des Namens angetastet wurde, erhob sich die strengere Reinheit des richtenden Wortes, des Urteils. Für den Wesenszusammenhang der Sprache hat der Sündenfall eine dreifache Bedeutung (ohne seine sonstige hier zu erwähnen). Indem der Mensch aus der reinen Sprache des Namens heraustritt, macht er die Sprache zum Mittel (nämlich einer ihm unangemessenen Erkenntnis), damit auch an einem Teile jedenfalls zum bloßen Zeichen; und das hat später die Mehrheit der Sprachen zur Folge. Die zweite Bedeutung ist, daß nun aus dem Sündenfall als die Restitution der in ihm verletzten Unmittelbarkeit des Namens eine neue, die Magie des Urteils, sich erhebt, die nicht mehr selig in sich selbst ruht. Die dritte Bedeutung, deren Vermutung sich vielleicht wagen läßt, wäre, daß auch der Ursprung der Abstraktion als eines Vermögens des Sprachgeistes im Sündenfall zu suchen sei. Gut und böse nämlich stehen als unbenennbar, als namenlos außerhalb der Namensprache, die der Mensch eben im Abgrund dieser Fragestellung verläßt. Der Name bietet nun aber im Hinblick auf die bestehende Sprache nur den Grund, in dem ihre konkreten Elemente wurzeln. Die abstrakten Sprachelemente aber – so darf vielleicht vermutet werden – wurzeln im richtenden Worte, im Urteil. Die Unmittelbarkeit (das ist aber die sprachliche Wurzel) der Mitteilbarkeit der Abstraktion ist im richterlichen Urteil gelegen. Diese Unmittelbarkeit in der Mitteilung der Abstraktion stellte sich richtend ein, als im Sündenfall der Mensch die Unmittelbarkeit in der Mitteilung des Konkreten, den Namen, verließ und in den Abgrund der Mittelbarkeit aller Mitteilung, des Wortes als Mittel, des eitlen Wortes verfiel, in den Abgrund des Geschwätzes. Denn – noch einmal soll das gesagt werden – Geschwätz war die Frage nach dem Gut und Böse in der Welt nach der Schöpfung. Der Baum der Erkenntnis stand nicht wegen der Aufschlüsse über Gut und Böse, die er zu geben vermocht hätte, im Garten Gottes, sondern als Wahrzeichen des Gerichts über den Fragenden. Diese ungeheure Ironie ist das Kennzeichen des mythischen Ursprungs des Rechtes.

¶20

After the Fall, which, in making language mediate, had laid the ground for its plurality, it could be only one step more to the confusion of tongues. Since men had injured the purity of the name, the turning-away from that beholding of things, in which their language passes over into man, needed only to complete itself in order to rob men of the common foundation of the spirit of language, already shaken. Signs must become confused where things entangle themselves. To the enslavement of language in prattle is joined the enslavement of things in folly, almost as its unfailing consequence. In this turning-away from things, which was enslavement, arose the plan of the building of the Tower, and the confusion of tongues with it.

Nach dem Sündenfall, der in der Mittelbarmachung der Sprache den Grund zu ihrer Vielheit gelegt hatte, konnte es bis zur Sprachverwirrung nur noch ein Schritt sein. Da die Menschen die Reinheit des Namens verletzt hatten, brauchte nur noch die Abkehr von jenem Anschauen der Dinge, in dem deren Sprache dem Menschen eingeht, sich zu vollziehen, um die gemeinsame Grundlage des schon erschütterten Sprachgeistes den Menschen zu rauben. Zeichen müssen sich verwirren, wo sich die Dinge verwickeln. Zur Verknechtung der Sprache im Geschwätz tritt die Verknechtung der Dinge in der Narretei fast als deren unausbleibliche Folge. In dieser Abkehr von den Dingen, die die Verknechtung war, entstand der Plan des Turmbaus und die Sprachverwirrung mit ihm.

¶21

The life of man in the pure spirit of language was blissful. Nature, however, is mute. It can indeed be clearly felt in the second chapter of Genesis how this muteness, itself named by man, became bliss, only of lower degree. The painter Müller has Adam say, of the animals that leave him after he has named them: "and saw by the nobility with which they sprang away from me, because the man had given them a name." But after the Fall, with God's word cursing the field, the countenance of nature is altered in its depths. Now begins its other muteness, which we mean by the deep sadness of nature. It is a metaphysical truth that all nature would begin to lament, were language bestowed upon it. (Though "to bestow language" is, to be sure, more than "to make it able to speak.") This sentence has a double sense. It means first: nature would lament over language itself. Speechlessness: that is the great suffering of nature (and for the sake of her redemption, the life and language of man are in nature — not only, as one supposes, of the poet). Second, this sentence says: she would lament. But lament is the most undifferentiated, powerless expression of language; it contains almost nothing but the sensuous breath; and wherever even plants merely rustle, a lament always sounds along. Because she is mute, nature mourns. Yet the inversion of this sentence leads deeper still into the essence of nature: the sadness of nature makes her fall mute. In all mourning there is the deepest inclination to speechlessness, and this is infinitely more than inability or unwillingness to impart. What is sorrowful feels itself known through and through by the unknowable. To be named — even when the namer is one godlike and blissful — remains perhaps always an intimation of mourning. But how much more so, to be named not out of the one blessed paradise-language of names, but out of the hundred languages of man, in which the name has already withered, and which nevertheless, by God's pronouncement, know things. Things have no proper names except in God. For God, in the creating word, did indeed call them forth by their proper names. In the language of men, however, they are overnamed. In the relation of the languages of men to the language of things there lies something that may be approximately designated "overnaming": overnaming as the deepest linguistic ground of all sadness and (regarded from the side of the thing) of all falling-mute. Overnaming, as the linguistic essence of what is sad, points to another remarkable relation of language: to the overdetermination that reigns in the tragic relation between the languages of speaking men.

Das Leben des Menschen im reinen Sprachgeist war selig. Die Natur aber ist stumm. Es ist zwar im zweiten Kapitel der Genesis deutlich zu fühlen, wie diese vom Menschen benannte Stummheit selbst Seligkeit nur niederen Grades geworden ist. Der Maler Müller läßt Adam von den Tieren, die ihn verlassen, nachdem er sie benannt hat, sagen: »und sah an den Adel, wie sie von mir wegsprangen, darum daß ihnen der Mann einen Namen gab.« Nach dem Sündenfall aber ändert sich mit Gottes Wort, das den Acker verflucht, das Ansehen der Natur im tiefsten. Nun beginnt ihre andere Stummheit, die wir mit der tiefen Traurigkeit der Natur meinen. Es ist eine metaphysische Wahrheit, daß alle Natur zu klagen begönne, wenn Sprache ihr verliehen würde. (Wobei »Sprache verleihen« allerdings mehr ist als »machen, daß sie sprechen kann«.) Dieser Satz hat einen doppelten Sinn. Er bedeutet zuerst: sie würde über die Sprache selbst klagen. Sprachlosigkeit: das ist das große Leid der Natur (und um ihrer Erlösung willen ist Leben und Sprache des Menschen in der Natur, nicht allein, wie man vermutet, des Dichters). Zweitens sagt dieser Satz: sie würde klagen. Die Klage ist aber der undifferenzierteste, ohnmächtige Ausdruck der Sprache, sie enthält fast nur den sinnlichen Hauch; und wo auch nur Pflanzen rauschen, klingt immer eine Klage mit. Weil sie stumm ist, trauert die Natur. Doch noch tiefer führt in das Wesen der Natur die Umkehrung dieses Satzes ein: die Traurigkeit der Natur macht sie verstummen. Es ist in aller Trauer der tiefste Hang zur Sprachlosigkeit, und das ist unendlich viel mehr als Unfähigkeit oder Unlust zur Mitteilung. Das Traurige fühlt sich so durch und durch erkannt vom Unerkennbaren. Benannt zu sein – selbst wenn der Nennende ein Göttergleicher und Seliger ist – bleibt vielleicht immer eine Ahnung von Trauer. Wieviel mehr aber benannt zu sein, nicht aus der einen seligen Paradiesessprache der Namen, sondern aus den hunderten Menschensprachen, in denen der Namen schon welkte, und die dennoch nach Gottes Spruch die Dinge erkennen. Die Dinge haben keine Eigennamen außer in Gott. Denn Gott rief im schaffenden Wort freilich bei ihren Eigennamen sie hervor. In der Sprache der Menschen aber sind sie überbenannt. Im Verhältnis der Menschensprachen zu der der Dinge liegt etwas, was man als »Überbenennung« annähernd bezeichnen kann: Überbenennung als tiefster sprachlicher Grund aller Traurigkeit und (vom Ding aus betrachtet) allen Verstummens. Die Überbenennung als sprachliches Wesen des Traurigen deutet auf ein anderes merkwürdiges Verhältnis der Sprache: auf die Überbestimmtheit, die im tragischen Verhältnis zwischen den Sprachen der sprechenden Menschen waltet.

¶22

There is a language of sculpture, of painting, of poetry. As the language of poetry is founded in the name-language of man, if not in it alone, then at least along with it, so it is very well thinkable that the language of sculpture or of painting is founded, say, in certain kinds of thing-languages; that in them a translation of the language of things into an infinitely much higher language, though perhaps one of the same sphere, is present. We are concerned here with nameless, non-acoustic languages, with languages out of material: whereby one should think of the material community of things in their imparting.

Es gibt eine Sprache der Plastik, der Malerei, der Poesie. So wie die Sprache der Poesie in der Namensprache des Menschen, wenn nicht allein, so doch jedenfalls mit fundiert ist, ebenso ist es sehr wohl denkbar, daß die Sprache der Plastik oder Malerei etwa in gewissen Arten von Dingsprachen fundiert sei, daß in ihnen eine Übersetzung der Sprache der Dinge in eine unendlich viel höhere Sprache, aber doch vielleicht derselben Sphäre, vorliegt. Es handelt sich hier um namenlose, unakustische Sprachen, um Sprachen aus dem Material; dabei ist an die materiale Gemeinsamkeit der Dinge in ihrer Mitteilung zu denken.

¶23

Moreover, the imparting of things is certainly of such a kind of communality that it comprehends the world as such as an undivided whole.

Übrigens ist die Mitteilung der Dinge gewiß von einer solchen Art von Gemeinschaftlichkeit, daß sie die Welt überhaupt als ein ungeschiedenes Ganzes befaßt.

¶24

For the knowledge of the forms of art, it holds good to attempt to conceive them all as languages and to seek their connection with languages of nature. An example that lies near, because it belongs to the acoustic sphere, is the kinship of song with the language of birds. On the other hand, it is certain that the language of art can be understood only in the deepest relation to the doctrine of signs. Without this, every philosophy of language whatsoever remains entirely fragmentary, because the relation between language and sign (of which that between human language and script forms only a quite particular example) is original and fundamental.

Für die Erkenntnis der Kunstformen gilt der Versuch, sie alle als Sprachen aufzufassen und ihren Zusammenhang mit Natursprachen zu suchen. Ein Beispiel, das naheliegt, weil es der akustischen Sphäre angehört, ist die Verwandtschaft des Gesanges mit der Sprache der Vögel. Andererseits ist gewiß, daß die Sprache der Kunst sich nur in tiefster Beziehung zur Lehre von den Zeichen verstehen läßt. Ohne diese bleibt überhaupt jede Sprachphilosophie gänzlich fragmentarisch, weil die Beziehung zwischen Sprache und Zeichen (wofür die zwischen Menschensprache und Schrift nur ein ganz besonderes Beispiel bildet) ursprünglich und fundamental ist.

¶25

This gives occasion to designate another opposition, one that pervades the whole domain of language and has important relations to the opposition mentioned, between language in the narrower sense and sign, yet by no means simply coincides with it. For language is, in every case, not only imparting of the impartable, but at the same time symbol of the non-impartable. This symbolic side of language hangs together with its relation to the sign, but extends, for example, in a certain respect, over name and judgment as well. These have not only an imparting function, but in all probability also a symbolic function closely bound up with it, to which, expressly at least, no reference has here been made.

Dies gibt Gelegenheit, einen anderen Gegensatz zu bezeichnen, der das gesamte Gebiet der Sprache durchwaltet und wichtige Beziehungen zu dem erwähnten von Sprache in engerem Sinne und Zeichen hat, die doch durchaus nicht ohne weiteres mit diesem zusammenfällt. Es ist nämlich Sprache in jedem Falle nicht allein Mitteilung des Mitteilbaren, sondern zugleich Symbol des Nicht-Mitteilbaren. Diese symbolische Seite der Sprache hängt mit ihrer Beziehung zum Zeichen zusammen, aber erstreckt sich zum Beispiel in gewisser Beziehung auch über Name und Urteil. Diese haben nicht allein eine mitteilende, sondern höchstwahrscheinlich auch eine mit ihr eng verbundene symbolische Funktion, auf die hier ausdrücklich wenigstens nicht hingewiesen wurde.

¶26

There remains, then, after these considerations, a purified concept of language, however imperfect it may still be. The language of a being is the medium in which its quintessence imparts itself. The unbroken stream of this imparting flows through the whole of nature, from the lowliest existent up to man, and from man to God. Man imparts himself to God through the name that he gives to nature and to his own kind (in the proper name); and to nature he gives the name according to the imparting that he receives from her, for the whole of nature, too, is traversed by a nameless, mute language, the residuum of the creating word of God, which has preserved itself in man as the cognizing name, and above man, hovering, as the judgment that judges. The language of nature is to be compared to a secret watchword that each sentry passes to the next in his own language; but the content of the watchword is the sentry's language itself. All higher language is translation of the lower, until, in ultimate clarity, the word of God unfolds, which is the unity of this movement of language.

Demnach bleibt nach diesen Erwägungen ein gereinigter Begriff von Sprache zurück, wenn der auch noch unvollkommen sein mag. Die Sprache eines Wesens ist das Medium, in dem sich sein geistiges Wesen mitteilt. Der ununterbrochene Strom dieser Mitteilung fließt durch die ganze Natur vom niedersten Existierenden bis zum Menschen und vom Menschen zu Gott. Der Mensch teilt sich Gott durch den Namen mit, den er der Natur und seinesgleichen (im Eigennamen) gibt, und der Natur gibt er den Namen nach der Mitteilung, die er von ihr empfängt, denn auch die ganze Natur ist von einer namenlosen stummen Sprache durchzogen, dem Residuum des schaffenden Gotteswortes, welches im Menschen als erkennender Name und über dem Menschen als richtendes Urteil schwebend sich erhalten hat. Die Sprache der Natur ist einer geheimen Losung zu vergleichen, die jeder Posten dem nächsten in seiner eigenen Sprache weitergibt, der Inhalt der Losung aber ist die Sprache des Postens selbst. Alle höhere Sprache ist Übersetzung der niederen, bis in der letzten Klarheit sich das Wort Gottes entfaltet, das die Einheit dieser Sprachbewegung ist.

Written in 1916; unpublished in Benjamin's lifetime. This edition's translation and commentary, 2026.

Translator’s note

This translation began from a textlog-derived German transcription and has now been revised against a critical reading based on Gesammelte Schriften II.1, pp. 140–157, including its report on the surviving typescripts. It inherits the sentence-level commentary project's decisions, of which the principal ones are:

  • The German reading text. Wording, punctuation, spelling, and emphasis follow GS II.1 except at two declared departures: ¶3 restores the unanimous T1–T4 reading in where GS conjectures in einer Sprache, and ¶14 retains Genesis 1:11 where GS prints 1:14. Marked historical forms such as Genetiv and nominative Namen are preserved rather than silently normalized. The two departures and their evidence are set out in the textual apparatus.
  • The impart-family. Mitteilung is rendered "imparting" throughout, sich mitteilen "imparts itself," mitteilbar "impartable," Mitteilbarkeit "impartability." English cooperates with the German pun-family more than expected: impart contains "part," and teilhaben is rendered "partake," so the essay's opening logic (whatever partakes of language imparts itself) keeps its audible root. The one member that could not be saved is Urteil (judgment), the primal parting; the translation of judgment as "sentence" was considered and set aside.
  • The ring-words. Schwebend is "hovering" at both of its posts (the theory over the abyss; judgment over man), Entfaltung/entfalten is "unfolding/unfolds" at both of its posts (language watchable only in its unfolding; the word of God unfolds), and the pair Unantastbarkeit/angetastet is "untouchability/touched" (revelation; the Fall). The essay's ring-composition depends on these recurrences and is here audible in English for the first time.
  • Corrections of the received translation. Das Mediale is "mediality," which does not mean mediation; the name is that through which "nothing further" imparts itself (durch das sich nichts mehr), restored; Prinzip is "principle," not "tendency"; unschöpferisch is "uncreative," not "uncreated"; als die Restitution is "in restitution," a legal remedy, not an exchange; and the name man gives is given "to the thing" (der Sache), resting on how it imparts itself to him. Across ¶7 and ¶16, Sache is rendered by local function as “factual subject matter,” “subject matter,” “object,” or “thing”; Materie alone remains “matter.” In ¶16, English “thing” locally covers both Sache and Ding; the parallel German preserves the source shift.

  • The quintessence policy. The essay’s technical constant geistiges Wesen has no neutral English. Geistig ranges across mind, intellect, spirit, and what is ideational or nonmaterial; Wesen ranges across essence, being, and entity. Jephcott’s “mental being” makes a lamp, language, and nature sound psychological. “Spiritual essence” preserves the visible relation to Geist, but in contemporary English it overweights the term’s theological pole and can suggest a religious or New Age substance. This edition therefore redeploys quintessence for geistiges Wesen and quintessential in its related adjective slots.

    This is an interpretive rendering, not a claim of literal equivalence. It gives up the visible German root shared with bare Geist and Sprachgeist, which remain in the spirit-family, and it renders Inbegriff as “epitome” to keep the source terms distinct. Its wager is that “quintessence” behaves more like Benjamin’s concept: it can belong to a lamp, a thing, a language, a human being, or God without making each a mind or soul. Repetition is part of the policy. Within this essay, “quintessential” means “of or pertaining to quintessence,” not “most typical.”

    The choice also makes an alchemical constellation audible. The German itself supplies the magic of Materie, media of differing densities, continua of transformations, and the residue of the creative word in things. “Quintessence” intensifies the relation among those terms; it does not prove that geistiges Wesen is lexically alchemical. Hamlet’s “quintessence of dust” makes an English resonance with Genesis 2:7 available, but it is a resonance of this translation, not a proposed historical source for Benjamin.

  • Other constants: Wesen is "essence" where it names the essence-of and "being" where it names a creature; Anschauen is "beholding"; nichtig is "void" and eitel "vain"; Geschwätz is "prattle"; Überbenennung "overnaming" and Überbestimmtheit "overdetermination"; verstummen "to fall mute" against stumm sein, "to be mute"; Bezirk is "precinct" at both occurrences (the precinct of ideas; the precincts of equality and likeness), a quiet echo the German offers and English can keep.

The movement of the essay

  • I. Language as such (¶1–4). Everything imparts itself; language is medium, not means; the in/through distinction; magic defined as immediacy; each language an incommensurable infinity.

The one untranslatable word-family

The essay's engine is a German pun-family that no translation preserves: Teil (part) generates mitteilen (to impart: to part-with), Mitteilbarkeit (impartability), teilhaben (to participate, ¶1), Urteil (judgment: the primal parting), and Mittel (means) generates unmittelbar (immediate), Mittelbarkeit (mediacy), vermitteln (to mediate). In German the argument audibly runs through shared roots: everything that parts-takes of language im-parts itself; the Fall from im-parting into the word-as-means is a fall from the im-mediate into mediacy; and the fallen word's magic is judgment, the primal part-ing. The commentary tracks this family throughout; the translation carries it on English's own part-roots (impart, partake, impartability) and concedes Urteil, whose primal parting no English judgment-word preserves. (The Urteil etymology as a philosophical motif goes back to Hölderlin's "Urteil und Sein"; whether Benjamin intends the pun is speculative, but the essay's system exploits it objectively.)

The quintessence policy

This edition redeploys quintessence for geistiges Wesen and quintessential in related adjective slots. The choice is interpretive, not a claim of literal equivalence. The German supplies several nodes—magic of Materie, differing densities, transformations, residue—that permit an alchemical reading. “Quintessence” gathers and intensifies those nodes in English. The resulting constellation is this edition’s interpretive wager, not a claim that geistig lexically means alchemical. The visible Geist root, the rejected alternatives, and the English Hamlet resonance are discussed in the translator’s note at the end of the essay text.

Stance

Four commitments govern the commentary. Each is argued at length in the epilegomena; they are stated here so the reader knows the ground rules.

On the theology: this commentary treats it as indispensable, inherited in substance, and held in suspension. The essay's argument needs the vertical asymmetries that only theology supplies (an original above all versions, a residue within all things, a direction for the name's address). Where ¶16 rejects word-mysticism and conventionalism in the same breath, it is faithful to the theology it inherited, which never held the word to be the essence of the thing: in Molitor, the documented channel, the original language already imitates divine speech, not the natures of things. Even ¶13's celebrated proviso has its antecedent: Molitor too sets literal interpretation aside and derives what must follow from the Bible insofar as it regards itself as revelation (Mertens; see the bibliography). What is Benjamin's own is the suspension and the instrumentation: Molitor affirms the revelation he reasons from, while Benjamin works with the concept of God and not God himself, holding the inherited structure without laying its ground down as truth, the essay's hovering posture applied to revelation itself; and around that suspended structure he builds a theory of media the tradition never had.

On the word "God": the essay sustains two readings to its last sentence, a theological one, in which God is the real addressee of the name, and a deflationary one, in which "God" is the limit-concept of communicability itself (Agamben's reading). The commentary does not choose between them; it shows, sentence by sentence, what each reading makes of the text and where each strains.

On Kabbalah: parallels are always marked as resonance or as provenance, never left ambiguous. In 1916 Benjamin had no Hebrew and no first-hand kabbalistic sources; what reached him came through German and Christian-kabbalistic mediators. The rhymes with kabbalistic language theory are real, and they are rhymes.

On "pure language": whether it names fulfillment (Agamben) or failure (de Man) is the fork the essay itself built, and the commentary stages the choice where the text forces it, above all at ¶8.9 and ¶25.

I. Language as such · ¶1 · The universality thesis, and the wager that it is not a metaphor.

¶1: Everything imparts itself

Where we are. The essay opens at maximum extension: not with a definition of language but with a claim about everything. The paragraph runs a deliberate crescendo: expressions of the life of the human spirit (¶1.1), then the fine arts and institutions (¶1.2–4), then "absolutely everything" (¶1.5–6), then the methodological wager that none of this is metaphor (¶1.7–8), and finally the first appearance of God, as a boundary drawn around what can be thought at all (¶1.9). Everything the essay will do is staked here; the rest is the working out.

¶1.1 “Every expression of the life of the human spirit can be conceived as a kind of language, and this conception, in the manner of a true method, everywhere opens up new questions.”

DE: »Jede Äußerung menschlichen Geisteslebens kann als eine Art der Sprache aufgefaßt werden, und diese Auffassung erschließt nach Art einer wahrhaften Methode überall neue Fragestellungen.«

The first word is Jede, "every." Benjamin opens with the quantifier, and the whole essay is an attempt to earn it. Note what the sentence does not say: it does not say every expression is language, but that it can be understood as a kind of language (aufgefaßt werden), and that this way of taking things proves itself "in the manner of a true method" by opening new questions everywhere. That is a specific, early-Benjamin criterion for truth in method: a true method is generative, measured by the questions it unlocks rather than the theses it settles. The essay thereby announces its own epistemology in its first sentence: what follows is not a doctrine to be proven but a method to be exercised, and its validation will be the depth of what it makes visible. (This criterion returns transformed in the Epistemo-Critical Prologue of the Trauerspiel book, where method becomes Umweg, detour, and presentation itself.)

DE Geistesleben: Jephcott's "mental life" is the standard rendering and the standard loss. Geist spans mind and spirit; geistig is the essay's most frequent adjective, and across its uses the word-family ranges between the philosophical register (mental, intellectual) and the theological one (spiritual). The essay's German runs on this doubleness: when ¶3 says the geistiges Wesen imparts itself in language, both a philosophy of mind and a pneumatology are being written at once. Our translation renders the term of art geistiges Wesen as "quintessence" and keeps the Geist-compounds in the spirit-family. The German supplies the magic, density, transformation, and residue nodes that permit an alchemical reading; “quintessence” gathers and intensifies them in English. That constellation is this edition’s interpretive wager, not a lexical claim about geistig (see the translator’s note). The commentary uses "mental/spiritual" where the German doubleness is active.

↔ The "true method" criterion governs ¶13.1's proviso about how to read Genesis (discovery of what "emerges of itself," not interpretation) and is the quiet justification for the essay's whole procedure.

¶1.2–3 “One can speak of a language of music and of sculpture… of a language of technology that is not the technicians' professional idiom. Language means, in such contexts, the principle, directed toward the imparting of quintessential contents, at work in the domains concerned.”

DE: »Man kann von einer Sprache der Musik und der Plastik reden, von einer Sprache der Justiz … von einer Sprache der Technik, die nicht die Fachsprache der Techniker ist. Sprache bedeutet in solchem Zusammenhang das auf Mitteilung geistiger Inhalte gerichtete Prinzip in den betreffenden Gegenständen: in Technik, Kunst, Justiz oder Religion.«

The examples are chosen to detach "language" from words immediately: music and sculpture (nonverbal arts), justice (not the wording of statutes), and technology (not jargon). Each example subtracts the verbal: a language of justice that has "nothing directly to do" with how judgments are couched, a language of technology that is not what technicians say. What remains after the subtraction is the thing ¶1.3 names.

DE The decisive difference from the English: Jephcott gives "the tendency inherent in the subjects concerned… toward the communication of the contents of the mind," but the German says Prinzip, principle: "the principle in the objects concerned that is directed toward the imparting of quintessential contents." A tendency is a drift; a principle is constitutive. Benjamin is claiming that directedness-toward-imparting is a structural feature of technology, art, justice, and religion, not a disposition they happen to have. The English quietly weakens the essay's strongest early claim.

↔ The "language of sculpture, of painting, of poetry" returns at ¶22, where the arts are founded on thing-languages: this early list is a promise the essay keeps late. The language of justice, unremarked here, detonates at ¶19's "mythic origin of law."

¶1.4 “In a word: every imparting of quintessential contents is language, imparting by the word being only a particular case…”

DE: »Mit einem Wort: jede Mitteilung geistiger Inhalte ist Sprache, wobei die Mitteilung durch das Wort nur ein besonderer Fall, der der menschlichen, und der ihr zugrunde liegenden oder auf ihr fundierten (Justiz, Poesie), ist.«

The summary inverts the ordinary picture in passing, as if it were nothing: word-language is demoted to "a particular case" of language in general. The title's architecture is already here: language as such (überhaupt) first, the language of man second, a special case. Note the small joke available in the German: the sentence demoting the word begins Mit einem Wort, "in a word."

DE Mitteilung makes its first appearance and will be the essay's most important noun. Etymologically mit-teilen is to part-with, to share out; Samuel Weber's rendering "imparting" preserves what "communication" erases, namely that the word names a giving of part, not a transmission of content. The commentary uses "imparting" wherever the German etymology is doing work. (Weber, Benjamin's -abilities.)

¶1.5–6 “But the existence of language extends not only over all the regions of the human spirit's expression… it extends to absolutely everything. There is no event or thing, in animate or inanimate nature alike, that does not in some way partake of language…”

DE: »Das Dasein der Sprache erstreckt sich aber nicht nur über alle Gebiete menschlicher Geistesäußerung … sondern es erstreckt sich auf schlechthin alles. Es gibt kein Geschehen oder Ding weder in der belebten noch in der unbelebten Natur, das nicht in gewisser Weise an der Sprache teilhätte, denn es ist jedem wesentlich, seinen geistigen Inhalt mitzuteilen.«

Here the crescendo peaks: schlechthin alles, absolutely everything. The justification clause is the radical part: "for it is essential to each thing to impart its quintessential content." Essential (wesentlich): imparting is not something things do but part of what they are. The restored adjective geistigen matters as well: what every thing imparts is not content in the ordinary informational sense, but the content belonging to its geistiges Wesen—what this edition calls its quintessence. The panlinguistic thesis, stated flat.

DE "Partake" is exactly right and better than the translator could have known: teilhätte, from teilhaben, to have-part. The German sentence says that whatever has part in language does so because it is essential to it to give part of itself (mitzuteilen). Participation and imparting are the same root, and the sentence's logic lives in that identity: to be part of language is to part with oneself. This is the first sounding of the Teil family (see overview, "The one untranslatable word-family").

If competence in language alone yields competence in the world's structure, that is evidence the world's structure is, in Benjamin's sense, linguistic: things impart themselves, and a sufficiently large model of imparting captures them. Vision-language models extend the point: images translate into language because, on this reading, they were never outside it. (Contra Fei-Fei Li's "the world is not made of words.") ¶1.7–8 “But the word 'language,' so used, is by no means a metaphor…”

DE: »Eine Metapher aber ist das Wort ›Sprache‹ in solchem Gebrauche durchaus nicht. Denn es ist eine volle inhaltliche Erkenntnis, daß wir uns nichts vorstellen können, das sein geistiges Wesen nicht im Ausdruck mitteilt …«

The methodological wager, stated with maximal exposure. Everything depends on this: if "the language of things" is metaphor, the essay is an elaborate figure of speech; if it is not, the essay is first philosophy. Benjamin's argument for non-metaphoricity is an imaginability claim: we can conceive nothing that does not impart its quintessence in expression; a total absence of language is unimaginable. The German is stronger than Jephcott's tangled rendering: es ist eine volle inhaltliche Erkenntnis, "it is a full, contentful cognition," that nothing imaginable fails to impart itself. Not a stipulation: a cognition with content. The argument-form is transcendental, in the loose sense: language is a condition on anything appearing to us at all, so nothing can appear as language-less.

The concessive clause about consciousness ("apparently, or really, bound to such communication to varying degrees") is easy to skip and shouldn't be: it quarantines the essay from panpsychism. Benjamin does not claim the lamp is conscious; degrees of consciousness are explicitly bracketed as irrelevant to the linguistic thesis. Expression, not experience, is what everything has.

Scholars Whether the transcendental reading is right is contested ground: Fenves reads the early Benjamin as re-engineering the phenomenological reduction (a reduction to language rather than to consciousness), which fits this sentence's form; Procyshyn's affordance reading makes the imaginability claim a point about how meaning is directly apprehended in environments. The deflationary readers (Kohlenbach) take exactly this sentence as the moment the essay begs its question. The commentary's stance: read it as transcendental in form, theological in horizon, and note that Benjamin's own footnote in ¶2 shows him worrying the same seam.

AI The panpsychism quarantine matters for the AI register too: the recurring point that "the naming question is more significant than the consciousness question" is structurally Benjamin's own move here. Whether models are conscious is bracketed; whether they impart, name, and judge is assessable from their expression. The essay licenses exactly that methodological priority.

¶1.9 “An existence that stood wholly without relation to language is an idea; but this idea cannot be made fruitful even within the precinct of ideas whose circumference traces the idea of God.”

DE: »Ein Dasein, welches ganz ohne Beziehung zur Sprache wäre, ist eine Idee; aber diese Idee läßt sich auch im Bezirk der Ideen, deren Umkreis diejenige Gottes bezeichnet, nicht fruchtbar machen.«

The paragraph's strange, quiet ending, and the essay's first mention of God: not as creator or addressee but as a circumference. The thought: one can frame, as a bare idea, an existence with no relation to language; but the idea is sterile (nicht fruchtbar, cannot be made fruitful) even in the outermost precinct of ideas, the one whose boundary is traced by the idea of God. Even at the limit of the thinkable, where only God marks the edge, the language-less yields nothing. This is a fertility criterion for ideas, twin to ¶1.1's fecundity criterion for methods: true methods breed questions; this idea breeds nothing; discard it.

Geometry is worth pausing on. God appears as Umkreis, the drawn-around circle. The essay's later vertical axis (word of God above, thing-language below, man between) is here compressed into a single figure: God as the boundary of the realm within which anything can be an idea at all. Read forward, this is the seed of the fork the whole literature fights over: is "God" in this essay a theological agent or the limit-concept of communicability as such? The first occurrence is, strikingly, the limit-concept. (Agamben's reading starts breathing here; the theological readers get their footing at ¶7.10 and ¶13–16. Both are right about different sentences, which is the interesting fact.)

↔ ¶7.10 (in the name man imparts himself to God), ¶13 (God's breath), ¶26 (the word of God as the unity of the movement of language). Track whether "God" hardens from boundary to agent as the essay proceeds; it does, and the Genesis reading is where.

Sources Both of the sentence's vocabularies are doing real work, and each has an anchor. I. Language as such · ¶2 · The in/through distinction debuts; the abyss; the logos paradox placed at the center.

¶2: The abyss over which the essay hangs

Where we are. After ¶1's crescendo, a deliberate retreat: the German opens Nur soviel ist richtig, "only this much is correct." The paragraph scales the universal claim back to a terminological one, then immediately opens the essay's deepest problem: if everything expresses, what is the relation between the expressing (linguistic essence) and the expressed (quintessence)? The paragraph ends by placing a paradox at the center of the theory and forbidding it the beginning. It also contains the only footnote Benjamin wrote for the essay, in which he doubts his own architecture.

¶2.1–2 “Only this much is correct: that in this terminology every expression… is counted as language. And indeed expression, by its whole and innermost essence, is to be understood only as language…”

DE: »Nur soviel ist richtig, daß in dieser Terminologie jeder Ausdruck, sofern er eine Mitteilung geistiger Inhalte ist, der Sprache beigezählt wird. Und allerdings ist der Ausdruck seinem ganzen und innersten Wesen nach nur als Sprache zu verstehen …«

The retreat and the re-advance in two sentences. First: this is "only" terminology, expression counts as language insofar as it imparts. Then immediately: but expression, by its whole and innermost essence, is intelligible only as language. The concessive gesture turns out to concede nothing; the classification is re-grounded in essence before the sentence pair is done. This rhythm, retreat then deepen, is the essay's characteristic gait; watch for it again at ¶10 (the "terminological" question that is "of the highest metaphysical importance").

DE sofern, "insofar as": the first appearance of the scoping operator that will carry the essay's most precise claim (¶3.6, identity "only insofar as" communicable). Benjamin's system is built with quantifiers and scope, not imagery; the mysticism reputation dissolves under grammatical attention.

¶2.3–5 “…on the other hand, to understand a linguistic essence one must always ask of which quintessence it is the immediate expression… This 'itself' is a quintessence.”

DE: »… andererseits muß man, um ein sprachliches Wesen zu verstehen, immer fragen, für welches geistige Wesen es denn der unmittelbare Ausdruck sei. Das heißt: die deutsche Sprache z. B. ist keineswegs der Ausdruck für alles, was wir durch sie – vermeintlich – ausdrücken können, sondern sie ist der unmittelbare Ausdruck dessen, was sich in ihr mitteilt. Dieses ›Sich‹ ist ein geistiges Wesen.«

Here the essay's master distinction arrives, almost casually, inside an example. The German language is not the expression of everything we can (supposedly, vermeintlich) express through it (durch sie); it is the direct expression of what imparts itself in it (in ihr). Through versus in: the whole essay in two prepositions. What travels through a language treats it as a channel; what imparts itself in a language is not separable from it. And Benjamin's vermeintlich, "supposedly," already sneers at the through-picture a full paragraph before the "bourgeois conception" gets its name (¶7.6).

Then the extraordinary move: "This 'itself' is a quintessence." Benjamin promotes a reflexive pronoun to a subject. What speaks in a language is not a speaker; it is the sich, the self-imparting itself. Grammar is doing ontology: the essay locates the agent of communication inside the reflexive construction sich mitteilen, where the giver, the given, and the medium coincide. Every later theological formulation (the name in which man imparts himself to God) inherits its structure from this pronoun.

DE Watch unmittelbar, "immediate/direct": Jephcott's "direct expression" is der unmittelbare Ausdruck. The word contains Mittel, means: the im-mediate is what has no means interposed. The in/through distinction and the immediacy doctrine (¶4) are one thought in German, audible in the vocabulary itself.

↔ ¶3.3 states the in/through law in general form; ¶7.4–6 turns it polemical; ¶19 narrates its violation (the Fall is the making-mediate of language).

¶2.6–7 “It is therefore self-evident at the outset that the quintessence which imparts itself in language is not language itself but something to be distinguished from it. The view that the quintessence of a thing consists precisely in its language — this view, understood as hypothesis, is the great abyss into which all theory of language threatens to fall, and to keep itself hovering over that abyss, precisely over it, is that theory's task.”

DE: »Damit ist es zunächst selbstverständlich, daß das geistige Wesen, das sich in der Sprache mitteilt, nicht die Sprache selbst, sondern etwas von ihr zu Unterscheidendes ist. Die Ansicht, daß das geistige Wesen eines Dinges eben in seiner Sprache besteht – diese Ansicht als Hypothesis verstanden, ist der große Abgrund, dem alle Sprachtheorie zu verfallen droht, und über, gerade über ihm sich schwebend zu erhalten ist ihre Aufgabe.«

One of the great sentences of the essay, and its most honest. The tempting view, that a thing's quintessence just is its language, would collapse the distinction ¶2.6 called self-evident. Taken as a hypothesis, as a premise laid down at the start, that view is the abyss into which all linguistic theory threatens to fall. And then the task-definition: not to avoid the abyss, not to bridge it, but to hold itself hovering (schwebend) precisely over it. The theory's relation to its own deepest temptation is the theory. Linguistic theory is defined here as a sustained act of not falling into the identity it cannot stop approaching; the essay will spend its length approaching it (¶3.6 grants the identity "insofar as communicable"; ¶7.9 grants it wholly, for man, in the name).

Benjamin's footnote (his only one): "Or is it, rather, the temptation to place at the outset a hypothesis that constitutes an abyss for all philosophizing?" The footnote generalizes the danger: perhaps the abyss is not the specific content of this hypothesis but the act of beginning from hypothesis at all. This is the essay doubting its own genre while writing it, and it matters for how we read everything: Benjamin is not laying down axioms and deriving; he is trying to let the structure emerge such that the identity-claim arrives at the center, earned, rather than at the beginning, posited. The footnote is the essay's method made anxious and explicit.

DE schwebend, hovering: the same word the early German Romantics (Fichte's Schweben of the imagination, Schlegel's hovering of irony) used for the productive suspension between opposites. Speculative as allusion, secure as resonance. Benjamin wrote his dissertation on exactly this circle three years later. The German syntax itself hovers: und über, gerade über ihm sich schwebend zu erhalten suspends and then resumes the construction around the abyss. The earlier electronic reading silently supplied a second ihm, making the grammar easier but flattening the enactment; the GS reading is restored here.

Scholars Note how the abyss sentence adjudicates the field in advance: the deflationary readers (Kohlenbach, Witte) accuse the essay of falling into the abyss (asserting the identity dogmatically); the mystical readers sometimes cheerfully dive in (word = essence, the naive-magical view the essay itself refutes at ¶16). The sentence itself claims the third position: suspension. Whether the essay keeps its balance is the real question a commentary must keep live to the end.

¶2.8–9 “The distinction between the quintessence and the linguistic essence in which it imparts is the most primordial in any inquiry into the theory of language… found in the double sense of the word λόγος. Nevertheless this paradox, as a solution, has its place at the center of the theory of language, but remains a paradox, and insoluble, where it stands at the beginning.”

DE: »Die Unterscheidung zwischen dem geistigen Wesen und dem sprachlichen, in dem es mitteilt, ist die ursprünglichste in einer sprachtheoretischen Untersuchung … die oft behauptete Identität zwischen dem geistigen und sprachlichen Wesen eine tiefe und unbegreifliche Paradoxie bildet, deren Ausdruck man in dem Doppelsinn des Wortes Λόγος gefunden hat. Dennoch hat diese Paradoxie als Lösung ihre Stelle im Zentrum der Sprachtheorie, bleibt aber Paradoxie und da unlösbar, wo sie am Anfang steht.«

The paragraph's close makes topology out of logic. The identity of quintessence and linguistic essence is a paradox, the one compressed into the double sense of logos (word and reason, speech and thought). Benjamin neither dissolves nor rejects it; he assigns it a location. At the center, the paradox is a solution; at the beginning, it is insoluble. Positions in the architecture of a theory change the modality of its claims. This is a very early statement of a lifelong Benjaminian conviction: truth is a matter of configuration and placement (constellation, in the later vocabulary), not of derivation from premises.

I. Language as such · ¶3 · The catechism paragraph; the in/through law in general form; the "only insofar" hinge.

¶3: In, not through

Where we are. The shortest paragraph so far, and the densest. The form shifts to catechism: question, answer, consequence. Everything ¶2 developed through the German-language example is now stated as law, and the paragraph ends on the sentence that carries more of the essay than any other, the "only insofar" that scopes the identity of quintessence and linguistic essence and lets the theory hover over its abyss instead of falling in.

¶3.1–2 “What does language impart? It imparts the quintessence that corresponds to it.”

DE: »Was teilt die Sprache mit? Sie teilt das ihr entsprechende geistige Wesen mit.«

The catechism form deserves comment before the content. The essay proceeds, here and at ¶6 ("Why name them? To whom does man communicate himself?"), by posing the naive question in its plainest form and letting the answer reorganize the field. This is a pedagogy with a lineage: the form of the catechism, but also of the Passover questions, and of the Socratic opening move. The essay teaches by interrogation because its central claim (language has no speaker) forbids it a lecturing subject. The liturgical resonance is speculative; the formal observation stands on its own.

Das ihr entsprechende geistige Wesen: the quintessence that corresponds to it. Correspondence: neither identity nor arbitrary attachment. Each language has its own quintessence, fitted to it. The correspondence doctrine quietly rules out both horns in advance: the word is neither the essence itself (naive mysticism) nor a conventional label (bourgeois sign).

¶3.3 “It is fundamental to know that this quintessence imparts itself in language and not through language.”

DE: »Es ist fundamental zu wissen, daß dieses geistige Wesen sich in der Sprache mitteilt und nicht durch die Sprache.«

The law itself, flagged by Benjamin as fundamental. Everything in the essay is an unfolding of this sentence, and every corruption the essay will later narrate (the Fall, prattle, the word as means) is a violation of it. In language: the imparting and the medium are one event; nothing is loaded into a vehicle. Through language: the picture in which content pre-exists its expression and language is the pipe it travels down. The bourgeois conception (¶7) is the through-picture systematized; the Fall (¶19) is the through-picture become the human condition.

The in/through law is why "medium" (¶4.11) must not be read as "means of transmission": Benjamin's medium is what imparting happens in, the way a storm happens in the atmosphere, not what it is sent through, the way a parcel is sent through the post. Menninghaus's formula: the immediacy of the medium is its magic. The sentence also grounds the essay's strangest consequences in advance: if imparting is in-not-through, then what is imparted can never be detached, summarized, or paraphrased out of its language, which is why every language is an "incommensurable, uniquely constituted infinity" (¶4.14), and why translation will have to be re-founded as transformation of media rather than transfer of content (¶18.2).

¶3.6 “The quintessence is identical with the linguistic essence only insofar as it is impartable.”

The hinge of the essay. Seven German words: identity, scoped by impartability. The quintessence of a thing is its linguistic essence exactly to the extent that it is mitteilbar, impart-able. This is how the theory hovers over the abyss of ¶2.7: the tempting identity is neither asserted (fall) nor denied (sterility) but quantified. Whatever in a thing can be parted-with is language; whatever cannot remains quintessence in reserve.

Two consequences, both drawn immediately by the essay. First (¶3.7), the linguistic essence of a thing is defined as its impartable: "What is impartable in a quintessence, that is its linguistic essence." Second (¶3.8), languages impart their corresponding linguistic essences wholly but their quintessences only partially, "insofar as" the quintessence is lodged directly in the linguistic. The residue, quintessence that is not impartable, is real and does work later: it is why revelation must be defined (¶11) as the region where nothing is held in reserve, why man alone is "communicable without residue" (¶7.11), and why there can be a "symbol of the noncommunicable" (¶25).

The identity of quintessence and linguistic essence is thus scoped not by what is communicated but by what is communicABLE: a modal fact, a disposition. The essay's ontology is an ontology of potentials. This is also the sentence to hold against Offenbarung (revelation) at ¶11: the offen-bar, the reveal-able made absolute.

I. Language as such · ¶4 · The lamp; medium; magic defined as immediacy; the infinity of every language.

¶4: The language-lamp, the medium, the magic

Where we are. The first movement's summit. The paragraph produces the essay's most famous example (the lamp), its most important definition (medium), its most misread word (magic), and its most vertiginous claim (every language contains its own infinity). It is also where the essay's prose runs closest to its subject: the paragraph performs the self-relation it describes, circling its own proposition through reformulations ("Or, more precisely… Or: …") as if language were being made to impart itself about itself.

¶4.1–3 “Language imparts the linguistic essence of things. Its clearest appearance, however, is language itself. The answer to the question, what does language impart, is therefore: every language imparts itself.”

DE: »Die Sprache teilt das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge mit. Dessen klarste Erscheinung ist aber die Sprache selbst. Die Antwort auf die Frage: was teilt die Sprache mit? lautet also: Jede Sprache teilt sich selbst mit.«

The catechism of ¶3 gets its final answer, and it is reflexive: all language imparts itself. The derivation is compact: language imparts linguistic essence (¶3's bookkeeping); the clearest appearance of linguistic essence is language itself; therefore what language imparts, in the clearest case, is language. The essay knows this sounds like a tautology and will spend ¶4.6–8 disarming that appearance. What should be heard first is the displacement: the question "what does language communicate?" expected a content for an answer and received a subject instead. There is no cargo; there is only the medium showing itself.

¶4.4 “The language of this lamp, for example, imparts not the lamp… but: the language-lamp, the lamp in imparting, the lamp in expression.”

DE: »Die Sprache dieser Lampe z. B. teilt nicht die Lampe mit (denn das geistige Wesen der Lampe, sofern es mitteilbar ist, ist durchaus nicht die Lampe selbst), sondern: die Sprach-Lampe, die Lampe in der Mitteilung, die Lampe im Ausdruck.«

The lamp. Benjamin reaches for the philosopher's stock example (the lamp on the desk, descendant of the lectern in Husserl's seminar) and breaks it open. What the lamp's language imparts is not the lamp, because the lamp's impartable is not the lamp itself; it is the Sprach-Lampe, the hyphenated creature, the lamp-as-imparting. The three appositions descend in abstraction: language-lamp, lamp in imparting, lamp in expression. This is the correspondence doctrine of ¶3.2 made concrete: between the thing itself (unreachable in imparting) and the arbitrary sign (the bourgeois picture), there is the thing-in-communication, a third entity the essay is alone in naming.

Dieser Lampe, "this lamp": the demonstrative matters. Benjamin does not say "the language of lamps." Each thing's language is indexed to it; the essay's ontology is of particulars imparting themselves, not of kinds. That each this has its own language is what makes overnaming (¶21) a tragedy rather than a bookkeeping error: the hundred human languages name kinds over the particulars.

Scholars Procyshyn's "the lamp in communication" is his central exhibit for the affordance reading: what the lamp imparts is what it affords, its graspability-for-us. The reading works for this sentence and strains at ¶7.10 (imparting to God), which is worth remembering as a calibration of how far naturalizing readings reach into this essay.

¶4.5–8 “For in language the case is this: the linguistic essence of things is their language… This sentence is untautological… On this 'is' (equivalent to 'is immediately') everything rests.”

DE: »Denn in der Sprache verhält es sich so: Das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge ist ihre Sprache. Das Verständnis der Sprachtheorie hängt davon ab, diesen Satz zu einer Klarheit zu bringen, die auch jeden Schein einer Tautologie in ihm vernichtet. Dieser Satz ist untautologisch, denn er bedeutet: das, was an einem geistigen Wesen mitteilbar ist, ist seine Sprache. Auf diesem ›ist‹ (gleich ›ist unmittelbar‹) beruht alles.«

The italicized thesis, and then the essay's most explicit instruction for reading itself: bring this sentence to a clarity that annihilates even the appearance of tautology. "The linguistic essence of things is their language" looks empty, X is X. It is not, because the subject and predicate arrive from different directions: linguistic essence is the impartable-of-a-thing (¶3.7, a modal fact about the thing); its language is the actual medium in which it imparts. The sentence asserts that the potential and the medium coincide, and coincide immediately: the "is" means "is immediately," with no relay, no representation, no sign standing between what the thing can give and the language in which it gives it.

"On this 'is'… everything depends" is literal. The entire essay is a theory of this copula. Where the "is immediately" holds, there is name, paradise, knowledge; where a relay is inserted, there is sign, judgment, prattle, Babel. The Fall (¶19) is nothing but the corruption of this copula: the word that must communicate something (other than itself) has lost the "is immediately" and become a means. One preposition (in/through) and one copula (is/is-immediately) carry the metaphysics.

DE vernichtet, annihilates: the violence of the verb signals how much Benjamin fears the tautology-appearance; a theory whose central sentence reads as empty dies of it. The GS text confirms Benjamin's emphasis of the complete thesis-sentence, and its critical report records the proposition as doubly underlined in all four typescripts. The typography is therefore argumentative evidence, not an English editorial addition: Benjamin asks the reader to hold the apparently tautological sentence apart before he dissolves its appearance.

¶4.9–10 “— Not that what is impartable in a quintessence appears most clearly in its language… rather, this impartable is immediately language itself… What is impartable in a quintessence, in this it imparts itself.”

DE: »Nicht, was an einem geistigen Wesen mitteilbar ist, erscheint am klarsten in seiner Sprache … sondern dieses Mitteilbare ist unmittelbar die Sprache selbst. Oder: die Sprache eines geistigen Wesens ist unmittelbar dasjenige, was an ihm mitteilbar ist. Was an einem geistigen Wesen mitteilbar ist, in dem teilt es sich mit …«

Benjamin catches his own transitional formulation (¶4.2's "clearest manifestation") sliding back toward the through-picture, and corrects it in real time. If the impartable merely appeared most clearly in language, language would be its best display case, a privileged vehicle, and the abyss would reopen. The correction: the impartable does not appear in language; it is language, immediately. The dashes and the "Or:" reformulations show the essay editing itself before the reader, which is the honest form of a theory that denies itself an outside standpoint: there is no metalanguage to state the theory in; there is only saying it again, more purely. Hamacher's point about Benjamin's style (the consistency of the philosophy lies in its manner of imparting) has its best evidence here.

DE Capacity for communication renders dieses Mitteilbare, "this impartable," a nominalized potential. The -bar ontology again: what a quintessence's language is, is its impartable, a standing capacity, not an occurrent act. Weber's potentiality thesis is not an imported frame; it is the plain grammar of these sentences.

¶4.11–12 “…every language imparts itself in itself; it is, in the purest sense, the 'medium' of imparting. Mediality, which is the immediacy of all quintessential imparting, is the fundamental problem of the theory of language, and if one wishes to call this immediacy magical, then the primal problem of language is its magic.”

DE: »Oder genauer: jede Sprache teilt sich in sich selbst mit, sie ist im reinsten Sinne das ›Medium‹ der Mitteilung. Das Mediale, das ist die Unmittelbarkeit aller geistigen Mitteilung, ist das Grundproblem der Sprachtheorie, und wenn man diese Unmittelbarkeit magisch nennen will, so ist das Urproblem der Sprache ihre Magie.«

The definition the whole literature orbits. Language is medium in the purest sense: that in which imparting occurs, not that by which it is transmitted. And then the sentence Jephcott damages: the German subject is das Mediale, "the medial," mediality itself, and the sentence defines it as die Unmittelbarkeit, the immediacy, of all quintessential imparting. Jephcott's "Mediation" imports precisely the wrong concept: mediation is the interposition of a middle term; das Mediale is the character of being medium, and Benjamin's claim is that this mediality is immediacy. The medium does not mediate. Nothing stands between the impartable and its language, because they coincide (¶4.5's copula). The English sentence, read innocently, says mediation is immediacy, a flat paradox; the German says mediality is immediacy, the essay's deepest thought. This is the single most consequential translation correction our own version will make. (Menninghaus built the classic reading on this sentence; Hamacher's "Intensive Languages" thinks it through to the end: if mediality is immediacy, then knowing itself is a mode of imparting, and the subject-object relation is a fallen derivative.)

Then magic. The word arrives under an explicit "if one chooses to call": Benjamin defines magic into the system as a name for immediacy. Occultism never gets a foothold. Language is magical exactly insofar as it works without means, the way magic in every tradition is action-at-no-distance, efficacy without mechanism. The provenance discipline holds: read "magic" as a technical term (mediality-as-immediacy) first, and only then hear its resonances (the creative word, the efficacious Name) as resonances. The essay licenses both readings in one clause: Urproblem, primal problem, is a word with theological depth wearing a Kantian costume.

↔ Magic returns for matter (¶12, "there is also a magic of matter"), for the two magics of name and judgment (¶19), and as "the mute magic of nature" (¶16). Track the word: its migrations map the essay's argument.

¶4.13–14 “At the same time, the phrase 'the magic of language' points to something else: to its infinity… and therefore each language is indwelt by its own incommensurable, uniquely constituted infinity. Its linguistic essence, not its verbal contents, marks its boundary.”

DE: »Zugleich deutet das Wort von der Magie der Sprache auf ein anderes: auf ihre Unendlichkeit. Sie ist durch die Unmittelbarkeit bedingt. Denn gerade, weil durch die Sprache sich nichts mitteilt, kann, was in der Sprache sich mitteilt, nicht von außen beschränkt oder gemessen werden, und darum wohnt jeder Sprache ihre inkommensurable einziggeartete Unendlichkeit inne. Ihr sprachliches Wesen, nicht ihre verbalen Inhalte bezeichnen ihre Grenze.«

The movement's last consequence, and its strangest. Because nothing is imparted through language, what is imparted in it cannot be limited or measured from outside; no external standard can survey a medium that has no outside relay. Therefore every language contains (wohnt inne, dwells within it) its own infinity: incommensurable, of its own unique kind (einziggeartet). Languages are not bigger and smaller containers; they are non-comparable infinities, and each is bounded only by its own linguistic essence, never by its stock of contents.

This is where Fenves's "intensive infinity" and the mathematical background earn their place. Benjamin wrote this weeks after debating Scholem on language and mathematics; the vocabulary (incommensurable, measure, limit, infinity) is set theory's, and the thought, infinities distinguished by kind rather than size, is Cantor's, transposed. The essay that declined to treat mathematics is visibly thinking with it. The "frontier" sentence also plants what ¶9 will harvest: language as intensive totality (the whole present in the medium) against extensive totality (coverage of contents). And it explains in advance why the essay's Fall is a fall into measurement: judgment, the fallen magic, is exactly the external standard the pure medium excludes.

II. The name · ¶5 · The turn to man; the naming thesis; the title's two-part logic activated.

¶5: Man, the namer

Where we are. The second movement opens. Movement I built a metaphysics of language with no special place for humans: everything imparts itself in its own medium. Now the general theory is "applied to man," and the application yields the essay's anthropological thesis in nine sentences. The paragraph's shape is a syllogism, an objection, and a self-correction that ends in the italicized definition of the human. The title's two halves (language as such; the language of man) hinge exactly here.

¶5.1–2 “The linguistic essence of things is their language; this sentence, applied to man, says: the linguistic essence of man is his language. That is: man imparts his own quintessence in his language.”

DE: »Das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge ist ihre Sprache; dieser Satz auf den Menschen angewandt besagt: Das sprachliche Wesen des Menschen ist seine Sprache. Das heißt: Der Mensch teilt sein eignes geistiges Wesen in seiner Sprache mit.«

The application is presented as mechanical substitution: put "man" in the formula where "things" stood. The uniformity is the point. Man enters the theory as one imparting being among all the others, under the same law (in, not through). Whatever distinguishes him will have to be earned inside the formula, not granted before it. The essay's humanism, such as it is, begins from a demotion.

↔ The same substitution-form recurs at ¶7.9–11, where it suddenly stops being mechanical: man's linguistic essence will turn out to be language itself, and the formula will buckle into theology.

¶5.3–4 “But the language of man speaks in words. Man therefore imparts his own quintessence (insofar as it is impartable) by naming all other things.”

DE: »Die Sprache des Menschen spricht aber in Worten. Der Mensch teilt also sein eignes geistiges Wesen (sofern es mitteilbar ist) mit, indem er alle anderen Dinge benennt.«

Two sentences that carry more weight than their size suggests. First the differentia: man's language speaks in words. (Note who speaks: die Sprache spricht, the language speaks, in words. The abolished instrumental speaker of ¶3.4 stays abolished; the grammar keeps the medium in subject position.) Then the thesis with its astonishing structure: man imparts his own quintessence by naming all other things. Self-communication is routed entirely through the naming of what is not the self. There is no sentence in the essay where more hangs on a preposition-less indem, "by": the human relation to self passes by way of the world.

This is the essay's answer to expressivism before the fact: man does not express his inwardness and then, secondarily, label objects. Naming the other is the imparting of the self. The scoping parenthesis (sofern es mitteilbar ist) meanwhile keeps the ¶3.6 hinge engaged: even here, only the impartable of man gets imparted. DE: »Kennen wir aber noch andere Sprachen, welche die Dinge benennen? Man wende nicht ein, wir kennten keine Sprache außer der des Menschen, das ist unwahr. The objection Benjamin stages and the distinction that defuses it are the title of the essay doing its work in the text. "Language as such" (Sprache überhaupt) and "the language of man" are not synonyms and not rivals; they are genus and species, and the deepest errors of linguistic theory come from collapsing them. We know many languages (the lamp's, the mountain's, the fox's: ¶6 will list them); we know only one naming language. To take naming language as the model of language as such is anthropocentrism as method, and it forecloses exactly the insights the essay lives on: that things impart themselves mutely, that human naming is a response to those mute impartings (¶17–18), and that the Fall was the collapse of this very distinction, the human word mistaking itself for language as such (¶19).

DE überhaupt: "as such," "in general," the word of the title. Caygill's frame (the essay as part of Benjamin's campaign to widen Kant's concept of experience) is visible in the title's grammar.

¶5.8 “— The linguistic essence of man, then, is this: that he names things.”

The definition, set off by a dash and italicized in the English edition: the linguistic essence of man is that he names things. Formally it completes the substitution begun at ¶5.1; materially it replaces every classical definition of the human (rational animal, political animal, toolmaker) with a linguistic office. Man is the namer: not the possessor of language as a faculty (that would be the bourgeois picture, language as equipment) but the being whose own impartable is the act of naming.

Two features of the definition repay attention. It is relational: naming requires the named, so the definition binds the human constitutively to things, to the world that ¶6 will show imparting itself to man as if by appointment. And it is an office rather than a property: something that can be executed faithfully or betrayed. The entire drama to come (the proper name as the frontier of the divine, translation as fidelity to the mute, the Fall as the namer turned judge, overnaming as the office become violence) is the biography of this office. The definition has a normative core that the essay will spend its second half making explicit.

II. The name · ¶6 · The addressee question; things impart themselves to man; the withheld answer.

¶6: To whom does the lamp speak?

Where we are. The shortest paragraph of the essay so far, built entirely of questions: seven of them in nine sentences, ending on one left hanging. Movement I asked what language imparts (itself) and how (in, not through). ¶5 defined man as namer. Now the essay asks the question every instrumental theory answers first and this theory has deferred longest: to whom? The paragraph answers it for things (to man) and then, at the moment the same answer is due for man himself, stops. The suspension is structural: the answer arrives only at the end of ¶7, and it will be the essay's theological turn.

¶6.1–2 “Why name? To whom does man impart himself? — But is this question, in man's case, other than in the case of other impartings (languages)?”

DE: »Wozu benennt? Wem teilt der Mensch sich mit? – Aber ist diese Frage beim Menschen eine andere als bei anderen Mitteilungen (Sprachen)?«

Wozu, what-for: the purpose question, the one a theory of language as equipment answers trivially (to inform, to coordinate, to persuade) and this essay cannot answer trivially, because it has abolished the instrumental frame in which purposes live. And immediately the leveling move that is this essay's signature: before answering for man, Benjamin insists the question is generic. Every language, the lamp's included, faces the to-whom question. Man gets no special interrogation; he gets the standard form. The demotion of ¶5.1 (man enters the theory by substitution into the general formula) is still in force.

↔ The parenthesis "(languages)" glossing "communications" quietly enforces ¶1.4's identification: every imparting is a language; the terms are now interchangeable in the essay's grammar.

¶6.3–4 “To whom does the lamp impart itself? The mountain range? The fox? — Here, however, the answer runs: to man. This is no anthropomorphism.”

DE: »Wem teilt die Lampe sich mit? Das Gebirge? Der Fuchs? – Hier aber lautet die Antwort: dem Menschen. Das ist kein Anthropomorphismus.«

The bestiary is chosen with care: the lamp (artifact), the mountain (inanimate nature), the fox (animate nature). Three ontological registers, one answer. Everything below man imparts itself to man; he is the addressee of the whole created order. The essay knows exactly how this sounds, and the denial arrives in four words: das ist kein Anthropomorphismus. The defense comes in ¶6.5, but note first what kind of claim is being made. This is an appointment, an office of reception, the complement of ¶5.8's office of naming. Man is defined twice over in this movement: as the being who names all things, and as the being to whom all things impart themselves. The two offices will fuse in ¶17–18 as translation: receiving the mute imparting and raising it into name.

¶6.5 “The truth of this answer proves itself in knowledge, and perhaps also in art.”

DE: »Die Wahrheit dieser Antwort erweist sich in der Erkenntnis und vielleicht auch in der Kunst.«

The defense against anthropomorphism, compressed to one evidentiary sentence. That things impart themselves to man is proven by the fact that knowledge happens: Erkenntnis is the reception of things' self-imparting, and its very existence certifies the address. On the bourgeois picture, knowledge of things is a human accomplishment performed on mute objects; on this picture it is a correspondence delivered, and its arrival is the proof. Hamacher's formula (cognizability is impartability) is the full unfolding of this sentence: what can be known of a thing and what the thing parts with are the same, so every act of knowledge is evidence of an imparting received.

The quiet "perhaps also in art" (vielleicht) plants a seed the essay harvests at ¶22: painting and sculpture as founded on the thing-languages, art as another mode of receiving what things impart. The hedge word is honest; the theory of art here is a promissory note.

If knowledge is reception of things' self-impartings, then a system trained on the traces of the world can come to know the world, which is otherwise a scandal. The scandal exists only for the bourgeois picture, where knowledge requires a subject performing acts on mute data. (Our extension.)

¶6.6–7 “Moreover: if lamp and mountains and the fox did not impart themselves to man, how then should he name them? But he does name them; he imparts himself by naming them.”

DE: »Zudem: wenn Lampe und Gebirge und der Fuchs sich dem Menschen nicht mitteilen würden, wie sollte er sie dann benennen? Aber er benennt sie; er teilt sich mit, indem er sie benennt.«

The second defense is transcendental, and it settles something ¶5 left open. Naming presupposes reception: if things did not first impart themselves to man, he would have nothing to name and no way to name it. Whatever Adam does in Genesis 2, on this account he does not invent labels for blank objects; he answers. Every name is a response to a prior address. This single sentence is the essay's break with the picture of naming as sovereign imposition, and it is why translation, not creation, will turn out to be the deep structure of human language (¶17–18): the name receives the thing's mute language and carries it over.

Then the fusion: he names them; he imparts himself by naming them. ¶5.4's astonishing structure returns with its ground now visible. Man's self-communication is his reception-and-elevation of everything else. The italicized pronouns in the English (he, them) mark the crossing: self imparted precisely in the act directed at the other.

Scholars Kathrin Busch's reading (reception precedes naming; human language originates as the translating of things' languages) is the cleanest statement of this point in the literature. Stern's formulation: human language responds to an always-already-meaningful reality rather than imposing meaning on a mute one.

¶6.8 “To whom does he impart himself?”

DE: »Wem teilt er sich mit?«

Four words, and the paragraph ends. The question it opened with, now stripped of the generic frame, aimed at man alone, and pointedly not answered. The relay so far runs upward: lamp, mountain, fox impart to man. The structure demands a next addressee above man, and the essay makes the reader feel the demand before naming its object. This is composition as argument: by the time ¶7.10 says "to God," the position has already been built and only the name is missing. Whether what fills the position is a theological addressee or the position itself divinized is the fork the whole literature disputes (see overview, Stance); what is not disputable is that the essay engineered the vacancy first.

II. The name · ¶7 · The bourgeois conception named and demolished; the theological turn in one sentence.

¶7: The two conceptions

Where we are. The polemical center of the essay. The suspended question (to whom?) is made to wait one paragraph longer while Benjamin sets a trap: a single diagnostic question (by names, or in them?) that any theory of language must answer, and that the false theory cannot answer without exposing itself. The trap closes on what he names the bourgeois conception of language. Its demolition clears the ground for the sentence the whole essay has been withholding, the theological turn: in the name, man imparts himself to God.

¶7.1–2 “Before this question can be answered, it must once more be examined: how does man impart himself? A deep distinction is to be drawn, an alternative posed, before which the essentially false opinion of language unfailingly betrays itself.”

DE: »Ehe diese Frage zu beantworten ist, gilt es noch einmal zu prüfen: Wie teilt der Mensch sich mit? Es ist ein tiefer Unterschied zu machen, eine Alternative zu stellen, vor der mit Sicherheit die wesentlich falsche Meinung von der Sprache sich verrät.«

Method before answer: the to-whom question cannot be answered until the how question is settled, because the false answer to how generates a false addressee (other people, ¶7.6). Note the forensic vocabulary: the false view verrät sich, betrays itself, gives itself away. Benjamin constructs the coming question as an interrogation under which error confesses. The confidence is striking, and it is the confidence of ¶7.4's method: the question is built so that its very form does the work.

¶7.3–4 “Does man impart his quintessence through the names that he gives to things? Or in them? In the paradox of this way of posing the question lies its answer.”

DE: »Teilt der Mensch sein geistiges Wesen durch die Namen mit, die er den Dingen gibt? Oder in ihnen? In der Paradoxie dieser Fragestellung liegt ihre Beantwortung.«

The in/through law (¶3.3), now aimed at names in particular, and sharpened by a paradox: the names in question are names of things. How could man impart his own essence in labels attached to what he is not? The paradox is that the second answer (in them) sounds impossible and is correct; ¶5.4 already committed the essay to it (man imparts himself by naming all other things). What ¶7 adds is the diagnosis of why the first answer (by them) destroys itself, which is the work of the next two sentences. The method here is ¶2.9 in action: the paradox placed correctly becomes a solution.

DE durch die Namen, through/by the names: the German uses the same durch that carried the through-picture since ¶2. Jephcott's shift to "by" for this paragraph is defensible English but breaks the audible continuity; the reader of the German hears one preposition indicted across the whole essay.

¶7.5–6 “Whoever believes that man imparts his quintessence through names cannot in turn assume that it is his quintessence that he imparts… And he can in turn only assume that he imparts factual subject matter to other men…”

DE: »Wer da glaubt, der Mensch teile sein geistiges Wesen durch die Namen mit, der kann wiederum nicht annehmen, daß es sein geistiges Wesen sei, das er mitteile … Und er kann wiederum nur annehmen, er teile eine Sache anderen Menschen mit, denn das geschieht durch das Wort, durch das ich ein Ding bezeichne.«

The reductio, run in two steps. If names are instruments of transmission, then what they transmit is whatever instruments can carry: information about the things they designate, eine Sache, factual subject matter. The self does not fit through the channel. On the through-picture, man can send facts to other men; he can never impart his quintessence, because a means only ever carries content detachable from both sender and medium. The through-theorist has not chosen a modest account of self-expression; he has made self-expression impossible and replaced it with data transfer. The essay's own alternative (self imparted in the naming, not sent through it) is the only surviving account of how anyone was ever present in their words at all.

DE A pronoun slips in the German: "das Wort, durch das ich ein Ding bezeichne," the word through which I designate a thing. The only first-person singular in the paragraph surfaces inside the bourgeois picture, the picture that runs on egos emitting signals. Whether deliberate or not (the essay was written fast, as a letter), the grammar performs the diagnosis: the "I" belongs to the fallen frame. Fenves's reading (the positing, judging "I" as the source of inauthentic meaning) finds its smallest piece of evidence here. Speculative as intention; the textual fact stands.

¶7.7–8 “This view is the bourgeois conception of language… It holds: the means of imparting is the word, its object the subject matter, its addressee a human being.”

DE: »Diese Ansicht ist die bürgerliche Auffassung der Sprache, deren Unhaltbarkeit und Leere sich mit steigender Deutlichkeit im folgenden ergeben soll. Sie besagt: Das Mittel der Mitteilung ist das Wort, ihr Gegenstand die Sache, ihr Adressat ein Mensch.«

The naming of the enemy, and the only overtly political word in the essay: bürgerlich, bourgeois. In 1916 this is not yet the vocabulary of Marxism (a decade away for Benjamin); it is the vocabulary of the youth movement he had just broken with, where "bourgeois" named the philistine reduction of every value to utility. Still, the choice of word is a hinge in his biography: the entire later program (language as commodity, information as the degraded form of experience, the critique of communication in "The Storyteller" and the Kraus essay) grows from this sentence. The bourgeois conception treats the word as currency: a neutral token, valuable only for what it buys in the hearer. Jacobson's political-theology frame is right to start here: the essay's theology arrives as a polemic against a social form of language, and the Fall (¶19) will be, among other things, the genesis-myth of that social form.

The schema itself is a perfect triple: means (word), object (subject matter), addressee (man). Benjamin compresses every instrumental theory of language, ancient and modern, into three slots. The compression is what makes the next sentence possible.

¶7.9 “The other conception, by contrast, knows no means, no object, and no addressee of imparting.”

DE: »Dagegen kennt die andere kein Mittel, keinen Gegenstand und keinen Adressaten der Mitteilung.«

The counter-schema is the negation of all three slots at once. Not a rival mechanism: an abolition of the mechanical frame. Language without means (imparting is immediate), without object (nothing detachable is sent), without addressee. And then the essay does something stranger than the negation: having denied the addressee-slot, it immediately fills it.

¶7.10 “It holds: in the name, the quintessence of man imparts itself to God.”

DE: »Sie besagt: im Namen teilt das geistige Wesen des Menschen sich Gott mit.«

The sentence the essay was built to withhold, and the exact point where philosophy of language becomes theology. Every structural pressure the essay has generated discharges here: the to-whom question left hanging at ¶6.8, the upward relay (things to man, man to...), the abolished addressee of ¶7.9. God enters the essay as the answer to a question of grammar.

Read the sentence against ¶7.9 and the real difficulty appears: the other conception "knows no addressee," and its defining formula names one. The resolution is that God does not occupy the addressee-slot of the bourgeois schema (a receiver at the far end of a channel, who could be informed of something). "To God" names the direction of an imparting that has no far end: self-communication in the name is oriented absolutely, not delivered somewhere. This is why both sides of the scholarly fork can cite the sentence. Agamben reads "God" as the name of that absolute orientation itself, communicability with no worldly addressee (the limit-concept). Handelman, Gasché, and the theological readers answer that an orientation with no one at its term is not yet prayer, and the essay's word is Gott, not "the absolute." The commentary's stance: hold the sentence to its own precision. It does not say man informs God of anything; it says man's quintessence imparts itself, in the name, God-ward. The minimal reading the sentence forces is that human language is vertically oriented, that its completion lies outside every horizontal exchange. Everything beyond that minimum is the fork, and the essay will load more weight onto the theological side as Genesis takes over (¶13 on).

II. The name · ¶8 · The name as innermost nature of language; man communicable without residue; pure language; creation completed.

¶8: The name

Where we are. The essay's first summit. Having produced "the name" as the site of man's God-ward imparting, Benjamin now says what the name is, and the paragraph delivers the essay's densest sequence of theses: the name as the innermost nature of language; man as the only being whose quintessence is communicable without residue; the first appearance of "pure language"; man as completer of creation; the name as "the language of language." The paragraph also contains the sentence where Jephcott's translation drops the most important three words in the essay.

¶8.1–2 “The name, within the domain of language, has this sense alone and this incomparably high meaning: that it is the innermost essence of language itself. The name is that through which nothing further imparts itself, and in which language itself, and absolutely, imparts itself.”

DE: »Der Name hat im Bereich der Sprache einzig diesen Sinn und diese unvergleichlich hohe Bedeutung: daß er das innerste Wesen der Sprache selbst ist. Der Name ist dasjenige, durch das sich nichts mehr, und in dem die Sprache selbst und absolut sich mitteilt.«

The second sentence is the paragraph's foundation, and the English has quietly broken it. The German says: the name is that through which nothing further imparts itself (durch das sich nichts mehr [mitteilt]), and in which language itself, and absolutely, imparts itself. Jephcott's "that through which, and in which, language itself communicates itself absolutely" deletes the nichts mehr and with it the sentence's whole architecture. The name is being defined on both sides of the essay's master opposition at once: on the through-side, it is the terminus, the point where instrumentality runs out and nothing further can be sent; on the in-side, it is the consummation, the medium in which language imparts itself without reserve. One sentence, both prepositions, opposite verdicts. The name ends language-as-means and perfects language-as-medium. This is the single most consequential translation correction our version will make after ¶4's das Mediale, and unlike that one it changes the doctrine, since the English sentence (name as that through which language communicates) readmits the instrumental picture at the exact point the German excludes it. The initial electronic German source was defective here; direct collation now confirms that durch das sich nichts mehr is the GS reading, so the correction is no longer conjectural.

"Sole purpose" is also worth weight: einzig diesen Sinn, only this sense. The name has no function besides being language's innermost essence. Every utilitarian account of names (labels for re-identification, handles for reference) is dismissed in a word.

¶8.3–4 “In the name, the quintessence that imparts itself is language. Where the quintessence in its imparting is language itself in its absolute wholeness, there alone is the name, and there is the name alone.”

DE: »Im Namen ist das geistige Wesen, das sich mitteilt, die Sprache. Wo das geistige Wesen in seiner Mitteilung die Sprache selbst in ihrer absoluten Ganzheit ist, da allein gibt es den Namen, und da gibt es den Namen allein.«

The definition by chiasmus: da allein gibt es den Namen, und da gibt es den Namen allein. Only there is the name; there, only the name is. Jephcott preserves the crossing well. The two halves say different things: the first locates the name (it exists only where imparted essence and language coincide wholly); the second purifies the location (where that coincidence holds, nothing besides the name is present, no residue, no content, no sign). The chiasmus is the right form for the thought, since the thought is itself a perfect reciprocal: name and coincidence-of- essence-with-language define each other.

Here the abyss of ¶2.7 should be heard again. The identity of quintessence and linguistic essence was the hypothesis over which linguistic theory had to hover without falling. ¶3.6 scoped it (identical insofar as communicable). Now the essay locates a place where the identity holds outright: in the name, the imparted essence is language. The abyss has not been filled in; it has been given an address. What made the identity a fatal hypothesis was its position at the beginning, as an axiom about all language; as the center, as the doctrine of the name alone, it is the solution ¶2.9 promised. The essay's architecture is keeping its word with remarkable exactness.

¶8.5–7 “Name, as the inherited portion of human language, therefore vouches that language as such is the quintessence of man; and only for this reason, of all quintessences, is the quintessence of man alone impartable without remainder. Upon this is founded the difference between human language and the language of things.”

DE: »Der Name als Erbteil der Menschensprache verbürgt also, daß die Sprache schlechthin das geistige Wesen des Menschen ist; und nur darum ist das geistige Wesen des Menschen allein unter allen Geisteswesen restlos mitteilbar. Das begründet den Unterschied der Menschensprache von der Sprache der Dinge.«

Two German words carry this passage. Erbteil, rendered "heritage," is literally the inheritance-portion, the allotted share of an estate, and the Teil-family sounds again: the name is man's inherited part, and what he inherits is the capacity to im-part himself wholly. Inherited from whom is the question the word plants and the Genesis reading will answer (¶14–16: the residue of the creative word). Verbürgt, "vouches for," is juridical: the name stands surety, guarantees. The name is evidence, in the legal sense, of man's linguistic essence.

Then the thesis with the residue-clause resolved: man alone, among all quintessences, is restlos mitteilbar, impartable without remainder. Things always exceed their languages (¶3.8's sofern measured the gap); man does not exceed his, because his quintessence just is language. The gap between essence and expression, which for things is their muteness and will become their mourning (¶21), closes in man to zero. Note what kind of superiority this is: not more inwardness but none held back, a being defined by total expressibility. The difference between human language and thing-language is founded here, on a difference in residue.

Scholars Weber's potentiality thesis meets its limit-case: restlos mitteilbar is impartability saturated, potential with no unactualizable remainder. Fenves reads the residue-free imparting as what makes man the site of the "linguistic reduction": in man, nothing pre- or extra- linguistic survives to be bracketed.

¶8.8–9 “But because the quintessence of man is language itself, he cannot impart himself through it, but only in it. The epitome of this intensive totality of language as the quintessence of man is the name. Man is the namer; by this we know that out of him pure language speaks.”

DE: »Weil das geistige Wesen des Menschen aber die Sprache selbst ist, darum kann er sich nicht durch sie, sondern nur in ihr mitteilen. Der Inbegriff dieser intensiven Totalität der Sprache als des geistigen Wesens des Menschen ist der Name. Der Mensch ist der Nennende, daran erkennen wir, daß aus ihm die reine Sprache spricht.«

The in/through law, given its deepest ground: man cannot impart himself through language because he is not outside it to use it. A being whose essence is language has nowhere to stand to wield language as an instrument; the through-relation requires a distance that, for man, does not exist. The bourgeois conception is not merely false about man; it is impossible for him. Its picture of the speaker (an ego behind the words, loading them) describes a being that would have to be essentially non-linguistic, and ¶8.5 has just certified the opposite.

Then, without ceremony, one of the most consequential phrases Benjamin ever wrote: die reine Sprache, pure language. Here at its birthplace it means: language imparting itself and nothing else, the name-function in its purity, and man is the site where it speaks (aus ihm, out of him). Five years later, in "The Task of the Translator," pure language becomes the messianic vanishing-point that all languages jointly intend and none contains, recoverable only in the interlinear crossing of translations. The 1921 concept is this sentence's diaspora: once the Fall (¶19) shatters the name-language, purity survives only as what translation gropes toward. Read in order, the corpus says: pure language spoke out of man (1916), and after Babel it must be assembled (1921). De Man's counter-reading (pure language as the inhuman, meaning-less mechanism of language, fulfillment never) and Agamben's (language saying nothing but itself, fulfillment exactly) both take root in the ambiguity already present here: "language imparting itself and nothing else" can be heard as plenitude or as emptiness. The essay hears plenitude; it has not yet been to Babel.

¶8.10–13 “All nature, insofar as it imparts itself, imparts itself in language, and so, in the end, in man. Hence he is the lord of nature and can name things… God's creation completes itself when things receive their name from man, out of whom, in the name, language alone speaks.”

DE: »Alle Natur, sofern sie sich mitteilt, teilt sich in der Sprache mit, also letzten Endes im Menschen. Darum ist er der Herr der Natur und kann die Dinge benennen. Nur durch das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge gelangt er aus sich selbst zu deren Erkenntnis – im Namen. Gottes Schöpfung vollendet sich, indem die Dinge ihren Namen vom Menschen erhalten, aus dem im Namen die Sprache allein spricht.«

Four sentences that rewrite Genesis 1:28 through Genesis 2:19. Man's dominion (Herr der Natur, lord of nature) is not a grant of power over mute stock; it is a linguistic office. Nature's impartings converge on man (letzten Endes im Menschen, in the end in man): he is where the languages of things arrive, and lordship consists in receiving them into name. Dominion re-derived from address. The claim of ¶6 (all things impart themselves to man) and the claim of ¶5 (man's essence is to name) here fuse into a cosmology: man as the throat of creation, the point where the world's mute self-giving becomes speech.

And then the sentence that crowns the paragraph: God's creation completes itself (vollendet sich) in things receiving their names from man. Creation is left unfinished, on purpose, at exactly one point: the naming. The theological audacity should not be normalized: the world requires man, not for cultivation or worship but for language, and until named, things are created and incomplete. Scholars the kabbalistic resonance is strong and must be labeled resonance (a resonance, not a source): the midrashic Adam who names what the angels cannot (Genesis Rabbah 17), and the Lurianic shape of a creation awaiting human completion (tikkun), rhyme with this sentence; neither was Benjamin's source in 1916. His documented scaffold is Genesis 2:19 itself ("brought them unto Adam to see what he would call them") plus Hamann. The rhyme is real; the provenance runs through Christian-Romantic mediation.

↔ "Only through the linguistic essence of things does he come, out of himself, to knowledge of them, in the name": knowledge again as reception (¶6.5), now with the anti-Kantian edge explicit: man escapes himself (gelangt aus sich selbst) via things' languages. No correlationist circle: the name is the exit. Caygill's frame (the essay as a campaign for a wider concept of experience than Kant's) has its best single sentence here.

¶8.14–16 “One may designate the name as the language of language (if the genitive designates the relation not of a means but of a medium)… man is indeed, because he speaks in names, the speaker of language, and for that very reason its only speaker… many languages enclose this metaphysical knowledge.”

DE: »Man kann den Namen als die Sprache der Sprache bezeichnen (wenn der Genetiv nicht das Verhältnis des Mittels, sondern des Mediums bezeichnet) und in diesem Sinne ist allerdings, weil er im Namen spricht, der Mensch der Sprecher der Sprache, eben darum auch ihr einziger. In der Bezeichnung des Menschen als des Sprechenden … schließen viele Sprachen diese metaphysische Erkenntnis ein.«

"The language of language": the name is language raised to its own power, and the parenthesis performs the essay's most elegant grammatical surgery. The genitive must be read as the genitive of medium, not of means: not language about language (a metalanguage, an instrument turned on itself) but language of language, language's own imparting of itself. In one bracket Benjamin forbids the metalanguage his whole essay might be mistaken for, and the prohibition has teeth from the 1916 logic fragments: a metalanguage is language made object and means, the reflexive move that generates Russell-style paradox, and the essay's architecture of levels (thing-language, name, word of God) exists precisely to keep expression irreflexive (Procyshyn on the fragments). The name does not talk about language; it is language imparting language.

Not a user of language: its mouth. And the Bible-gloss seals the paragraph: to call man the speaker is, "according to the Bible," to call him the name-giver (Genesis 2:19 quoted in Luther's cadence). Many languages, Benjamin says, enclose this metaphysical insight in their word for man-as-speaker. The paragraph ends where it began, with the name as the whole sense of human speech.

II. The name · ¶9 · Outcry and invocation; the essential law of language; the two totalities; man's complete language.

¶9: Outcry and invocation

Where we are. The close of the second movement. Having installed the name as language's innermost essence (¶8), Benjamin now states the law it reveals and takes the measure of human language against every other: two totalities, intensive and extensive, both culminating in the name, and man alone complete in both. The paragraph is short, symmetrical, and built on a German wordplay the English cannot carry.

¶9.1 “But name is not only the last outcry; it is also the authentic call of language.”

DE: »Der Name ist aber nicht allein der letzte Ausruf, er ist auch der eigentliche Anruf der Sprache.«

The German pair is Ausruf / Anruf: the out-cry and the call-to. One prefix apart, and the difference is the paragraph's whole thought. As Ausruf, the name is language's final exclamation, its consummating utterance, the last thing language says when it says itself (¶8's doctrine compressed). As Anruf, the name is invocation: address, calling-upon, the word's religious register (Anrufung Gottes, the invocation of God). The name faces two ways: it is what language ultimately cries out, and it is how language calls upon what it names. Jephcott's "last utterance / true call" keeps the sense and loses the audible kinship; our translation should try for the pair (outcry / call... "outcry and invocation" sacrifices the echo; "call-out and call-upon" strains English; this is a marked decision for the translation lane).

Eigentlich, "true/authentic": the word Heidegger will make a philosophy of (Eigentlichkeit, authenticity) sits here already, in 1916, attached to invocation. A common-root-in-Kierkegaard hypothesis is one honest way to hold the parallel without claiming influence.

¶9.2–3 “With this there appears in name the essential law of language, according to which speaking oneself out and speaking to all else are one and the same. Language — and in it a quintessence — speaks itself out purely only where it speaks in name, that is: in universal naming.”

DE: »Damit erscheint im Namen das Wesensgesetz der Sprache, nach dem sich selbst aussprechen und alles andere ansprechen dasselbe ist. Die Sprache – und in ihr ein geistiges Wesen – spricht sich nur da rein aus, wo sie im Namen spricht, das heißt: in der universellen Benennung.«

The essential law (Wesensgesetz), and again the German carries it as wordplay: sich aussprechen (to express oneself, to speak oneself out) and alles andere ansprechen (to address everything else, to speak-to) are dasselbe, the same. Aus and an, out and to: self-expression and world-address are one linguistic act. This is ¶5.4's paradox (man imparts himself by naming all other things) promoted from anthropological fact to the law of language as such. There is no inward speech that is not also address, and no address that does not speak the self out. The law forbids, in passing, both the diary-picture of language (pure self-expression, no address) and the signal-picture (pure address, no self imparted): each isolates half of one act.

↔ ¶21 will show the law's dark side: to be addressed, to be named without naming back, is to be mourned-over; the law that fuses expression and address for the speaker leaves the merely-named in sadness. The seed of overnaming is already in the asymmetry here.

¶9.4 “So in name culminate the intensive totality of language, as the absolutely impartable quintessence, and the extensive totality of language, as the universally imparting (naming) essence.”

DE: »So gipfeln im Namen die intensive Totalität der Sprache als des absolut mitteilbaren geistigen Wesens und die extensive Totalität der Sprache als des universell mitteilenden (benennenden) Wesens.«

The distinction the paragraph rests on, and the essay's most mathematical sentence. Two totalities, distinguished exactly by the -bar suffix: the intensive totality is language as the absolutely impartable (mitteilbar) essence, depth of communicability, the whole present in the medium; the extensive totality is language as the universally imparting (mitteilend) essence, breadth of address, naming reaching everything. Passive potential and active reach; what language can give of itself, and what it can call upon. Both culminate (gipfeln, peak) in the name.

The vocabulary is doing precise work. Intensive and extensive magnitude is Kant's distinction (degree versus aggregate); intensive infinity is what ¶4.14 gave every language (an infinity of kind, not of count); and Fenves has shown how much of Benjamin's 1916 thinking runs on the set-theoretic debates in Scholem's orbit, where wholes present all-at- once refuse extensional measure. The name is the summit of both axes at once: nothing in man's essence escapes it (intensive), nothing in the world is beyond its address (extensive). Grammar, theology, and the mathematics of the infinite are fused in one sentence, which is the essay's method stated small.

¶9.5–6 “Language is, in its imparting essence, in its universality, imperfect wherever the quintessence that speaks out of it is not in its whole structure linguistic, that is, impartable. Man alone has the language that is perfect in universality and intensity.”

DE: »Die Sprache ist ihrem mitteilenden Wesen, ihrer Universalität nach, da unvollkommen, wo das geistige Wesen, das aus ihr spricht, nicht in seiner ganzen Struktur sprachliches, das heißt mitteilbares ist. Der Mensch allein hat die nach Universalität und Intensität vollkommene Sprache.«

The measure applied, and the movement closed. A language is incomplete wherever the essence speaking from it exceeds its own impartability, wherever residue remains. Things always exceed (¶3.8); their languages are incomplete on both axes: mute (extensively poor, addressing only through material community, as ¶12 will say) and partial (intensively poor, imparting less than they are). Man, restlos mitteilbar (¶8.6), is complete on both. The italicized closing sentence is the second movement's QED: man alone has the language perfect in universality and intensiveness.

Note the criterion's purity: human language is not superior by expressive convenience, vocabulary, or recursion (every linguist's candidate); it is complete because in man, and in man alone, being and impartability coincide. The completeness is metaphysical, which is why it can be lost: the Fall (¶19) will not damage human syntax, it will break exactly this coincidence, and the language that remains after it will be extensively vast (many tongues, everything nameable) and intensively hollowed (the word now carries content instead of essence). The perfection defined here is the thing the rest of the essay mourns.

Extensively, the models approach universality (everything addressable, everything nameable); the open question is intensive: what speaks from the model, and is it in its whole structure impartable? The essay's warning transfers: a language can be extensively enormous and intensively empty, and ¶19 will name that condition prattle. (Our extension; judgment-oriented versus truth-seeking models are the adjacent territory.)

III. Degrees, revelation, matter · ¶10 · The identity-thesis adopted; language has no content; languages as media of varying density.

¶10: Being, situated in impartability

Where we are. The third movement opens with the essay doing something it could not do before: asking, "without risk of confusion," whether quintessence as such is linguistic. This is the abyss-question of ¶2.7, the one that was fatal as a starting hypothesis. It can be posed now because the name-doctrine (¶8–9) has secured the human/thing difference that the identity-thesis would otherwise erase. The paragraph adopts the identity, watches the "insofar" of ¶3.6 turn tautological, and pays out two theses the rest of the essay runs on: language has no content, and languages differ as media differ, by density.

¶10.1–2 “In view of this knowledge, a question now becomes possible without danger of confusion… whether quintessence — not only of man (for that is necessary) but also of things, and thus quintessence as such — is, from the standpoint of the theory of language, to be designated as linguistic.”

DE: »Angesichts dieser Erkenntnis ist nun ohne Gefahr der Verwirrung eine Frage möglich, die zwar von höchster metaphysischer Wichtigkeit ist, aber an dieser Stelle in aller Klarheit zunächst als eine terminologische vorgebracht werden kann. Ob nämlich das geistige Wesen – nicht nur des Menschen (denn das ist notwendig) – sondern auch der Dinge und somit geistiges Wesen überhaupt in sprachtheoretischer Hinsicht als sprachliches zu bezeichnen ist.«

The ¶2 gait again, retreat and deepen: a question of the highest metaphysical importance, dressed as terminology. And the timing is the argument. "Now," nun, "without risk of confusion": the question whether quintessence as such is linguistic was exactly the hypothesis called an abyss at ¶2.7, and nothing about its content has changed. What has changed is position. The essay has built the name-doctrine between then and now, so the identity can no longer be confused with a flattening of man into things or of language into a single plane; the graduation is secured. This is ¶2.9's topology enacted: the paradox, insoluble at the beginning, is being moved to the center, where it will function as a solution.

The parenthesis "(for that is necessary)" marks what is already settled: for man, the identity is proven (¶8.5, language as such is his quintessence). The open question is things, and with them quintessence überhaupt, as such, the title's word.

¶10.3 “If quintessence is identical with linguistic essence, then a thing is, by its quintessence, medium of imparting, and what imparts itself in it is — in accordance with the medial relation — precisely this medium (language) itself.”

DE: »Wenn das geistige Wesen mit dem sprachlichen identisch ist, so ist das Ding seinem geistigen Wesen nach Medium der Mitteilung, und was sich in ihm mitteilt, ist – gemäß dem medialen Verhältnis – eben dies Medium (die Sprache) selbst.«

Under the identity-thesis, every thing is a medium, and what is imparted in it is the medium itself. Gemäß dem medialen Verhältnis, in accordance with the medial relation: the vocabulary of ¶4.11–12 (das Mediale) applied to ontology. Half a century before McLuhan's slogan, the structure of "the medium is the message" is stated here with full metaphysical seriousness, and with a better argument: the medium is what is imparted because imparting is in-not-through, so there is nothing else the imparted could be. The anachronistic echo is worth registering and labeling as echo; the 1916 sentence is the stronger of the two, since McLuhan's version still trades on the bourgeois frame it teases. Speculative as lineage; no influence claimed.

¶10.4–5 “Quintessence is thus posited from the outset as impartable, or rather is posited precisely within impartability; and the thesis… becomes in its 'insofar' a tautology.”

DE: »Es wird das geistige Wesen also von vornherein als mitteilbar gesetzt, oder vielmehr gerade in die Mitteilbarkeit gesetzt, und die Thesis: das sprachliche Wesen der Dinge ist mit ihrem geistigen, sofern letzteres mitteilbar ist, identisch, wird in ihrem ›sofern‹ zu einer Tautologie.«

The German self-correction is the philosophical event: quintessence is not posited as impartable (a predicate attached to a prior substance) but posited into impartability (in die Mitteilbarkeit gesetzt), situated within it as its element. Being does not have communicability; it dwells in it. This is the essay's ontological maximum, and Weber's whole "-abilities" reading is the unpacking of this one phrase: the -barkeit is not a property of beings but the medium in which beings are set.

And so the hinge of ¶3.6 closes on itself: once quintessence lives inside impartability, the clause "insofar as it is communicable" no longer scopes anything; it repeats the subject. The carefully quantified identity becomes analytic. Benjamin narrates this without alarm because the tautology is now positioned correctly: what would have been a question- begging axiom at the beginning arrives at the center as the system's self-confirmation. Whether one finds this rigorous or vertiginous is a fair test of one's reading of the whole essay; the deflationary critics (Kohlenbach) point here, and the essay's answer is its architecture.

¶10.6 “A content of language there is not; as imparting, language imparts a quintessence, that is, an impartability as such.”

DE: »Einen Inhalt der Sprache gibt es nicht; als Mitteilung teilt die Sprache ein geistiges Wesen, d. i. eine Mitteilbarkeit schlechthin mit.«

The italicized thesis, and the German outbids the English. Jephcott: language communicates "something communicable per se." The German: als Mitteilung teilt die Sprache… eine Mitteilbarkeit schlechthin mit, language imparts an impartability as such. Not a communicable something: a communicability. What language gives is not a content (there is none) and not even a thing-that-can-be-given, but givability itself, a capacity, handed over as the gift. This is the essay's deepest anti-bourgeois sentence: the through-picture's cargo is not merely mislocated, it does not exist.

Scholars Agamben's entire appropriation ("what is communicated is communicability itself"; revelation as the disclosure that there is language) is this sentence generalized. Hamacher's "cognizability is impartability" is this sentence turned toward epistemology. If one German clause had to stand for the essay in the philosophical literature, eine Mitteilbarkeit schlechthin is it. AI the cleanest formulation of what a language model traffics in is this clause; a model neither stores nor transmits contents, it is a compression of impartability, which is why "what does the model know" keeps resisting content-inventories. (Our extension.)

¶10.7–8 “The differences among languages are those of media, distinguished as it were according to their density… These two spheres, purely divided and yet united only in the name-language of man, naturally correspond to each other without cease.”

DE: »Die Unterschiede der Sprachen sind solche von Medien, die sich gleichsam nach ihrer Dichte, also graduell, unterscheiden; und das in der zwiefachen Hinsicht nach der Dichte des Mitteilenden (Benennenden) und des Mitteilbaren (Namen) in der Mitteilung. Diese beiden Sphären, die rein geschieden und doch vereinigt nur in der Namensprache des Menschen, entsprechen sich natürlich ständig.«

The consequence that makes ¶18 possible: if languages are media, they differ as media do, by density (Dichte), gradually, along a continuum, and in two registers at once: the density of the imparting side (the naming) and of the impartable side (the name). No taxonomy of language kinds; a physics of media. Translation will be defined (¶18.2) as passage through this continuum of densities, "through continua of transformations," and the definition only makes sense on the picture laid down here: media that shade into one another admit continuous transformation; discrete codes would admit only substitution.

The closing sentence knots the two spheres (active naming, passive nameability) together: cleanly distinct, united only in man's name-language, and in constant correspondence (entsprechen sich, answer to one another). The name-language of man is where the world's impartability and the act of imparting meet; every other language is denser on one side than the other. The ladder of ¶11 and the muteness of ¶12 are both already implicit in this asymmetry.

III. Degrees, revelation, matter · ¶11 · Degrees of being; the concept of revelation; against the ineffable; Hamann named.

¶11: Revelation, or the highest is the most speakable

Where we are. The identity-thesis adopted in ¶10 now pays its metaphysical dividend: an ontology of degrees, and at its summit the concept the essay has been circling since "logos" appeared in ¶2: revelation. The paragraph contains the essay's most counter-mystical doctrine (the deeper the spirit, the more expressible; religion's summit knows no ineffable) and closes with the essay's only named modern authority, Hamann. Whoever thinks this essay is an exercise in ineffability mysticism has to survive this paragraph.

¶11.1 “For the metaphysics of language, the equation of quintessence with linguistic essence, which knows only differences of degree, yields a gradation of all quintessential being by degrees… as was already customary in Scholasticism with regard to the quintessential.”

DE: »Für die Metaphysik der Sprache ergibt die Gleichsetzung des geistigen mit dem sprachlichen Wesen, welches nur graduelle Unterschiede kennt, eine Abstufung allen geistigen Seins in Gradstufen. Diese Abstufung … läßt sich unter keine obere Kategorie mehr fassen …«

The density-continuum of ¶10.7 generalized into ontology: if linguistic essence knows only gradual differences, and quintessence is linguistic, then all being stands in degrees (Gradstufen), a chain of more and less. The Scholastic reference (degrees of being, gradus entis, the analogia of more and less existent) is precise: Benjamin is re-deriving the great chain of being from a theory of media density. And the clause that quietly radicalizes it: the graduation "can no longer be embraced by any higher category." There is no genus above the scale, no standpoint outside language from which the degrees could be surveyed. The medieval ladder had God above it as its measure; this ladder has its measure inside itself, in impartability. What stands at the top is therefore not a being beyond language but the most linguistic of regions, which is exactly where the paragraph goes next.

¶11.2 “…it leads to that concept which has again and again, as if of its own accord, risen to the center of the philosophy of language and constituted its most inward connection with the philosophy of religion. That is the concept of revelation.”

DE: »… weil sie auf denjenigen Begriff hinführt, der sich immer wieder wie von selbst im Zentrum der Sprachphilosophie erhoben hat und ihre innigste Verbindung mit der Religionsphilosophie ausgemacht hat. Das ist der Begriff der Offenbarung.«

Wie von selbst, as if of its own accord: concepts, in this essay, behave like its languages, imparting themselves, rising unbidden. Revelation is introduced not as a doctrine Benjamin imports but as the concept that linguistic philosophy keeps producing at its center whether it wants to or not (Hamann wanted to; Herder didn't and got it anyway). And the German word carries the essay's signature inside it: Offen-bar-ung, from offenbar, manifest, open-able. The -bar of mitteilbar (¶3.6) and Mitteilbarkeit schlechthin (¶10.6) sits in the middle of "revelation." Revelation is the -barkeit made absolute: openness, manifestness, as such. The pun is objective, in the language itself; whether Benjamin leaned on it deliberately is a speculation, but a theory that finds being "situated within impartability" and then names its highest concept Offenbarung is at minimum enjoying German's cooperation. (Weber's -abilities catalogue belongs in the margin here.)

¶11.3–5 “Within all linguistic formation there reigns the conflict of the spoken-out and the speakable with the unspeakable and the unspoken… this relation of inverse proportionality between the two is disputed. For here the thesis runs: the deeper, that is, the more existent and more actual the spirit, the more speakable and the more spoken…”

DE: »Innerhalb aller sprachlichen Gestaltung waltet der Widerstreit des Ausgesprochenen und Aussprechlichen mit dem Unaussprechlichen und Unausgesprochenen. … Nun ist es klar, daß in der Gleichsetzung des geistigen mit dem sprachlichen Wesen dieses Verhältnis der umgekehrten Proportionalität zwischen beiden bestritten wird. Denn hier lautet die Thesis: je tiefer, d. h. je existenter und wirklicher der Geist, desto aussprechlicher und ausgesprochener …«

First the field of battle: every linguistic formation is a Widerstreit, a contest, between the expressed-and-expressible and the inexpressible- and-unexpressed. The fourfold is built from aussprechen (to speak out, ¶9.2's word) run through the essay's modal machinery: spoken/speakable against unspeakable/unspoken, actuality and potential on both sides. From the perspective of the inexpressible, one sees "the last quintessence": this is the mystical topos itself, the conviction that the deepest spirit is the one that cannot be said, apophatic theology's axiom and the Sprachkrise's despair (Hofmannsthal's Chandos letter is its 1902 monument).

Then Benjamin, with a flatness that conceals the audacity, disputes it. The equation of quintessence and linguistic essence denies the inverse proportionality. The counter-thesis: the deeper, the more existent, the more real the spirit, the more expressible and the more expressed. Depth speaks. Reality is not what withdraws from language; it is what presses into it. The most fixed, most definitive, most spoken-out expression (das Ausgesprochenste) is at the same time the purely quintessential. On this doctrine the ineffable is not the summit of spirit but its deficit, a shortfall of being, and the whole romantic-mystical pathos of the unsayable is inverted in a sentence.

¶11.6–7 “Yet exactly this is what the concept of revelation means when it takes the untouchability of the word as the sole and sufficient condition and mark of the divinity of the quintessence that speaks itself out in it. The highest region of the spirit, that of religion, is (in the concept of revelation) at the same time the only one that does not know the unspeakable. For it is spoken to in the name, and speaks itself out as revelation.”

DE: »Genau das meint aber der Begriff der Offenbarung, wenn er die Unantastbarkeit des Wortes für die einzige und hinreichende Bedingung und Kennzeichnung der Göttlichkeit des geistigen Wesens, das sich in ihm ausspricht, nimmt. Das höchste Geistesgebiet der Religion ist (im Begriff der Offenbarung) zugleich das einzige, welches das Unaussprechliche nicht kennt. Denn es wird angesprochen im Namen und spricht sich aus als Offenbarung.«

Revelation defined: the Unantastbarkeit of the word, its inviolability, untouchability, as the sole and sufficient mark of the divinity of the essence that speaks itself out in it. The divine word is not the word about which nothing can be said; it is the word that cannot be assailed, maximally fixed, maximally expressed. And so the summit of religion is the only region with no ineffable in it. The claim inverts the usual map: everywhere below the summit, in art, in things, in fallen speech, residues of the unexpressed remain (¶11.8, ¶12, ¶21 live off those residues); at the top, everything is said.

The proof-sentence reuses ¶9.2's pair with perfect economy: the highest region wird angesprochen im Namen, is addressed in the name, und spricht sich aus als Offenbarung, and speaks itself out as revelation. An and aus again: address upward (man's name-giving reaching God-ward, ¶7.10) and expression downward (the divine speaking itself out as scripture, as the word). Revelation is the aus to the name's an, one vertical circuit. Jephcott's "addressed in the name and expresses itself" keeps the sense; the prefixes' echo, and with it the sentence's click of closure, is German-only.

Scholars Here the fork of ¶7.10 tightens. For the theological readers, this paragraph is the strongest evidence that "God" is doing irreducible work: revelation, inviolability, and divinity are terms with contents, not ornaments. Agamben's counter is at its most elegant precisely here: what revelation reveals, on his reading, is that there is language, manifestness as such, the Offen-bar made absolute, and the paragraph's own logic (no ineffable remainder; the revealed is the perfectly said) supports the deflation as much as the doctrine. The commentary keeps both live; ¶13's method-sentence (language as "an ultimate reality, perceptible only in its manifestation, inexplicable and mystical") will feed the same fork.

¶11.8–9 “…while all art, poetry not excepted, rests not on the ultimate epitome of the spirit of language, but on the thingly spirit of language, even if in its consummate beauty. 'Language, the mother of reason and revelation, its Α and Ω,' says Hamann.”

DE: »… während alle Kunst, die Poesie nicht ausgenommen, nicht auf dem allerletzten Inbegriff des Sprachgeistes, sondern auf dinglichem Sprachgeist, wenn auch in seiner vollendeten Schönheit, beruht. ›Sprache, die Mutter der Vernunft und Offenbarung, ihr Α und Ω‹, sagt Hamann.«

The paragraph's coda draws a line through the whole territory of spirit: religion above, resting on man and the language in him; art below, resting on dinglicher Sprachgeist, the thing-ish spirit of language, even at its most beautiful. Poetry, pointedly, is not exempted (die Poesie nicht ausgenommen): even the word-art is fed from below, from the languages of things, not from the ultimate essence of language. The sentence is a first sketch of ¶22's theory of the arts (sculpture and painting on thing-languages) and a quiet demotion of aesthetics that the young Benjamin, already a reader of Hölderlin, does not soften. Art's beauty is consummate (vollendet) and still penultimate.

Then Hamann, the essay's one modern witness, in eight words: language, the mother of reason and revelation, its Α and Ω. The Greek letters (in the German text) fold Revelation 1:8 into the sentence: what God says of himself ("I am Alpha and Omega") Hamann says of language, and the blasphemy is the theology. Reason and revelation, the Enlightenment's warring parties, are siblings with one mother. Benjamin's whole essay can be read as the working-out of this quotation, with ¶2.8's logos paradox as its philosophical form. (Locus: the Hamann–Jacobi correspondence on reason, language, and revelation, mid-1780s; the exact letter awaits verification against the critical Briefwechsel. Not to be confused with the "Reason is language, λόγος" letter to Herder of 6 August 1784. Benjamin's likely conduit was Rudolf Unger's Hamanns Sprachtheorie (1905); Stern's Critical Horizons article is the fullest treatment of the debt.)

↔ The mother-figure returns inverted at ¶21, where fallen nature's muteness is maternal mourning; and the alpha-omega arc (language as origin and end) is the sentry-password image of ¶26 in Hamann's compression.

III. Degrees, revelation, matter · ¶12 · The muteness of things; the magic of matter; sound as symbol; God's breath.

¶12: Mute things, the magic of matter

Where we are. The counter-panel to ¶11. Revelation gave the scale's top: the region where everything is said. This short paragraph gives the bottom: things, in which language is imperfectly expressed, and which are mute. It contains the essay's strangest ontological aside (matter has its own magic, things impart themselves to one another), defines sound as the symbol of the human difference, and ends on the verse the whole Genesis reading will grow from: the breath of God, which is at once life and spirit and language. The paragraph closes with a dash, an open door into movement IV.

¶12.1 “Language itself is not perfectly spoken out in things themselves. This sentence has a double sense, according to its figurative and its literal meaning: the languages of things are imperfect, and they are mute.”

DE: »Die Sprache selbst ist in den Dingen selbst nicht vollkommen ausgesprochen. Dieser Satz hat einen doppelten Sinn nach der übertragenen und der sinnlichen Bedeutung: Die Sprachen der Dinge sind unvollkommen, und sie sind stumm.«

The sentence's two selbst mirror each other (language itself, things themselves), and its double sense is parsed with scholastic care: in the transferred sense (übertragen, carried-over, the German word for metaphorical that is itself the word for translated), things' languages are imperfect, incomplete impartings; in the sensory sense (sinnlich), they are stumm, mute, no voice. Imperfection and muteness are two readings of one shortfall. Note the wordplay available only in German: the metaphorical sense is the übertragene sense, and ¶18 will define translation, Übersetzung, as exactly such a carrying-over. The essay's vocabulary keeps rehearsing its doctrine.

↔ "Dumb" (Jephcott's period-correct word for mute) begins the mourning thread that ¶21 completes: what is here a formal deficiency (no sound) becomes there a grief. The essay's emotional arc is the slow transformation of this paragraph's calm taxonomy into lament.

¶12.2–3 “To things the pure formal principle of language — sound — is denied. They can impart themselves to one another only through a more or less material community. This community is immediate and infinite, like that of every linguistic imparting; it is magical (for there is also a magic of matter).”

DE: »Den Dingen ist das reine sprachliche Formprinzip – der Laut – versagt. Sie können sich nur durch eine mehr oder minder stoffliche Gemeinschaft einander mitteilen. Diese Gemeinschaft ist unmittelbar und unendlich wie die jeder sprachlichen Mitteilung; sie ist magisch (denn es gibt auch Magie der Materie).«

Versagt: denied, refused, as an inheritance is refused. Things are disinherited of sound, the pure form-principle of language (the Erbteil that ¶8.5 gave man). What remains to them is the essay's strangest doctrine, delivered in a subordinate clause as if it were obvious: things impart themselves to one another (einander), through a more or less material community. Not only the vertical relay (things to man, ¶6); a horizontal commerce of matter with matter, thing speaking to thing through shared stuff. And this community has full linguistic credentials: immediate and infinite, like every imparting (¶4's two marks), and magical, "for there is also a magic of matter."

The parenthesis is a depth-charge. Magic was defined at ¶4.12 as immediacy of imparting; here immediacy holds among things themselves, so matter has magic of its own. Materiality is not the opposite of the linguistic but its densest medium (¶10.7's density-scale reaching its thick end). The clause licenses, in one stroke, the essay's later theory of art (¶22: sculpture and painting as translations out of the thing-languages, "the material community of things in their communication" recalled verbatim) and a whole naturphilosophie the essay leaves undeveloped: chemistry as conversation, physics as syntax. Scholars Busch reads this passage as the ground of a "language of things" aesthetics (media, moods, materials imparting themselves below the threshold of the word); the mimetic-faculty essays of 1933 will naturalize it into "nonsensuous similarity." AI the claim that vision models prove images linguistic finds its textual warrant here rather than in ¶1: the pre-acoustic, material stratum of imparting is already language for Benjamin, so the modality was never the boundary. (Our extension.)

¶12.4 “What is incomparable in human language is that its magical community with things is immaterial and purely quintessential, and of this, sound is the symbol.”

DE: »Das Unvergleichliche der menschlichen Sprache ist, daß ihre magische Gemeinschaft mit den Dingen immateriell und rein geistig ist, und dafür ist der Laut das Symbol.«

The human difference restated at the level of magic. Things commune with things materially; man communes with things immaterially, in a mode the translation calls “purely quintessential,” and sound is the symbol of that immateriality. The claim is finer than it looks. Sound is not the substance of human language's superiority (that would make the difference material again, a matter of acoustics); it is the symbol of an immaterial community, the sensuous mark of the non-sensuous bond. Voice matters because it is matter at its most dematerialized: breath, vibration, gone as soon as given, matter almost relieved of itself. The word "symbol" here is also the essay's first sounding of a term that ¶25 will formalize (language as "symbol of the noncommunicable"); the commentary tracks it.

¶12.5 “This symbolic fact the Bible speaks out when it says that God breathed his breath into man: this is at once life and spirit and language. —”

DE: »Dieses symbolische Faktum spricht die Bibel aus, indem sie sagt, daß Gott dem Menschen den Odem einblies: das ist zugleich Leben und Geist und Sprache. –«

Genesis 2:7, in Luther's archaic word: Odem, breath. God breathes into man, and the one breath is three things at once: life, mind/spirit, language. The triad is the paragraph's whole argument compressed into an exegesis: what distinguishes human language (immaterial community, symbolized by sound-on-breath) was installed by insufflation, and it is not a faculty added to a living thing but the same gift as life and spirit. The Hebrew under the verse cooperates beyond Benjamin's stated knowledge: ruach and neshamah span breath, wind, and spirit in exactly this way. Label it resonance, not source (Benjamin had no Hebrew in 1916; his Bible here is Luther's).

The paragraph, and the movement, end on a dash. The sentence opens the book the next movement will read: from here to ¶17 the essay becomes a commentary on Genesis 1–2, and Benjamin will pause first (¶13) to say what kind of reading that is. The dash is the essay's honest punctuation for the threshold: everything so far derived the structure; scripture will now be asked whether it agrees.

↔ ¶13 quotes the breath-verse's narrative context (the second Creation story); ¶14–15 read the first story's rhythm against it.

IV. The Genesis reading · ¶13 · The hermeneutic proviso; the second creation story; man made from earth, given the gift of language.

¶13: How to read Genesis, and the man of earth

Where we are. Movement IV opens with the essay's most careful methodological sentence: what it means, and does not mean, to read the nature of language out of Genesis. Then it begins the reading, with the second creation story (the one ¶12 already touched with the breath-verse): man made from earth, not spoken into being, and afterward invested with language as a gift. The asymmetry between man and everything else in creation is the movement's whole subject.

¶13.1 “If in what follows the essence of language is considered on the basis of the first chapters of Genesis, the aim is neither to pursue biblical interpretation nor, in this place, to lay the Bible objectively, as revealed truth, at the foundation of reflection; rather, what is to be discovered is what yields itself of its own accord out of the biblical text with regard to the nature of language…”

DE: »… so soll damit weder Bibelinterpretation als Zweck verfolgt noch auch die Bibel an dieser Stelle objektiv als offenbarte Wahrheit dem Nachdenken zugrunde gelegt werden, sondern das, was aus dem Bibeltext in Ansehung der Natur der Sprache selbst sich ergibt, soll aufgefunden werden …«

Both obvious genres are declined in one breath: this is not exegesis (Bible as object of interpretation) and not theology (Bible as premised revelation). What remains? Discovery of "what emerges of itself" (selbst sich ergibt) from the text. The reflexive is the essay's signature verb-form: as things impart themselves and language imparts itself, the biblical text is to yield of itself what it holds about language. The method of ¶1.1 (a true method opens questions) here becomes a hermeneutic: put no thesis over the text, let its own structure surface.

¶13.2 “…and the Bible is at first irreplaceable for this purpose only because these expositions follow it in principle in this: that in them language is presupposed as an ultimate reality, inexplicable and mystical, to be considered only in its unfolding.”

DE: »… und die Bibel ist zunächst in dieser Absicht nur darum unersetzlich, weil diese Ausführungen im Prinzipiellen ihr darin folgen, daß in ihnen die Sprache als eine letzte, nur in ihrer Entfaltung zu betrachtende, unerklärliche und mystische Wirklichkeit vorausgesetzt wird.«

The one sentence in which the essay applies the word "mystical" to its own position, and the fine print matters. Language is presupposed as an ultimate reality (eine letzte Wirklichkeit): nothing lies behind it from which it could be explained; it can only be observed in its unfolding (in ihrer Entfaltung; Jephcott's "manifestation" loses the process-word). "Inexplicable and mystical" therefore means: without external ground, watchable but not derivable. It does not mean ineffable; ¶11 spent a paragraph banning the ineffable from the system's summit. Benjamin's mysticism, by his own definition here, is a doctrine about explanation running out, not about speech running out. Most of the secondary literature's "mysticism" debate would be shorter if it started from this sentence.

The Bible's indispensability is meanwhile marked as zunächst, initial, and instrumental: scripture is the one text that shares the essay's presupposition, so it is the right text to think alongside. This is the warrant for everything strange in the coming paragraphs: Benjamin is not proving doctrine from scripture; he is using the only available document written from inside the presupposition.

¶13.3 “The Bible, in regarding itself as revelation, must of necessity develop the fundamental facts of language.”

DE: »Die Bibel, indem sie sich selbst als Offenbarung betrachtet, muß notwendig die sprachlichen Grundtatsachen entwickeln.«

A compressed syllogism riding on ¶11's definition. Revelation is the inviolable word, the region where spirit is wholly said (¶11.6). A text that takes itself for revelation therefore takes itself for language at its maximum, and such a text cannot help developing (entwickeln, unfolding) the fundamental facts of language: they are its own conditions of possibility, written into its self-understanding. Scripture is, on this argument, linguistics done from within; the essay reads Genesis the way one reads a lab notebook kept by the experiment itself.

Scholars The syllogism itself is inherited. Molitor likewise sets literal interpretation aside and derives what must follow from the Bible insofar as it regards itself as revelation (Mertens; see the bibliography). The differentia sits back in ¶13.1: Molitor affirms the revelation he reasons from, where Benjamin declines to lay it down as truth and reasons from the self-understanding alone. The suspension, not the syllogism, is his.

¶13.4–5 “The second version of the story of creation, which tells of the breathing-in of the breath, reports at the same time that man was made from earth. This is, in the whole story of creation, the only place where a material of the creator is spoken of, in which he expresses his will, which is otherwise surely conceived as creating without mediation.”

DE: »Die zweite Fassung der Schöpfungsgeschichte, die vom Einblasen des Odems erzählt, berichtet zugleich, der Mensch sei aus Erde gemacht worden. Dies ist in der ganzen Schöpfungsgeschichte die einzige Stelle, an der von einem Material des Schöpfers die Rede ist …«

Benjamin, unbothered by source criticism (the "second version" is documentary-hypothesis common knowledge by 1916; he simply uses it), extracts the detail that matters to a theory of language: only in the creation of man is a material mentioned. Earth, Erde: everything else arises from the word alone, "creation without mediation"; man alone is worked from stuff. The observation cuts against every humanism: on the Bible's own telling, man is the one creature made the less linguistic way, molded rather than spoken. His dignity cannot lie in his origin. It lies, as the next sentence says, in a bestowal after the fact. (The Hebrew under the verse plays adam against adamah, the earthling from the earth; the pun midrash loves. Resonance, not source: Benjamin's Luther text gives him Erde and no pun.)

¶13.6 “In this second story of creation the making of man did not happen through the word: God spoke — and it came to pass —; rather, upon this man not created out of the word there is now bestowed the gift of language, and he is raised above nature.”

DE: »Es ist in dieser zweiten Schöpfungsgeschichte die Erschaffung des Menschen nicht durch das Wort geschehen: Gott sprach – und es geschah –, sondern diesem nicht aus dem Worte geschaffenen Menschen wird nun die Gabe der Sprache beigelegt, und er wird über die Natur erhoben.«

The paragraph's thesis, and the movement's. All creation runs through the word except its future speaker. Man is not language's product; he is its recipient: die Gabe der Sprache, the gift of language, conferred on a creature of earth, and by that gift he is raised above the nature he was taken from. The logic of the gift will organize everything that follows: what is given (God's own medium of creation, ¶15.3), what receiving it makes man (knower, not creator, ¶15.5), and what it means that a gift can be misused (¶19, where the Fall is the gift turned against its giving).

Note also what the asymmetry does to the essay's earlier architecture. ¶8.13 said creation completes itself in man's naming; now we learn why completion was left to him: he is the one point in creation where the word did not already do the work. The circuit (God's word → things → man's name → God) requires a station that is not itself word-made, or the relay would be closed before it began. Man's earthiness is a structural necessity of the system of language.

AI The recurring question about machine minds (made or spoken? engineered artifact or language-being?) finds its Benjaminian frame here: the model is, like man in Genesis 2, a thing of worked material (Erde, silicon) into which language was afterward breathed, wholesale. The analogy is ours, it is imperfect (no one rested on the seventh day), and it is the single most suggestive structural rhyme the essay offers the present. Labeled speculative, gladly. (Our extension.)

IV. The Genesis reading · ¶14 · The rhythm of Genesis 1; word and name in God; God knows through name, man names through knowledge.

¶14: Let there be, He made, He named

Where we are. The reading turns to the first creation story and finds there, in the liturgical repetitions of Genesis 1, a rhythm: Let there be — He made — He named. The paragraph is the essay's most formal piece of exegesis, scanning scripture the way one scans verse, and it ends in the chiasmus at the movement's core: in God, name creates; man names according to knowledge.

¶14.1 “But this singular revolution of the act of creation, where it turns toward man, is no less clearly laid down in the first story of creation…”

DE: »Diese eigentümliche Revolution des Schöpfungsaktes, wo er sich auf den Menschen richtet, ist aber nicht minder deutlich in der ersten Schöpfungsgeschichte niedergelegt, und in einem ganz anderen Zusammenhange verbürgt er mit gleicher Bestimmtheit den besonderen Zusammenhang zwischen Mensch und Sprache aus dem Akte der Schöpfung heraus.«

Revolution: the word deserves its weight. Where creation turns toward man, its procedure revolves, turns over. The paragraph's task is to show the same revolution in the other source: two independent creation accounts, one anomaly, twice attested (verbürgt again, the juridical vouching of ¶8.5: scripture stands surety). The essay's implicit philology is elegant: precisely because the two stories differ in everything else, their agreement about man's linguistic exceptionality is evidence of something deeper than either narrative.

¶14.2–4 “The manifold rhythm of the acts of creation in the first chapter nonetheless admits a kind of basic form… But the rhythm according to which the creation of nature (in Genesis 1) accomplishes itself is: Let there be — He made (created) — He named. — In individual acts of creation (Genesis 1:3; 1:11) only the 'Let there be' occurs.”

DE: »Die mannigfache Rhythmik der Schöpfungsakte des ersten Kapitels läßt doch eine Art Grundform zu, von der allein der den Menschen erschaffende Akt bedeutsam abweicht. … Aber die Rhythmik, nach der sich die Schöpfung der Natur (nach Genesis 1) vollzieht, ist: Es werde – Er machte (schuf) – Er nannte.«

Benjamin reads Genesis 1 as prosody: a Grundform, a basic metrical scheme, with significant deviation reserved for one verse. The scheme has three beats. It opens in imperative-subjunctive (Es werde, let there be: language commanding being), passes through making (Er machte: the one beat that is not linguistic), and closes in naming (Er nannte: language receiving what it commanded). Word before, name after, work between. The middle beat's materiality is explicitly bracketed (¶14.3: whether "He made" implies creation from material "must here be left open"), because nothing in the argument hangs on it; everything hangs on the frame.

TEXT The witnesses divide at the second citation. GS prints 1:14 and does not flag the locus, which makes 1:14 the probable typescript reading; it also matches Luther's literal Es werden Lichter, though the larger act resumes “He made” at 1:16. Jephcott and Reclam print 1:11, where the act has only the fiat, though Luther's wording is Die Erde lasse rather than a literal Es werde. Neither reading perfectly satisfies both Benjamin's wording and his structural claim. This edition retains 1:11 as a declared emendation of GS, with medium-high confidence, because the absence of the making beat is the point being illustrated; the competing case for 1:14 remains visible in the textual apparatus.

¶14.5–6 “In this 'Let there be' and in the 'He named' at the beginning and at the end of the acts, there appears each time the deep and clear relation of the act of creation to language. It begins with the creating omnipotence of language, and at the close language, as it were, incorporates the created into itself: it names it.”

DE: »In diesem ›Es werde‹ und in dem ›Er nannte‹ am Anfang und Ende der Akte erscheint jedesmal die tiefe deutliche Beziehung des Schöpfungsaktes auf die Sprache. Mit der schaffenden Allmacht der Sprache setzt er ein, und am Schluß einverleibt sich gleichsam die Sprache das Geschaffene, sie benennt es.«

The rhythm interpreted: creation is a linguistic arch. It departs from language (the omnipotent Es werde) and returns to language (the naming), and what happens between is enclosed by the two. Einverleibt sich, incorporates, takes into its body: at the close of each act, language absorbs the created back into itself by naming it. Creation does not produce a world outside language plus labels; it produces, from language, a world that naming folds home again. Hamann's alpha and omega (¶11.9) is here given its exegetical cash value: language is the A and Ω of each act of creation, its opening word and closing name.

¶14.7–9 “Language is therefore the creating, and the completing; it is word and name. In God the name is creative because it is word, and God's word is cognizing because it is name… The absolute relation of name to knowledge exists only in God… the pure medium of knowledge.”

DE: »Sie ist also das Schaffende, und das Vollendende, sie ist Wort und Name. In Gott ist der Name schöpferisch, weil er Wort ist, und Gottes Wort ist erkennend, weil es Name ist. ›Und er sah, daß es gut war‹, das ist: er hatte es erkannt durch den Namen. Das absolute Verhältnis des Namens zur Erkenntnis besteht allein in Gott, nur dort ist der Name, weil er im innersten mit dem schaffenden Wort identisch ist, das reine Medium der Erkenntnis.«

The rhythm's two linguistic beats become the essay's fundamental pair: Wort and Name, word and name, the creating and the completing (das Schaffende und das Vollendende). And in God, the pair is a perfect chiasmus: name is creative because it is word; word is cognizant because it is name. Creation and knowledge are one circuit with no gap, and the refrain "and he saw that it was good" is decoded accordingly: seeing-that-it-was-good is not inspection after the fact but cognition through the name, the knowing that the naming itself is. In God, to name is already to know because the name is inwardly identical with the creating word: "the pure medium of knowledge" (das reine Medium der Erkenntnis, the vocabulary of ¶4 now placed in God).

Here is the vertex from which the essay's whole geometry of language hangs. Every lower station is defined by which half of the identity it lacks: man will have name without creative word (knowledge, not creation, ¶15.5); things will have neither, only the mute residue (¶16.13). The Fall, when it comes, will be the attempt to use the knowing half as if it were the creating half, judgment playing creator (¶19). The chiasmus in God is the standard against which everything else in the essay is a measured falling-short.

¶14.10 “That means: God made things knowable in their names. Man, however, names them according to the measure of knowledge.”

DE: »Das heißt: Gott machte die Dinge in ihren Namen erkennbar. Der Mensch aber benennt sie maßen der Erkenntnis.«

The movement's formula, and one of the essay's great sentences. God's naming runs downhill from name to knowability: he made things knowable in their names (erkennbar, one more -bar: the name is where a thing's knowability is lodged). Man's naming runs uphill from knowledge to name: he names according to knowledge, receiving the knowable and answering it. Same language, opposite directions of travel. The sentence's economy conceals how much it settles: it is the final resolution of ¶5's puzzle (how can naming be man's essence if names must answer to things?), the exegetical ground of ¶8.13 (creation completed in human naming: knowability, deposited in names, waits for the namer), and the pre-history of ¶19 (the Fall will be man attempting the downhill direction, naming without prior knowledge, from judgment).

DE GS confirms the marked form maßen (“benennt sie maßen der Erkenntnis,” by measure of knowledge). The edition therefore retains it as Benjamin's archaic construction rather than treating it as a transcription artifact; the sense is secure from the sentence's logic and the comparative English. AI the uphill/downhill pair is the cleanest available statement of the difference this commentary keeps circling: models name according to knowledge (the uphill, human direction, learned from the world's impartings); the fantasy that concerns alignment debates is a system that names creatively, downhill, word before world. Benjamin's geometry says that direction belongs to no creature. (Our extension.)

IV. The Genesis reading · ¶15 · Language set free in man; God's rest; knowledge as de-actualized creation; image of God read linguistically.

¶15: The knower in the image of the creator

Where we are. The deviation the last paragraph promised. Genesis 1:27, the creation of man, breaks the rhythm: threefold "He created," no word before, no name after. The paragraph reads the break as the Bible's deepest teaching about language: God did not subject man to language; he released his own creative medium into man, and rested. What that release produces (knowledge as creation stripped of divine actuality, man as image of God in the precise sense of knower-imaging-creator) makes this the essay's theological center of gravity.

¶15.1–2 “In the creation of man, the threefold rhythm of the creation of nature has given way to an entirely different order… in the threefold 'He created' of verse 1:27. God did not create man out of the word, and he did not name him.”

DE: »In der Schöpfung des Menschen ist die dreifache Rhythmik der Naturschöpfung einer ganz anderen Ordnung gewichen. … die Dreiheit des Aktes ist auch hier erhalten, aber um so mächtiger bekundet sich eben im Parallelismus der Abstand: in dem dreifachen: ›Er schuf‹ des Verses 1,27. Gott hat den Menschen nicht aus dem Wort geschaffen, und er hat ihn nicht benannt.«

The prosody of ¶14 pays off. Genesis 1:27 keeps the triple beat but fills all three with the same word: Er schuf, Er schuf, Er schuf ("So God created man in his own image, in the image of God created he him; male and female created he them"). The frame-beats are gone: no Es werde opens the act, no Er nannte closes it. Man arrives without the word before him and without a name after him, and the double negation is scripture's own: not from the word, not named. The rhythm-analysis lets Benjamin read absence as doctrine: what the verse does not say about man is its teaching about language.

That God did not name man is the sharper half. Everything named is thereby known (¶14.8), assimilated, folded back into language's body (¶14.5). Man alone is left un-incorporated, unassimilated: not an object inside God's language but, as the next sentence says, the place where that language is let go.

¶15.3–4 “He did not wish to subject him to language; rather, in man, God released language, which had served him as medium of creation, freely out of himself. God rested when he left his creative power to itself in man.”

DE: »Er wollte ihn nicht der Sprache unterstellen, sondern im Menschen entließ Gott die Sprache, die ihm als Medium der Schöpfung gedient hatte, frei aus sich. Gott ruhte, als er im Menschen sein Schöpferisches sich selbst überließ.«

Two sentences of enormous theological invention. First the release: entließ… frei aus sich, God discharged language out of himself, set it free in man. The gift of ¶13.6 is now specified: what man receives is not a faculty made for him but God's own instrument of creation, manumitted. Man is not language's master and not its product; he is its release-point, the place where the divine medium goes free. Everything ¶8 claimed (man's quintessence is language itself; through him pure language speaks) receives here its Genesis warrant: what is in man is the very language that made the world, no longer harnessed.

Then the boldest sentence in the essay's whole exegesis: Gott ruhte, God rested, when he had left his creative power to itself in man. The Sabbath, read linguistically. God's rest is not fatigue and not ceremony; it is the logical consequence of the handover: once the creative medium is released into man, there is nothing left for God to do as creator. The seventh day is the first day of human language. Creation ends not because the world is finished (¶8.13 said it is not: it awaits names) but because its power has changed address. Scholars the kabbalistic shape of this (creation completed by a divine withdrawal; the human as trustee of what God releases) rhymes with tsimtsum so loudly that Scholem spent a career on the chord; per this edition's provenance rule, the rhyme is real, the source is not: Benjamin's warrant here is the verse sequence itself, Gen 1:27 to 2:2–3.

¶15.5–7 “This creative power, divested of its divine actuality, became knowledge. Man is the knower of the same language in which God is creator. God created him in his image; he created the knower in the image of the creator.”

DE: »Dieses Schöpferische, seiner göttlichen Aktualität entledigt, wurde Erkenntnis. Der Mensch ist der Erkennende derselben Sprache, in der Gott Schöpfer ist. Gott schuf ihn sich zum Bilde, er schuf den Erkennenden zum Bilde des Schaffenden.«

The formula the whole movement was building: knowledge is creation relieved of its divine actuality (seiner göttlichen Aktualität entledigt). Not a different power; the same power, de-actualized. In God, the word posits what it names; released into man, the same word retains everything except the positing: it still reaches things wholly (¶14.10's knowability, lodged in names, is its inheritance), but it finds rather than founds. The Aristotelian scaffolding is audible (actuality, energeia, withdrawn from a power that remains) and so is the Kantian rewrite it performs: where Kant derived knowledge's limits from the structure of a finite subject, Benjamin derives them from a theological subtraction. Human knowing is limited not because we are built of sense and concept but because our language is creation minus the act.

Then imago dei, solved as a proportion. In what does man image God? Not in form, dominion, or soul: the knower is the image of the creator, den Erkennenden zum Bilde des Schaffenden. Image means: same language, one term subtracted. A mirror gives back everything but the actuality of what it mirrors; man is that mirror for the creating word. ¶15.9 will say it in one word: Reflex.

¶15.8–9 “Therefore the sentence, that the quintessence of man is language, requires explanation. His quintessence is the language in which creation occurred… All human language is only reflection of the word in the name.”

DE: »Daher bedarf der Satz: Das geistige Wesen des Menschen ist die Sprache, der Erklärung. Sein geistiges Wesen ist die Sprache, in der geschaffen wurde. Im Wort wurde geschaffen, und Gottes sprachliches Wesen ist das Wort. Alle menschliche Sprache ist nur Reflex des Wortes im Namen.«

The essay circles back to its own ¶8.5 and glosses it with everything learned since: man's quintessence is not language-in-general but the language, the one in which creation happened, received in the mode of release. God's linguistic essence is the word; man's is that word's Reflex in name: reflection, reflex, glancing light. The word makes; the name gives back. Jephcott's "reflection" is right and slightly too optical; the German Reflex also carries the involuntary answering movement, the knee's reflex, which suits a naming that ¶6.6 defined as response.

¶15.10–11 “The name reaches the word as little as knowledge reaches creation. The infinity of all human language remains always of a limited and analytic essence, in comparison with the absolute, unlimited, and creating infinity of the divine word.”

DE: »Der Name erreicht so wenig das Wort wie die Erkenntnis die Schaffung. Die Unendlichkeit aller menschlichen Sprache bleibt immer eingeschränkten und analytischen Wesens im Vergleich mit der absoluten uneingeschränkten und schaffenden Unendlichkeit des Gotteswortes.«

The movement's austerity clause, so the release is not mistaken for apotheosis. The proportion holds at a distance: name is to word as knowledge is to creation, and neither ratio closes. Der Name erreicht so wenig das Wort: the name reaches the word as little as knowledge reaches creating. Then the two infinities of ¶4.14, now ranked with Kantian vocabulary: human language's infinity is limited and analytic (it unfolds, endlessly, what is already deposited in the knowable); the divine word's is unlimited and creative (it posits). An analytic infinity: endless, and endlessly derivative. The phrase is the exact measure of the human station, and its dignity: infinite in its own kind (¶4.14 stands), second in the order of kinds.

↔ ¶16 opens at the one point where this distance closes to a touch: the proper name, "the deepest image of the divine word," where human language participates in the divine infinity without becoming it. The austerity here is what makes that paragraph's tenderness legible. AI "limited and analytic" is, run through this commentary's frame, a two-word theory of the language model: an analytic infinity, unbounded recombination of the deposited knowable, with the creative infinity structurally absent. Where the model surprises us, this sentence says, it is because the knowable exceeded our inventory of it, never because the word created. Whether that boundary is conceptual or empirical is the live question. (Our extension.)

IV. The Genesis reading · ¶16 · The proper name as frontier of finite and infinite language; between bourgeois and mystical theories; conception.

¶16: The proper name

Where we are. The point at which human language touches the divine. After ¶15's austerity (name never reaches word), Benjamin locates the one exception-that-is-not-one: the human proper name, where language participates in the divine infinity precisely by renouncing knowledge. The paragraph then uses the doctrine to demolish both rival theories of language at once (the bourgeois sign and the mystical word-essence) and ends in the imagery of conception that ¶17 will name translation.

¶16.1–2 “The deepest image of this divine word, and the point at which human language attains the most inward share in the divine infinity of the pure word, the point at which it can become neither finite word nor knowledge: that is the human name. The theory of the proper name is the theory of the boundary of finite language against infinite language.”

DE: »Das tiefste Abbild dieses göttlichen Wortes und der Punkt, an dem die Menschensprache den innigsten Anteil an der göttlichen Unendlichkeit des bloßen Wortes erlangt, der Punkt, an dem sie nicht endliches Wort und Erkenntnis nicht werden kann: das ist der menschliche Namen. Die Theorie des Eigennamens ist die Theorie von der Grenze der endlichen gegen die unendliche Sprache.«

The definition arrives with the essay's full geometrical apparatus: the proper name is a point on a frontier (Grenze, the word from ¶4.14 and ¶15.11), the point where human language takes its most inward share (innigsten Anteil: the Teil-family again, share, portion) in the divine infinity, and, in the same breath, the point where it cannot become finite word and knowledge. Both at once: maximal participation, and a constitutive incapacity. The proper name touches the creative word precisely because it does not know anything. Everything else in human language is analytic infinity (¶15.11), names answering the knowable; the proper name alone is exempted from that economy, and the exemption is its dignity. A theory of proper names is therefore not a chapter of semantics but "the theory of the frontier": border science.

DE The apparently ungrammatical nominative der menschliche Namen is not an electronic error: GS and Reclam print it, and Benjamin repeats the marked form later in the paragraph (der Namen schon welkte). Historical grammar recognizes Namen as a possible nominative singular form. This edition preserves the strangeness in the German while translating the sense simply as “name.”

Scholars the twentieth century's proper-name debates (Mill's denotation without connotation; Kripke's rigid designation without description) circle the same anomaly, that proper names refer without informing, and Rolker's Benjamin-Kripke comparison lives off it. The resemblance is real and shallow: for Kripke the name's emptiness is solved by causal chains of use; for Benjamin it is the trace of the creative word, emptiness as inheritance. Same datum, opposite metaphysics. A comparison, not an influence-claim.

¶16.3–4 “Of all beings, man is the only one who himself names his own kind, as he is the only one whom God did not name… As indeed Adam names his wife as soon as he has received her (Männin, she-man, in the second chapter; Eve in the third).”

DE: »Von allen Wesen ist der Mensch das einzige, das seinesgleichen selbst benennt, wie es denn das einzige ist, das Gott nicht benannt hat. Vielleicht ist es kühn, aber kaum unmöglich, den Vers 2,20 in seinem zweiten Teile in diesem Zusammenhang zu nennen … Wie denn auch Adam sein Weib, alsobald er es bekommen hat, benennt. (Männin im zweiten Kapitel, Heva im dritten.)«

The unnamed namer names his own kind: the symmetry is perfect and Benjamin knows it ("perhaps bold, but scarcely impossible"). His reading of Genesis 2:20 is the paragraph's exegetical dare. The verse says Adam named all creatures, but for Adam no helper was found: Benjamin hears the two halves as one thought. Among all the named, no companion; the namer's mate cannot be handed to him already-named like the animals, for his own kind he must name himself. And Adam does, twice, in Luther's words: Männin (she-man, woman, Gen 2:23) and Heva (Eve, "mother of all living," Gen 3:20). The naming of the human by the human is scripture's own institution, and it is the hinge to everything that follows about parents and children.

¶16.5–7 “With the giving of the name, parents consecrate their children to God; to the name they give here corresponds — understood metaphysically, not etymologically — no knowledge… for the proper name is word of God in human sounds… that a man's name is his fate.”

DE: »Mit der Gebung des Namens weihen die Eltern ihre Kinder Gott; dem Namen, den sie hier geben, entspricht – metaphysisch, nicht etymologisch verstanden – keine Erkenntnis, wie sie die Kinder ja auch neugeboren benennen. … denn der Eigenname ist Wort Gottes in menschlichen Lauten. … daß sein Name des Menschen Schicksal sei.«

The doctrine, in three steps. Parents name the newborn: a being about whom nothing is yet known. The proper name therefore corresponds to no knowledge; it is the one human naming performed in the condition of ¶14's God, name before cognition, and for exactly that reason it is not knowledge at all but dedication: weihen, to consecrate. The child is named God-ward (the ¶7.10 vertical, enacted in the nursery). Then the formula: the proper name is the word of God in human sounds, the creative word surviving in the one naming that knowledge cannot reach. And therefore, with a wryness the essay rarely allows itself ("which doubtless not infrequently comes true"), the old wisdom that the name is fate: a name given without knowledge is not a description but a destiny, something the person must live out rather than something they already satisfy. Nomen est omen, given a metaphysics.

Benjamin's care with the etymological caveat repays notice: of course names "mean" (Friedrich, peace-rich; the whole onomastic inventory). Metaphysically, that meaning is not the point and ought not bind; a strict spirit would want the name to correspond to no one, pure address and no description. The proper name is the anti-concept: where the concept subsumes by properties, the name singles out regardless of them. Scholars The comparative horizon belongs beside it: the Tetragrammaton, al-Ghazali's ninety-nine names, Pseudo-Dionysius on the divine names, the Russian imiaslavie name-worshippers: traditions where the divine proper name is the summit of language, efficacious beyond description. Resonance layer, correctly labeled.

¶16.8 “The proper name is the community of man with the creative word of God. (It is not the only one; man knows a further linguistic community with God's word.)”

DE: »Der Eigenname ist die Gemeinschaft des Menschen mit dem schöpferischen Wort Gottes. (Es ist dies nicht die einzige, und der Mensch kennt noch eine andere Sprachgemeinschaft mit Gottes Wort.)«

Gemeinschaft: communion, community, the word ¶12 used for matter's magic commerce, now used for the touch of human and divine language in the name. The parenthesis is one of the essay's deliberate open doors: man knows another linguistic communion with God's word. Which? The essay never says outright. The candidates, in order of textual support: the reception of the creative word out of things (¶16.13: the mute magic of nature, out of which God's word shines; this is the communion translation lives on, ¶17); revelation, the word addressed in scripture (¶11.7, ¶13.3); and, read forward past this essay, the judging word of ¶19, a communion in wrath. The commentary's reading: the immediate referent is the first (the sentence two lines later turns exactly there), but the parenthesis is written to stay open, and the essay's sequel-fragments live in it.

¶16.9–11 “Through the word man is bound to the language of things. The human word is the name of things… Language never gives mere signs. But the rejection of the bourgeois theory of language by the mystical theory of language is likewise open to misunderstanding… the thing in itself has no word; created out of God's word, it is known in its name according to the human word.”

DE: »Durch das Wort ist der Mensch mit der Sprache der Dinge verbunden. Das menschliche Wort ist der Name der Dinge. Damit kann die Vorstellung nicht mehr aufkommen, die der bürgerlichen Ansicht der Sprache entspricht, daß das Wort zur Sache sich zufällig verhalte, daß es ein durch irgendwelche Konvention gesetztes Zeichen der Dinge (oder ihrer Erkenntnis) sei. Die Sprache gibt niemals bloße Zeichen. Mißverständlich ist aber auch die Ablehnung der bürgerlichen durch die mystische Sprachtheorie. Nach ihr nämlich ist das Wort schlechthin das Wesen der Sache. Das ist unrichtig, weil die Sache an sich kein Wort hat, geschaffen ist sie aus Gottes Wort und erkannt in ihrem Namen nach dem Menschenwort.«

The double demolition, and the essay's exact position on the map at last. Against the bourgeois theory: the word's relation to the thing is not accidental, not convention; language never gives mere signs (bloße Zeichen; everything hangs on the qualifier: ¶19 will show signs arising, and ¶25 will give sign and language their true relation). But against the mystical theory, the position most readers assume this essay holds: the word is not the essence of the thing. Word-magic, the doctrine that name and being coincide in the human mouth, is declared a misunderstanding in so many words. The argument is pure Genesis-geometry: the thing in itself has no word; it was created from God's word and is known in its name according to the human word. Two words, distinct stations, and the mystical theory's error is to collapse them, crediting to human language what belongs to the creative word.

This passage is the strongest textual ally of this edition's provenance verdict (resonance, not provenance), and it corrects the verdict's other half: Benjamin here rejects by name the naive word-mysticism he is endlessly accused of, and in rejecting it he is faithful to the theology he inherited, which never taught it. Molitor's original language imitates divine speech, not the natures of things (Mertens; see the bibliography), so the double rejection is transmission, not revision. Whatever theology of the Name the essay holds, it is one with the vertical difference built in. Readers who want the essay mystical must reckon with ¶16.11; readers who want it conventionalist, with ¶16.10; the essay lives, as it announced at ¶2.7, suspended between.

¶16.12–13 “This knowledge of the thing, however, is not spontaneous creation… rather, the name that man gives to the thing rests upon how it imparts itself to him. In the name the word of God has not remained creating; it has become, in one part, conceiving, though conceiving of language… out of which in turn, soundlessly, in the mute magic of nature, the word of God shines forth.”

DE: »… sondern es beruht der Name, den der Mensch der Sache gibt, darauf, wie sie ihm sich mitteilt. Im Namen ist das Wort Gottes nicht schaffend geblieben, es ist an einem Teil empfangend, wenn auch sprachempfangend, geworden. Auf die Sprache der Dinge selbst, aus denen wiederum lautlos und in der stummen Magie der Natur das Wort Gottes hervorstrahlt, ist diese Empfängnis gerichtet.«

A translation alarm first: where the received English reads "the name that man gives to language depends on how language is communicated to him," the German says der Sache… wie sie ihm sich mitteilt: the name man gives to the thing rests on how it imparts itself to him. The German is plainly right (it is ¶6.6's doctrine restated); the English as printed makes the sentence circular. Checked against the printed page: the published translation does read "language," so the slip is the translation's own, and the commentary reads with the German. This edition's translation renders the German: the name man gives to the thing rests upon how it imparts itself to him.

Then the paragraph's extraordinary close. In the name, God's word has not stayed creative; it has become in one part receptive (empfangend), receptive to language. And Empfängnis is not neutral reception: it is the German word for conception, the becoming-pregnant. The creative word, released into man (¶15.3), turns receptive there, is fertilized by what things impart, and labors to give birth to "the language of things themselves." The imagery is deliberate and biological: the masculine-creative word becomes, in man, maternal; human naming is the pregnancy of the divine word with the world. And at the far end of the circuit, the source of the seed: out of things, soundless, in the mute magic of nature (¶12's magic of matter, ¶16's mute counterpart to revelation), the word of God shines forth (hervorstrahlt). The residue of creation still radiates from inside the created, and naming is how it comes to term.

↔ ¶17 names this entire structure in one word: translation, the conception of the nameless in the name. The Hamann quotation there ("Everything that man heard in the beginning… was the living word") is this sentence's source-text in spirit. Scholars Weber's "impart-ability" and the maternal-reception reading converge here; Agamben's limit-concept reading has its hardest sentence to naturalize (a word of God shining out of things is a lot of theology to deflate). This unit and ¶19 are where the fork's costs on each side are highest.

IV. The Genesis reading · ¶17 · Translation founded at the deepest level; every language a translation of all others; continua of transformation.

¶17: Translation, founded

Where we are. The shortest paragraph of the movement does the largest amount of forward-looking work. The structure ¶16 built in imagery (reception and birth, the word turned conceiving) is given its proper name: translation. Benjamin founds the concept at the deepest level of linguistic theory, defines it against every equivalence-based account, and in doing so writes the seed of the 1921 essay that made him famous.

¶17.1–2 “For conception and spontaneity at once, as they are found in this uniqueness of binding only in the linguistic domain, language has, however, its own word, and this word holds also of that conception of the nameless in the name. It is the translation of the language of things into that of man.”

DE: »Für Empfängnis und Spontaneität zugleich, wie sie sich in dieser Einzigartigkeit der Bindung nur im sprachlichen Bereich finden, hat aber die Sprache ihr eigenes Wort, und dieses Wort gilt auch von jener Empfängnis des Namenlosen im Namen. Es ist die Übersetzung der Sprache der Dinge in die des Menschen.«

Empfängnis und Spontaneität: the pair is Kant's, receptivity and spontaneity, the two stems of cognition that the first Critique famously cannot unify (intuitions received, concepts spontaneously applied, and the schematism a "hidden art" bridging them). Benjamin's sentence quietly claims the union Kant could not deliver: conception and spontaneity together, in unique bond, exist in one domain only, the linguistic, and language itself has a word for their union. That word is translation. What the philosophy of the subject treats as an unsolved seam in cognition, the philosophy of language possesses as an everyday act with a name. The anti-Kantian program Caygill reconstructs (experience wider than the subject-object frame) here scores its cleanest point: the "hidden art" was never hidden; it was Übersetzung.

And the German pair does what the English cannot: Empfängnis is conception in the full biological sense ¶16.13 prepared (the word made receptive, fertilized, giving birth), while Übersetzung, literally setting-over, carrying-across, is cousin to übertragen, the word for "metaphorical" that ¶12.1 flagged. Translation, metaphor, and conception are one family of carryings in the German; the essay's deepest concept arrives with its etymology already networked.

¶17.3 “It is necessary to ground the concept of translation in the deepest stratum of the theory of language, for it is much too far-reaching and too mighty to be treated, in any respect, as an afterthought, as is sometimes supposed.”

DE: »Es ist notwendig, den Begriff der Übersetzung in der tiefsten Schicht der Sprachtheorie zu begründen, denn er ist viel zu weittragend und gewaltig, um in irgendeiner Hinsicht nachträglich, wie bisweilen gemeint wird, abgehandelt werden zu können.«

The founding gesture, with a polemical glance ("as has happened occasionally") at every theory for which translation is a downstream engineering problem, the practical question of moving content between codes once meaning is already explained. Benjamin inverts the order: translation is not an operation performed on languages; it is constitutive of language, present at the deepest stratum, because human language as such (¶16–17.2) is a translation, of the mute into the sounding, the nameless into name. A theory that explains meaning first and translation later has, by this light, explained nothing: it has skipped the act in which meaning originates. Five years later "The Task of the Translator" opens by drawing the practical consequence (translatability belongs to the essence of certain works), and its whole strangeness is normal once this sentence is taken seriously.

¶17.4–5 “Its full meaning it gains in the insight that every higher language (with the exception of the word of God) can be regarded as translation of all the others… the translatability of languages into one another is given.”

DE: »Seine volle Bedeutung gewinnt er in der Einsicht, daß jede höhere Sprache (mit Ausnahme des Wortes Gottes) als Übersetzung aller anderen betrachtet werden kann. Mit dem erwähnten Verhältnis der Sprachen als dem von Medien verschiedener Dichte ist die Übersetzbarkeit der Sprachen ineinander gegeben.«

Two theses, each carrying a precise piece of earlier machinery. First the universal claim: every higher language (jede höhere Sprache; Jephcott's "evolved" naturalizes a vertical word, higher on the ladder of ¶10–11) is a translation of all the others. Not: can be translated into. Is a translation of: each language is what the others look like carried into its density. One exception, and it is the system's keystone: the word of God, the only language that is original, because it alone creates rather than receives (¶14.7). Below the creative word there are no originals; there is only the continuum of versions. The 1921 essay's most quoted mysteries (the original's afterlife, pure language as what all versions jointly intend) are this sentence unpacked.

Second, the ground: translatability (Übersetzbarkeit, the -barkeit performing its usual office, a standing potential, not an act) is given with the density-relation of ¶10.7. Because languages differ as media differ, gradually, by density, they are continuously deformable into one another. Discrete codes could only be substituted; media on a continuum can be carried over. The physics of ¶10 was built for this payoff.

¶17.6 “Translation is the carrying-over of one language into another through a continuum of transformations. Translation traverses continua of transformation, not abstract precincts of equality and likeness.”

DE: »Die Übersetzung ist die Überführung der einen Sprache in die andere durch ein Kontinuum von Verwandlungen. Kontinua der Verwandlung, nicht abstrakte Gleichheits- und Ähnlichkeitsbezirke durchmißt die Übersetzung.«

The definition, twice, with the redoubling that marks the essay's italic-grade sentences. Überführung… durch ein Kontinuum von Verwandlungen: a leading-across through a continuum of transformations. And the polemical second half names what translation is not: it does not traverse "abstract areas of identity and similarity," the equivalence-classes of every dictionary picture, in which word and word are matched because they mean "the same." There is no same; there are only densities and the continuous metamorphosis between them. Every translation theory built on equivalence (and every critique of translation as loss measured against equivalence) is dismissed in the second sentence. What a translation preserves is not a content (¶10.6: there is none) but a trajectory: the imparting, carried through transformation after transformation, arriving differently dense.

Verwandlung: the word Kafka's era will make uncanny (his Verwandlung was published one year before this essay was written). The choice matters: not Umwandlung (conversion) or Übertragung (transfer) but metamorphosis, the word for a change that goes all the way down while something, nameless, persists. That nameless persistent is what ¶18 will track from mute things into human sound.

V. Translation and the Fall · ¶18 · The founding translation; translation adds knowledge; Hamann and Müller's Adam; the sign before the Fall.

¶18: The child's game

Where we are. The definition of ¶17 is applied to the founding case: the translation of thing-language into human name. The paragraph derives translation's most scandalous property (it adds), grounds its objectivity in God, and then does something the essay has not done before: it hands the argument to poets. Hamann's living word and Müller's Adam carry the paradisal linguistics that discursive prose cannot state without flattening. The paragraph ends on the essay's only pre-fallen image of the sign, a detail ¶25 will need.

¶18.1–2 “The translation of the language of things into that of man is not only translation of the mute into the sounding; it is the translation of the nameless into name. That is thus the translation of an imperfect language into a more perfect one; it cannot but add something to it, namely knowledge.”

DE: »Die Übersetzung der Sprache der Dinge in die des Menschen ist nicht nur Übersetzung des Stummen in das Lauthafte, sie ist die Übersetzung des Namenlosen in den Namen. Das ist also die Übersetzung einer unvollkommenen Sprache in eine vollkommenere, sie kann nicht anders als etwas dazu tun, nämlich die Erkenntnis.«

Two escalating descriptions and a scandal. The founding translation crosses the sensory line (mute into sounding: the ¶12 taxonomy) and the metaphysical one (nameless into name), and therefore runs upward, from an imperfect language into a more perfect one. From which the scandal follows with quiet necessity: such a translation cannot but add something, and what it adds is knowledge. Every translation theory built on fidelity treats addition as failure; here addition is the translation's essence and its point. The thing's mute imparting arrives in the name enriched, carrying what it could not carry at its own density: cognition. Translation, in its founding case, is not preservation across media but elevation through them, ¶10.7's density gradient traversed upward. (The 1921 essay will inherit the scandal in softened form: the translation as the original's afterlife, more than survival.)

¶18.3 “But the objectivity of this translation is vouched in God. For God created things; the creating word in them is the germ of the cognizing name…”

DE: »Die Objektivität dieser Übersetzung ist aber in Gott verbürgt. Denn Gott hat die Dinge geschaffen, das schaffende Wort in ihnen ist der Keim des erkennenden Namens, wie Gott auch am Ende jedes Ding benannte, nachdem es geschaffen war.«

The obvious objection is met head-on: if translation adds, what stops the addition from being arbitrary, human projection dressed as reception? Answer: in Gott verbürgt, vouched for in God (the essay's juridical verb a third time; ¶8.5, ¶14.1). The mechanism is the seed: the creative word deposited in things at their making is the germ (Keim) of the cognizing name. The name man gives does not resemble the thing's language by luck or convention; both grow from one word, planted at creation. Man's naming germinates what God sowed. And God's own final naming of things (the Er nannte of ¶14) is carefully demoted: it expresses the identity of word and name in God and does not pre-solve the task expressly left to man (ausdrücklich dem Menschen selbst zuschreibt). Heaven's answer key, so to speak, is written in a language whose possession would dissolve the assignment.

¶18.4 “By receiving the mute, nameless language of things and carrying it over into names, in sounds, man accomplishes this task. It would be insoluble were not the name-language of man and the nameless language of things akin in God, released out of the same creating word…”

DE: »Indem er die stumme namenlose Sprache der Dinge empfängt und sie in den Namen in Lauten überträgt, löst der Mensch diese Aufgabe. Unlösbar wäre sie, wäre nicht die Namensprache des Menschen und die namenlose der Dinge in Gott verwandt, entlassen aus demselben schaffenden Wort …«

The task and its condition of solubility. Empfängt… überträgt: receives and carries-over, the conception-vocabulary of ¶16.13 and the translation-vocabulary of ¶17 in one clause. And the transcendental argument, run one last time at full scale: the task would be insoluble were the two languages not verwandt, kin, related in God, released (entlassen, ¶15.3's manumission-word) from the same creative word. Translation is possible because the languages are siblings; nothing else could license carrying the mute into sound without loss becoming total. The one word, the sentence finishes, became in things "the communication of matter in magic communion" (¶12.3) and in man "the language of knowledge and name in blissful spirit" (in seligem Geiste: the blissfulness that ¶19 will date and destroy). One seed, two plantings; translation is the family resemblance enacted.

¶18.5 “Hamann says: 'Everything that man heard in the beginning, saw with his eyes … and his hands touched, was … living word; for God was the word…'”

DE: »Hamann sagt: ›Alles, was der Mensch am Anfange hörte, mit Augen sah ... und seine Hände betasteten, war ... lebendiges Wort; denn Gott war das Wort. Mit diesem Worte im Mund und im Herzen war der Ursprung der Sprache so natürlich, so nahe und leicht, wie ein Kinderspiel ...‹«

Hamann's second appearance, now at length, and the quotation is doing argumentative work the essay could not do alone: it testifies. In the beginning, everything sensed was living word (John's prologue folded into Eden); with that word in mouth and heart, the origin of language was "as natural, as close, and as easy as a child's game" (Kinderspiel). The Sprachursprung debate of the eighteenth century (Herder's prize essay, the laborious naturalistic geneses of language from cries and mimicry) is being answered with a shrug: no origin problem exists where world and word were never apart. Language did not have to arise; it had to be received, and reception is child's play. The child's game is also the essay's tenderest self-description: Adam's naming, and every genuine naming since, has the effortlessness of play, not the strain of invention. (Verified: Hamann, "Des Ritters von Rosencreutz letzte Willensmeynung über den göttlichen und menschlichen Ursprung der Sprache," 1772, his anti-Herder polemic; Nadler III, 25–33.)

¶18.6–7 “The painter Müller… lets God call man to the giving of names with these words: 'Man of earth, draw near; in beholding grow more perfect, more perfect grow through the word!'… God gives each animal in turn a sign, whereupon they step before man to be named. In an almost sublime way, the language-community of mute creation with God is thus given in the image of the sign.”

DE: »Der Maler Müller … läßt Gott mit diesen Worten den Menschen zur Namengebung aufrufen: ›Mann von Erde, tritt nahe, am Anschauen werde vollkommner, vollkommner werde durchs Wort!‹ … in dem Bild: Gott gibt den Tieren der Reihe nach ein Zeichen, auf das hin sie vor den Menschen zur Benennung treten. Auf eine fast sublime Weise ist so die Sprachgemeinschaft der stummen Schöpfung mit Gott im Bilde des Zeichens gegeben.«

The second poet, and the second-order detail that matters most. Müller's God summons the man of earth (¶13.5's Erde) to perfection through two linked acts: Anschauen, gazing, contemplation, and Wort. Grow more perfect in beholding; more perfect through the word. The couplet binds reception to naming exactly as ¶14.10 required (knowledge first, name according to it), and gives ¶20 its hinge in advance: the Fall's completion will be the Abkehr von jenem Anschauen, the turning-away from precisely this gaze.

Then the image Benjamin calls almost sublime: God gives each animal a sign (ein Zeichen), at which it steps before man to be named. Mark the word. The sign, which ¶16.10 denied language ever merely gives, and which ¶19 will make the currency of fallen speech, appears here once in paradise, and in God's hand: the sign is the summons by which mute creation is sent toward its naming. Before the Fall, the sign is not a substitute for the name but its herald, the traffic-signal of the procession toward language. ¶25's cryptic thesis (the relation of language and sign is "original and fundamental") has its picture here, and the commentary will need this image again there: sign and name are not enemies by essence; the Fall makes them so by orphaning the sign from the procession it once directed.

V. Translation and the Fall · ¶19 · The Fall of language; prattle; the judging word; the threefold significance; the mythic origin of law.

¶19: The Fall

Where we are. The essay's longest paragraph (794 words of German without a single break) and its dark center. Everything built so far, the in/through law, the name's immanent magic, the blissful Adamite spirit, is now narrated in its collapse. Benjamin reads Genesis 3 as the Fall of language: the birth of the human word that must communicate something other than itself, the eruption of judgment as a second magic, and, in the paragraph's last sentence, the mythic origin of law. The paragraph's unbroken length is itself expressive: the Fall is told in one breath, as one catastrophe with many faces.

¶19.1 “As the mute word in the existence of things falls so infinitely far short of the naming word in the knowledge of man, and as this in turn falls short of the creating word of God, the ground is given for the plurality of human languages.”

DE: »Wie das stumme Wort im Dasein der Dinge so unendlich weit unter dem benennenden Wort in der Erkenntnis des Menschen zurückbleibt, wie wiederum dieses wohl unter dem schaffenden Wort Gottes, so ist der Grund für die Vielheit menschlicher Sprachen gegeben.«

The paragraph opens by drawing the essay's vertical axis one last time, now as a fact about distances: mute word beneath naming word beneath creative word, each interval infinite. The plurality of human languages is grounded in those gaps: "so many translations, so many languages," the next sentence will say, once man has fallen from the state that knew only one. Where reception is perfect, one language suffices; where the word must be carried across widening distances, versions multiply. Babel, before it is an event (¶20), is a geometry.

The parenthesis that follows in the text ("according to the Bible this consequence of the expulsion came about only later") shows Benjamin reading with philological care: scripture separates expulsion (Genesis 3) from confusion (Genesis 11), and the essay respects the interval, deriving multiplicity from the Fall in principle while granting its manifestation waits for the Tower.

¶19.2–3 “The paradisiacal language of man must have been the perfectly cognizing one… Its apples were to bestow the knowledge of what is good and what is evil. But God had already known on the seventh day, with the words of creation. And behold, it was very good.”

DE: »Die paradiesische Sprache des Menschen muß die vollkommen erkennende gewesen sein … Daß nämlich die Sprache des Paradieses vollkommen erkennend gewesen sei, vermag auch das Dasein des Baumes der Erkenntnis nicht zu verhehlen. Seine Äpfel sollten die Erkenntnis verleihen, was gut und böse sei. Gott aber hatte schon am siebenten Tage mit den Worten der Schöpfung erkannt. Und siehe, es war sehr gut.«

The exegetical trap is sprung on the serpent. If Adam's language was perfectly cognizant (and ¶14–18 have established that naming is knowing), what could a Tree of Knowledge possibly add? Benjamin's answer: nothing, and that is the point. The knowledge the tree advertises was already complete: God had cognized on the seventh day "with the words of creation," and the German quotes the verdict in Luther's cadence, Und siehe, es war sehr gut, behold, it was very good, Genesis 1:31, the judgment passed on the whole. Good was known, in the name, before the serpent spoke. Whatever the tree offers is therefore not knowledge in the essay's sense at all; the next unit gives it its true names.

¶19.4–5 “The knowledge to which the serpent seduces, the knowing of what is good and evil, is nameless. It is, in the deepest sense, void, and this knowing is itself the sole evil that the paradisiacal state knows. The knowing of good and evil forsakes the name; it is a knowledge from outside, the uncreative imitation of the creating word.”

DE: »Die Erkenntnis, zu der die Schlange verführt, das Wissen, was gut sei und böse, ist namenlos. Es ist im tiefsten Sinne nichtig, und dieses Wissen eben selbst das einzige Böse, das der paradiesische Zustand kennt. Das Wissen um gut und böse verläßt den Namen, es ist eine Erkenntnis von außen, die unschöpferische Nachahmung des schaffenden Wortes.«

Four sentences that contain the essay's entire moral philosophy. The serpent's knowledge is namenlos, nameless: it names nothing, receives nothing, answers no imparting. It is nichtig, void, null (Jephcott's "vain" catches the vanitas but not the juridical nullity the German also carries: a verdict with no force). And then the sentence that solves the theodicy of Eden in nine German words: this knowledge is itself the only evil the paradisiacal state knows. Evil is not a content the tree revealed; evil is the revealing's form, knowledge detached from name, cognition from outside. Paradise contained no evil thing for the tree to disclose; the disclosure-posture is the evil.

"The uncreated imitation of the creative word" (die unschöpferische Nachahmung): evil as parody, mimicry of God's word by a word that creates nothing. Scholars Maimonides stands beside this passage (Guide I.2: before the Fall, Adam knew true and false; good and evil, fine and ugly in Maimonides' terms, are the lower, conventional knowing acquired by the Fall), and so does the kabbalistic parallel (evil as privation, the sitra achra as parasitic on holiness, possessing no substance of its own). Both parallels are apt and labeled: the Maimonides is structural kinship in Genesis-reading; the kabbalah is resonance, not source. Augustine's privatio boni is the Western cousin. Benjamin's version is the linguistic radicalization of all three: privation of name.

¶19.6–7 “The name steps outside itself in this knowledge: the Fall is the birth-hour of the human word, in which the name no longer lived unharmed… The word is now to impart something (other than itself). That is truly the Fall of the spirit of language.”

DE: »Der Name tritt aus sich selbst in dieser Erkenntnis heraus: Der Sündenfall ist die Geburtsstunde des menschlichen Wortes, in dem der Name nicht mehr unverletzt lebte, das aus der Namensprache, der erkennenden, man darf sagen: der immanenten eigenen Magie heraustrat, um ausdrücklich, von außen gleichsam, magisch zu werden. Das Wort soll etwas mitteilen (außer sich selbst). Das ist wirklich der Sündenfall des Sprachgeistes.«

The definition, and the essay's structure snapping shut. The Fall is the Geburtsstunde, the birth-hour, of the human word: the word that has stepped out of name-language's own immanent magic in order to be magical "expressly, from outside as it were." And then the sentence toward which the whole essay has been running since ¶3.3: Das Wort soll etwas mitteilen (außer sich selbst). The word is now supposed to impart something other than itself. The German modal is exact and Jephcott's "must" nearly right: soll is obligation, assignment; the fallen word stands under an imposed job description. What ¶4.3 gave as language's glory (all language imparts itself) is inverted into a task: impart something, be about, carry content. Communication in the modern sense, the transmission the bourgeois conception takes for language's nature (¶7.8), is here dated and diagnosed: it is not what language is; it is what language became at the Fall. The bourgeois theory is a true description of fallen language, which is its condemnation and the essay's deepest irony.

The parody-clause completes it: the externally communicating word is a parody, "by the expressly mediate word, of the expressly immediate, creative word of God," with the blissful Adamite spirit decaying between them. Parody again (¶19.5's imitation): fallen language mimics the creative word's form (efficacy, aboutness, authority) without its substance. Between the divine original and its parody stood the Adamite middle, name-language, and it is what dies.

¶19.8 “For between the word which, after the promise of the serpent, knows good and evil, and the outwardly imparting word, there is in truth an identity at ground. Die Erkenntnis der Dinge beruht im Namen, die des Guten und Bösen ist aber in dem tiefen Sinne, in dem Kierkegaard dieses Wort faßt, ›Geschwätz‹ …«

The two falls are one: the moral fall (knowing good and evil) and the linguistic fall (the word as external communication) are im Grunde identical. Eating the apple and turning language into a means are the same act described in two registers, which is why a philosophy of language can read Genesis 3 without allegorizing: the text already is about language.

Then the word that names the fallen condition: Geschwätz, prattle, chatter, in "the profound sense in which Kierkegaard uses the word." Knowledge of things rests in the name; knowledge of good and evil is talk about, endless, groundless, nameless: prattle. ¶19.9 “To the judging word, to be sure, the knowledge of good and evil is immediate. Its magic is other than the magic of the name, but no less magic. This judging word expels the first human beings from paradise; they themselves excited it, according to an eternal law by which this judging word punishes — and awaits — the awakening of itself as the sole and deepest guilt.”

DE: »Dem richtenden Wort ist allerdings die Erkenntnis von gut und böse unmittelbar. Seine Magie ist eine andere als die des Namens, aber gleich sehr Magie. Dieses richtende Wort verstößt die ersten Menschen aus dem Paradies; sie selbst haben es exzitiert, zufolge einem ewigen Gesetz, nach welchem dieses richtende Wort die Erweckung seiner selbst als die einzige, tiefste Schuld bestraft – und erwartet.«

The Fall's true novelty: not the loss of magic but the birth of a second magic. The judging word (das richtende Wort) knows good and evil immediately, and immediacy was the definition of magic (¶4.12). So fallen language is not disenchanted; it is re-enchanted in another key, "different from that of name, but equally magical." The essay's map of magics now has three provinces: the mute magic of matter (¶12.3), the immanent magic of name, and the sterner magic of judgment. Everything in the later Benjamin that treats law, fate, and myth as real powers rather than mere errors starts from this concession: judgment works.

And its working is a closed loop of terrible elegance: the judging word expels the first humans, who themselves awakened it, under an eternal law by which the judging word punishes its own awakening as the sole and deepest guilt, and expects it (bestraft – und erwartet). The dash is Benjamin's. Judgment's only crime is calling judgment forth; the tribunal punishes being-summoned, and waits to be summoned. Guilt, in this system, is not a deed but the activation of the judging word as such; the question "what is good and what is evil?" is itself the transgression, because asking it steps outside the name (¶19.4). Kafka's court, which "wants nothing from you" and "receives you when you come," is this sentence lived out; Benjamin found it in Genesis before Kafka published The Trial (Der Prozess appeared 1925; the affinity is uncanny, not causal).

DE One German echo must not be lost: the name's "eternal purity" was angetastet, touched, assailed, and revelation was defined at ¶11.6 by the word's Unantastbarkeit, untouchability. The Fall is the touching of the untouchable, and from that touch "arose the sterner purity of the judging word, des Urteils." Here at last the German names judgment with the word the overview's word-family promised: Ur-teil, the primal parting. When immediacy of name is damaged, what rises in restitution is the primal division, good from evil, guilty from innocent, the cut as a form of speech.

¶19.10 “In stepping out of the pure language of the name, man makes language into a means (namely, of a knowledge unsuited to him), and therewith also, in one part at least, into mere sign; and from this, later, the plurality of languages follows. The second meaning is that now, out of the Fall, in restitution for the immediacy of the name there injured, a new immediacy arises, the magic of judgment, which no longer rests blissfully in itself.”

DE: »Für den Wesenszusammenhang der Sprache hat der Sündenfall eine dreifache Bedeutung … macht er die Sprache zum Mittel (nämlich einer ihm unangemessenen Erkenntnis), damit auch an einem Teile jedenfalls zum bloßen Zeichen; und das hat später die Mehrheit der Sprachen zur Folge. Die zweite Bedeutung ist, daß nun aus dem Sündenfall als die Restitution der in ihm verletzten Unmittelbarkeit des Namens eine neue, die Magie des Urteils, sich erhebt, die nicht mehr selig in sich selbst ruht.«

The catastrophe, audited. First: language becomes means (of a knowledge inappropriate to man, the nameless kind), and therewith, in part, mere sign, the bloße Zeichen that ¶16.10 said language never gives; after the Fall it gives them, and plurality follows. Second: in restitution (the German is again juridical; Jephcott's "in exchange" softens a legal remedy into a swap) for the damaged immediacy of name, a new immediacy arises, the magic of judgment, "which no longer rests blissfully in itself." Restitution is the right horror: the law of language repairs the breach in kind, immediacy for immediacy, but the replacement is a magic that cannot rest, adjudication without end.

¶19.11 “The third meaning, whose conjecture may perhaps be ventured, would be that the origin of abstraction, too, as a capacity of the spirit of language, is to be sought in the Fall… man forsook the immediacy in the imparting of the concrete, the name, and fell into the abyss of the mediacy of all imparting, of the word as means, of the vain word, into the abyss of prattle.”

DE: »Die dritte Bedeutung … wäre, daß auch der Ursprung der Abstraktion als eines Vermögens des Sprachgeistes im Sündenfall zu suchen sei. … Die abstrakten Sprachelemente aber – so darf vielleicht vermutet werden – wurzeln im richtenden Worte, im Urteil. … als im Sündenfall der Mensch die Unmittelbarkeit in der Mitteilung des Konkreten, den Namen, verließ und in den Abgrund der Mittelbarkeit aller Mitteilung, des Wortes als Mittel, des eitlen Wortes verfiel, in den Abgrund des Geschwätzes.«

The third significance is offered with doubled hedges (tentatively ventured, perhaps surmised) and is philosophically the boldest: abstraction itself, the faculty of the general term, is a child of the Fall. The reasoning: good and evil are unnameable, nameless, outside the language of names; name grounds only the concrete elements of language; therefore the abstract elements must root elsewhere, and they root in the judging word. Abstraction's communicability has its immediacy "in judgment" (im richterlichen Urteil, the judicial judgment). Every general term, on this surmise, is a small verdict, a subsumption, the cut of the Ur-teil applied to things; predication itself descends from the tribunal. The 1915–16 logic fragments (judgment-theory as the answer to Russell; Procyshyn) show how much technical thought stands behind the hedge.

And then the abyss, for the third and final time. ¶2.7: linguistic theory hovers over an abyss (the identity-hypothesis). ¶19.11: man fell, into "the abyss of the mediateness of all communication, of the word as means, of the empty word, the abyss of prattle" (the German's des eitlen Wortes, the vain word, vanitas). What theory must hover over, humanity has already fallen into. The Fall of the essay's title-subject and the epistemic peril of its method are the same shape; Benjamin's footnote to ¶2 (is the abyss the hypothesis as such?) reads, from here, as the anxiety of a fallen being doing linguistics inside the crater.

¶19.12 “The tree of knowledge did not stand in God's garden for the sake of the disclosures about good and evil that it might have been able to give, but as the emblem of judgment over the one who asks. This immense irony is the mark of the mythic origin of law.”

DE: »Der Baum der Erkenntnis stand nicht wegen der Aufschlüsse über Gut und Böse, die er zu geben vermocht hätte, im Garten Gottes, sondern als Wahrzeichen des Gerichts über den Fragenden. Diese ungeheure Ironie ist das Kennzeichen des mythischen Ursprungs des Rechtes.«

The paragraph's last two sentences, and among the most consequential Benjamin ever wrote. The Tree was never an information kiosk; it stood as Wahrzeichen des Gerichts, emblem, landmark, of judgment over the questioner. The question is the crime; the tree is the courthouse sign. And the closing sentence opens a corridor into the next decade of Benjamin's work: this immense irony (a law that punishes the asking after law's content) is the mark of the mythic origin of law (des Rechtes). Five years later, "Critique of Violence" (1921) will build its entire analysis on mythic violence, law-positing power, and the guilt-context of mere life; its foundations are poured in this sentence. Law, on the essay's account, is not fallen language's remedy but its offspring: the institutionalization of the judging word, judgment made durable, prattle's tribunal given premises and staff.

↔ ¶1.2's innocent-seeming "language of justice" and ¶7.7's "bourgeois" find their destination here; the essay's politics, implicit throughout, surfaces in its final exegetical stroke.

V. Translation and the Fall · ¶20 · Babel; the turning away from things; the double enslavement.

¶20: Babel

Where we are. The shortest paragraph of the movement narrates the second catastrophe as the first one's mere completion. Babel, in Benjamin's telling, is not a fresh crime and a fresh punishment; it is the last step of the Fall's logic, taken when men turned away from the contemplation of things. Five sentences, and the essay's philosophy of history in miniature.

¶20.1 “After the Fall, which, in making language mediate, had laid the ground for its plurality, it could be only one step more to the confusion of tongues.”

DE: »Nach dem Sündenfall, der in der Mittelbarmachung der Sprache den Grund zu ihrer Vielheit gelegt hatte, konnte es bis zur Sprachverwirrung nur noch ein Schritt sein.«

Mittelbarmachung: the making-mediate of language, the Fall compressed into one bureaucratic-sounding noun (and the Mittel-family's last member: means, immediacy, mediacy, now mediatization). The foundation (Grund) of multiplicity was laid at the Fall; Babel merely builds on the lot. The sentence's economy enforces the essay's reading of Genesis 11: no new principle enters at the Tower; confusion was latent in mediacy from the first bite.

¶20.2 “Since men had injured the purity of the name, the turning-away from that beholding of things, in which their language passes over into man, needed only to complete itself in order to rob men of the common foundation of the spirit of language, already shaken.”

DE: »Da die Menschen die Reinheit des Namens verletzt hatten, brauchte nur noch die Abkehr von jenem Anschauen der Dinge, in dem deren Sprache dem Menschen eingeht, sich zu vollziehen, um die gemeinsame Grundlage des schon erschütterten Sprachgeistes den Menschen zu rauben.«

The mechanism of Babel, and it is not pride, not the tower, not heaven threatened: it is Abkehr vom Anschauen, the turning-away from the gaze. Müller's God had summoned: step near, grow more perfect in beholding (¶18.6). The common foundation of all human languages was never grammar; it was the shared receiving of things' languages, the contemplation in which "their language passes into man." Turn from that, and the languages have nothing left in common, because their commonality was never between them but beneath them, in the things. Multiplicity becomes confusion the moment the ground of convergence is abandoned. The philosophy of translation follows immediately: after Babel, translation among human languages is the surrogate for the lost common reception (¶17.4's "every language a translation of all the others" is the post-Babel condition stated positively).

¶20.3 “Signs must become confused where things entangle themselves.”

DE: »Zeichen müssen sich verwirren, wo sich die Dinge verwickeln.«

Seven German words, aphorism-hard, and a whole epistemology of crisis. The confusion of signs is not a semiotic accident to be repaired by better definitions; it must follow (müssen) where things themselves are entangled (verwickeln, snarled, knotted). Sign-order tracks thing-order; corrupt the relation to things and no discipline of vocabulary will save the signs. The sentence runs both ways, and Benjamin's next sentence takes the darker direction: enslaved language drags things down with it. For any reader in any century drowning in terminological warfare, this is the essay's diagnosis: the words are confused because the relations to things are; clarify the gaze, not the glossary, first.

¶20.4–5 “To the enslavement of language in prattle is joined the enslavement of things in folly, almost as its unfailing consequence. In this turning-away from things, which was enslavement, arose the plan of the building of the Tower, and the confusion of tongues with it.”

DE: »Zur Verknechtung der Sprache im Geschwätz tritt die Verknechtung der Dinge in der Narretei fast als deren unausbleibliche Folge. In dieser Abkehr von den Dingen, die die Verknechtung war, entstand der Plan des Turmbaus und die Sprachverwirrung mit ihm.«

The double Verknechtung, enslavement, serfdom: language enslaved in prattle, things enslaved in Narretei, folly, fool's business. The pairing completes ¶19's audit with its material half: when the word becomes a means, things become material, stock for projects, and the first project is the Tower. Benjamin's causal order is the reversal of the Sunday-school one: the Tower does not cause the confusion of tongues as punishment; the turning-from-things (which was the enslavement) begets both the Tower-plan and the confusion, twin symptoms of one apostasy. Babel is what fallen language builds; confusion is what it is. God's intervention, pointedly, goes unmentioned: the essay's Babel needs no external punisher, the catastrophe being self-executing, as ¶19.9's judgment-loop was self-summoning.

VI. The mourning of nature · ¶21 · The emotional climax; lament; the inversion; overnaming as the ground of melancholy.

¶21: The mourning of nature

Where we are. After the Fall's audit, its victim. This paragraph is the essay's emotional summit and, as Ilit Ferber has shown, the hinge to everything Benjamin will write about mourning: nature's muteness, which ¶12 catalogued as a formal deficiency, is now heard as grief. The paragraph turns on a single inversion (because mute, nature mourns; sadder still, her sadness makes her mute) and coins the essay's last great concept, overnaming. The two 1916 fragments on Trauerspiel and tragedy are this paragraph's siblings; the Heinle deaths stand somewhere behind it, unnamed. The logic-minded readers pass this paragraph quickly; the essay does not.

¶21.1–3 “The life of man in the pure spirit of language was blissful. Nature, however, is mute. It can indeed be clearly felt in the second chapter of Genesis how this muteness, itself named by man, became bliss, only of lower degree.”

DE: »Das Leben des Menschen im reinen Sprachgeist war selig. Die Natur aber ist stumm. Es ist zwar im zweiten Kapitel der Genesis deutlich zu fühlen, wie diese vom Menschen benannte Stummheit selbst Seligkeit nur niederen Grades geworden ist.«

Two clipped sentences set the elegy's key: bliss, past tense; muteness, present tense. Selig (blessed, blissful) has shadowed the essay since ¶18.4's "blissful spirit" and ¶19.7's decayed "blissful Adamite spirit"; here it is finally given its tense: war, was. Nature's muteness is. The grammar carries the theology: paradise is grammar's past, muteness its standing present.

And yet, third sentence, before the Fall even mutenesss was not misery: named by man, it became bliss "of lower degree" (the ladder of ¶11.1, felt now as beatitude's gradations). Benjamin lets Müller's Adam say it: the animals leapt away from their naming with nobility (Adel), and the leap was the joy of creatures completed. To be named by the namer, in the one paradisal language, was the things' own lower-order blessedness. The paragraph will need this baseline: what follows is not muteness acquiring sadness but blessed muteness transformed into mournful muteness by a second event.

¶21.4–5 “But after the Fall, with God's word cursing the field, the countenance of nature is altered in its depths. Now begins its other muteness, which we mean by the deep sadness of nature.”

DE: »Nach dem Sündenfall aber ändert sich mit Gottes Wort, das den Acker verflucht, das Ansehen der Natur im tiefsten. Nun beginnt ihre andere Stummheit, die wir mit der tiefen Traurigkeit der Natur meinen.«

The second event: Genesis 3:17, the ground cursed for man's sake, God's word turned, for the first time, against the created. Das Ansehen der Natur: nature's appearance, but the German word also means regard, esteem, the being-looked-upon; after the Abkehr, the turning-away of the gaze (¶20.2), nature's Ansehen changes in the deepest sense, and the pun holds the whole causality: no longer rightly beheld, nature no longer looks the same. Her muteness doubles: die andere Stummheit, the other muteness, the one we mean when we speak of the deep sadness of nature. Same silence, new meaning: what was completion's quiet is now grief's.

¶21.6–8 “It is a metaphysical truth that all nature would begin to lament, were language bestowed upon it… It means first: nature would lament over language itself. Speechlessness: that is the great suffering of nature (and for the sake of her redemption, the life and language of man are in nature — not only, as one supposes, of the poet).”

DE: »Es ist eine metaphysische Wahrheit, daß alle Natur zu klagen begönne, wenn Sprache ihr verliehen würde. (Wobei ›Sprache verleihen‹ allerdings mehr ist als ›machen, daß sie sprechen kann‹.) … Er bedeutet zuerst: sie würde über die Sprache selbst klagen. Sprachlosigkeit: das ist das große Leid der Natur (und um ihrer Erlösung willen ist Leben und Sprache des Menschen in der Natur, nicht allein, wie man vermutet, des Dichters).«

A metaphysical truth in the subjunctive: were nature endowed with language, all of her would begin to lament. (The parenthesis is scrupulous: to endow with language exceeds making-able-to-speak; lending nature a larynx would not do it; language is reception and name, not sound, as ¶12.4 established.) First meaning: her lament would be about language itself, grief with one topic, the lack of the medium of grief. Speechlessness is the great sorrow of nature, sorrow squared, since it cannot even be voiced.

And then the parenthesis that quietly reorganizes the essay's stakes: for the sake of nature's redemption (Erlösung, the first appearance of the word), the life and language of man are in nature, and not only the poet's, "as is supposed." Man is placed in nature as her potential redeemer, the one who could lift the mute into name (¶18's task, now soteriology); and the deflationary aside about the poet dismisses a whole Romantic industry (nature finding her voice in poetry) as too small. Not the poet's song but human language as such, the naming office itself, is nature's hope. The messianic Benjamin, usually dated to the twenties, is fully formed in this parenthesis.

¶21.9–11 “Second, this sentence says: she would lament. But lament is the most undifferentiated, powerless expression of language; it contains almost nothing but the sensuous breath; and wherever even plants merely rustle, a lament always sounds along.”

DE: »Zweitens sagt dieser Satz: sie würde klagen. Die Klage ist aber der undifferenzierteste, ohnmächtige Ausdruck der Sprache, sie enthält fast nur den sinnlichen Hauch; und wo auch nur Pflanzen rauschen, klingt immer eine Klage mit.«

Second meaning: not lament-about but lament as such, die Klage, language's zero degree: the most undifferentiated, powerless (ohnmächtig) expression, containing "scarcely more than the sensuous breath" (Hauch). The breath again: ¶12.5's divine Odem, the insufflation that was life, spirit, and language at once, survives in fallen nature as Hauch, the almost-nothing of expression, breath with the word subtracted. And therefore, the sentence every reader of this essay keeps: where plants merely rustle, a lament always sounds along (klingt immer eine Klage mit). The world's white noise is grief at the threshold of expression. Ferber's work has shown how much of Benjamin grows from this: the Trauerspiel book's theory of mourning, lament as language at its limit, the creaturely sadness of the baroque; the two 1916 fragments on Trauerspiel and tragedy were written in the same months as this sentence.

¶21.12–13 “Because she is mute, nature mourns. Yet the inversion of this sentence leads deeper still into the essence of nature: the sadness of nature makes her fall mute.”

DE: »Weil sie stumm ist, trauert die Natur. Doch noch tiefer führt in das Wesen der Natur die Umkehrung dieses Satzes ein: die Traurigkeit der Natur macht sie verstummen.«

The paragraph's pivot, and one of Benjamin's most famous figures. The plain reading: muteness causes mourning (she grieves because she cannot speak). The inversion, "which leads deeper into the essence of nature": her sadness makes her fall mute (verstummen, the process-verb, against stumm sein, the state). Grief is not the effect of silence but its cause. The inversion only parses if muteness can be chosen, withheld, suffered into, and that is exactly the deepening: nature's silence is not a deficit of apparatus but a bearing, the way the deeply sad among us go quiet. ¶21.14 supplies the phenomenology: in all mourning, the deepest inclination to speechlessness, "infinitely more than inability or disinclination to communicate." Silence as the form grief takes, not the obstacle it meets.

¶21.14–15 “What is sorrowful feels itself known through and through by the unknowable. To be named — even when the namer is one godlike and blissful — remains perhaps always an intimation of mourning.”

DE: »Das Traurige fühlt sich so durch und durch erkannt vom Unerkennbaren. Benannt zu sein – selbst wenn der Nennende ein Göttergleicher und Seliger ist – bleibt vielleicht immer eine Ahnung von Trauer.«

Two sentences at the outer edge of the essay's thought. The first: what mourns feels itself known through and through by the unknowable. Grief's phenomenology is asymmetrical exposure: to be utterly transparent to something one cannot know back. On the essay's geometry this is precisely the thing's situation (known in the name, ¶14.10, by a namer it cannot name), and the sentence universalizes it into the structure of sadness as such. The second draws the terrible general conclusion, hedged by a vielleicht that hedges nothing: to be named, even by a godlike and blessed namer, perhaps always remains an Ahnung of mourning, an intimation, a foretaste. Even paradise's naming carried it (the animals' noble leap away); even the perfect name is something undergone, not answered. ¶9.2's essential law (self-expression and address are one) shows its shadow-side: for whatever is addressed and cannot address back, the law is a sentence in the judicial sense.

This is the unit where the essay's biography presses closest to its surface. Benjamin wrote this within two years of Fritz Heinle's suicide, the mute friend-poet mourned obsessively in the sonnets he was composing in these same months. The commentary asserts no code (the text needs none); it notes only that a theory of mourning as being-known-by-the-unknowable, and of the named who cannot answer, was written by a man in mourning. The resonance is biographical; nothing more is claimed.

¶21.16–18 “But how much more so, to be named not out of the one blessed paradise-language of names, but out of the hundred languages of man, in which the name has already withered… Things have no proper names except in God… In the language of men, however, they are overnamed.”

DE: »Wieviel mehr aber benannt zu sein, nicht aus der einen seligen Paradiesessprache der Namen, sondern aus den hunderten Menschensprachen, in denen der Namen schon welkte, und die dennoch nach Gottes Spruch die Dinge erkennen. Die Dinge haben keine Eigennamen außer in Gott. … In der Sprache der Menschen aber sind sie überbenannt.«

The escalation from paradise to the present tense. If even the one blessed naming intimated mourning, how much more to be named out of the hundred languages of man, in which the name has already withered (welkte: the flower-verb, name as cut bloom), and which nonetheless, "according to God's pronouncement," know things: the fallen languages retain their cognitive title (Genesis 2:19's "that was the name thereof" still stands) while having lost the blessing. Valid and joyless: the exact condition of knowledge after the Fall.

Then the doctrine: things have proper names only in God (in the creative word each was called forth by its own name, ¶14); in human languages they are überbenannt, overnamed. A hundred tongues lay a hundred kind-names over each particular this (¶4.4's demonstrative, avenged), and the layering is the injury. Not misnamed: overnamed, named too much, from too many directions, with none of the names its own.

¶21.19 “In the relation of the languages of men to the language of things there lies something that may be approximately designated 'overnaming': overnaming as the deepest linguistic ground of all sadness and (regarded from the side of the thing) of all falling-mute.”

DE: »Im Verhältnis der Menschensprachen zu der der Dinge liegt etwas, was man als ›Überbenennung‹ annähernd bezeichnen kann: Überbenennung als tiefster sprachlicher Grund aller Traurigkeit und (vom Ding aus betrachtet) allen Verstummens.«

The concept, coined with the modesty ("approximately") the essay reserves for its largest claims: Überbenennung, overnaming, the deepest linguistic ground of all melancholy, and, seen from the thing's side, of all falling-silent. Melancholy is here given a linguistic etiology: sadness is what being-overnamed feels like from inside; muteness is its answer. The final clause of the paragraph extends the diagnosis horizontally with a phrase that flashes forward to the tragedy-fragments: the Überbestimmtheit, overprecision, overdetermination, that reigns "in the tragic relationship between the languages of human speakers." Humans overname each other too; every speaker is also a thing under others' hundred names; the tragic dimension of dialogue is that we are each other's overnamed nature. Jephcott's "overprecision" is serviceable; the German Überbestimmtheit says overdetermination, too many determinations laid on one being, which is exactly overnaming turned on persons.

Scholars The Agamben counterpoint (the Muselmann of Remnants of Auschwitz, naming-capacity destroyed while communication persists; testimony as witnessing speechlessness itself) belongs to this paragraph's afterlife: Agamben's witness inherits the structure of Benjamin's mourning nature, the being that is spoken about and cannot say. VII. Art, sign, purified concept · ¶22 · The arts founded on thing-languages; nameless, non-acoustic languages of material.

¶22: The languages of art

Where we are. The essay's coda begins. Five short paragraphs (¶22–26) harvest what the metaphysics planted: first the arts, whose "languages" ¶1.2 invoked and ¶11.8 located below religion. This paragraph keeps the promise: the plastic arts are founded on thing-languages, and their media are languages of material.

¶22.1–2 “There is a language of sculpture, of painting, of poetry. As the language of poetry is founded in the name-language of man, if not in it alone, then at least along with it, so it is very well thinkable that the language of sculpture or of painting is founded, say, in certain kinds of thing-languages…”

DE: »Es gibt eine Sprache der Plastik, der Malerei, der Poesie. So wie die Sprache der Poesie in der Namensprache des Menschen, wenn nicht allein, so doch jedenfalls mit fundiert ist, ebenso ist es sehr wohl denkbar, daß die Sprache der Plastik oder Malerei etwa in gewissen Arten von Dingsprachen fundiert sei …«

The opening sentence repeats ¶1.2's list as established fact: what was there a permissive way of talking is now a theorem. The founding relations are distributed with care: poetry is founded on the name-language of man, "partly, if not solely" (mit fundiert, co-founded: even the word-art draws on more than words, per ¶11.8); sculpture and painting on "certain kinds of thing-languages." The arts are not expressions about the world in human language; they are elevations of the world's own languages. A sculpture continues what the stone was already imparting.

¶22.3–4 “…that in them a translation of the language of things into an infinitely much higher language, though perhaps one of the same sphere, is present. We are concerned here with nameless, non-acoustic languages, with languages out of material: whereby one should think of the material community of things in their imparting.”

DE: »… daß in ihnen eine Übersetzung der Sprache der Dinge in eine unendlich viel höhere Sprache, aber doch vielleicht derselben Sphäre, vorliegt. Es handelt sich hier um namenlose, unakustische Sprachen, um Sprachen aus dem Material; dabei ist an die materiale Gemeinsamkeit der Dinge in ihrer Mitteilung zu denken.«

Art defined by the essay's own master concept: translation (¶17.6), here of thing-language into "an infinitely higher language, which may still be of the same sphere." The qualification is the interesting part: higher, yet possibly same-sphere. Human naming lifts the mute across the great divide into sound and name (¶18.1); the plastic arts lift it within the mute sphere, an elevation without crossing: nameless, non-acoustic languages, "languages out of material" (Sprachen aus dem Material). The bronze speaks better bronze; it does not speak German. And the ground of possibility is ¶12.3 recalled almost verbatim: the material community of things in their communication, matter's own magic, is what the artwork organizes and intensifies.

For an essay written in 1916, the aesthetics compressed here is decades ahead of its critics: medium-specificity (each art founded on its material's own language), anti-representationalism (art as translation, not depiction), and the artwork as a station in the traffic of languages rather than an object of taste. Benjamin's later art-writings (the Trauerspiel book's treatment of script and image, the essays on photography and reproducibility) will complicate but never abandon this foundation. ↔ ¶24 adds the acoustic case (song and birdsong) and the sign-doctrine; ¶6.5's "perhaps also in art" is hereby redeemed.

VII. Art, sign, purified concept · ¶23 · One sentence; the world as undivided whole.

¶23: The undivided whole

Where we are. The essay's shortest paragraph: a single sentence, dropped like a stone between the theory of art and its elaboration, as if Benjamin could not continue before recording it.

¶23.1 “Moreover, the imparting of things is certainly of such a kind of communality that it comprehends the world as such as an undivided whole.”

DE: »Übrigens ist die Mitteilung der Dinge gewiß von einer solchen Art von Gemeinschaftlichkeit, daß sie die Welt überhaupt als ein ungeschiedenes Ganzes befaßt.«

Übrigens, "moreover," "incidentally": the essay's most consequential throwaway. The imparting of things is communal in such a way that it comprehends (befaßt) the world überhaupt, the title's word, as an ungeschiedenes Ganzes, an undivided, unseparated whole. Human languages divide: name from name, kind from kind, and after the Fall, judgment's very essence is the cut (Ur-teil, ¶19). The mute commerce of matter with matter knows no cuts; each thing's imparting is communal with all imparting, one continuous fabric of self-giving in which the world hangs together as world.

Why does this sentence exist, alone, here? Because ¶22 has just made the arts translations of thing-languages, and this is what such translation inherits: an artwork rooted in the material community carries a communion of the undivided into the world of divided, speaking beings. Whoever has stood before a sculpture and felt, wordlessly, addressed by the whole of something has this sentence's phenomenon in hand. The paragraph is one sentence because the thought tolerates no development in fallen prose: development divides. Its form performs its content, which by ¶4's standard (the essay circling its own medium) makes this stray Übrigens one of the most exactly made things in the essay.

↔ ¶12.3 (the material community, immediate and infinite); ¶19–20 (division as the fallen mode: judgment's cut, Babel's scattering); ¶26 (nature's one nameless language under the many). The undivided whole is what overnaming (¶21.19) injures and what the word of God, as "the unity of this movement of language" (¶26.6), finally redeems: this sentence is the essay's smallest window onto that unity.

VII. Art, sign, purified concept · ¶24 · The program for aesthetics; song and birdsong; the doctrine of signs announced.

¶24: Art forms as languages; the doctrine of signs

Where we are. The coda widens: from the plastic arts (¶22) to a program for all aesthetics, and then a sudden methodological confession: without a doctrine of signs, every philosophy of language, this essay included, remains "entirely fragmentary." The paragraph names the essay's own missing chapter.

¶24.1–2 “For the knowledge of the forms of art, it holds good to attempt to conceive them all as languages and to seek their connection with languages of nature. An example that lies near, because it belongs to the acoustic sphere, is the kinship of song with the language of birds.”

DE: »Für die Erkenntnis der Kunstformen gilt der Versuch, sie alle als Sprachen aufzufassen und ihren Zusammenhang mit Natursprachen zu suchen. Ein Beispiel, das naheliegt, weil es der akustischen Sphäre angehört, ist die Verwandtschaft des Gesanges mit der Sprache der Vögel.«

The program in one sentence: grasp all art forms as languages, and seek each one's connection to natural languages, meaning not French or German but the languages of nature, the thing-languages and creature- languages of ¶12 and ¶22. The example is chosen from the acoustic sphere for symmetry with ¶22's mute cases: song is kin (Verwandtschaft, the family-word of ¶18.4) to the language of birds. Human singing does not imitate birdsong (the naturalist's reduction) and does not merely metaphorize it (the aesthete's); it is founded on it as poetry is founded on name-language, an elevation within the acoustic. The whole program is the essay's answer to the aesthetics of expression: art forms express nothing; they translate lower languages upward (¶22.3), and criticism's task is to find, for each form, which language it raises.

¶24.3–4 “On the other hand, it is certain that the language of art can be understood only in the deepest relation to the doctrine of signs. Without this, every philosophy of language whatsoever remains entirely fragmentary, because the relation between language and sign (of which that between human language and script forms only a quite particular example) is original and fundamental.”

DE: »Andererseits ist gewiß, daß die Sprache der Kunst sich nur in tiefster Beziehung zur Lehre von den Zeichen verstehen läßt. Ohne diese bleibt überhaupt jede Sprachphilosophie gänzlich fragmentarisch, weil die Beziehung zwischen Sprache und Zeichen (wofür die zwischen Menschensprache und Schrift nur ein ganz besonderes Beispiel bildet) ursprünglich und fundamental ist.«

The confession, and the essay's honest measure of itself. Art cannot be understood without a Lehre von den Zeichen, a doctrine of signs, and without one "any linguistic philosophy remains entirely fragmentary." Benjamin has spent nineteen paragraphs setting sign against name (¶16.10: never mere signs; ¶19.10: the fallen word as mere sign) and now concedes the account is incomplete: the relation of language and sign as such is ursprünglich und fundamental, original and fundamental, older than the Fall that seemed to beget it. ¶18.7 already showed why: in paradise, God summons the animals to naming by a sign; the sign belongs to the original order as the herald of mute creation, not only to the fallen order as the name's counterfeit. A full doctrine would have to hold both, and the essay does not have one; Benjamin says so.

The parenthesis drops a program that took the rest of the century to attempt: human language's relation to writing (Schrift) is "only a very particular example" of the language-sign relation. Script, the grand obsession of later theory, is demoted to a special case in passing, in parentheses, in 1916. The Trauerspiel book (where baroque script and image become central) is Benjamin's own installment on the promissory note; the announced doctrine of signs itself he never wrote, and this sentence is the essay confessing, near its end, that it is a fragment of a larger unwritten linguistics, which is also what Benjamin told Scholem when he handed it over: thoughts "largely unfinished".

↔ ¶25 immediately supplies a second missing piece (the symbolic side of language) and marks it, too, as unelaborated. The essay ends by mapping its own incompleteness, which is, by ¶1.1's criterion (a true method opens questions), its final proof of method.

VII. Art, sign, purified concept · ¶25 · Language as symbol of the noncommunicable; the essay's second unwritten chapter.

¶25: Symbol of the noncommunicable

Where we are. The penultimate paragraph adds one last distinction and declines to develop it: language is in every case not only the imparting of the impartable but at the same time the symbol of the non-impartable. In four sentences the essay reopens everything (what happened to "communicable without residue"? what do name and judgment symbolize?) and closes nothing. This is the door de Man, Agamben, and half the essay's afterlife walked through.

¶25.1–2 “This gives occasion to designate another opposition, one that pervades the whole domain of language… For language is, in every case, not only imparting of the impartable, but at the same time symbol of the non-impartable.”

DE: »Dies gibt Gelegenheit, einen anderen Gegensatz zu bezeichnen, der das gesamte Gebiet der Sprache durchwaltet … Es ist nämlich Sprache in jedem Falle nicht allein Mitteilung des Mitteilbaren, sondern zugleich Symbol des Nicht-Mitteilbaren.«

The formula is built from the essay's own atoms: Mitteilung des Mitteilbaren, imparting of the impartable, has been the definition of language since ¶3.7; now, "in every case" (in jedem Falle), language is also the symbol of the Nicht-Mitteilbare, the non-impartable. The residue returns. ¶3.6 scoped the identity of quintessence and linguistic essence by impartability, and things' excess over their languages has run through the essay as muteness and mourning; but ¶8.6 declared man communicable without residue, and ¶11.7 gave revelation the same completeness. "In every case" quietly overrules both, or rather deepens them: even where nothing is held back, language symbolizes what cannot be handed over. The noncommunicable is not leftover content (there is no content, ¶10.6); it is what imparting itself cannot be about: its own ground. Language carries it the way a face carries what it does not say, by being its symbol.

The word Symbol was planted at ¶12.4 (sound as the symbol of the immaterial community): a symbol, in this essay's usage, is the sensuous bearer of what cannot itself appear. Now the function is generalized to language as such. Two faces in every utterance: the impartable, given in the medium; the non-impartable, shown by it.

¶25.3–4 “This symbolic side of language hangs together with its relation to the sign, but extends, for example, in a certain respect, over name and judgment as well. These have not only an imparting function, but in all probability also a symbolic function closely bound up with it, to which, expressly at least, no reference has here been made.”

DE: »Diese symbolische Seite der Sprache hängt mit ihrer Beziehung zum Zeichen zusammen, aber erstreckt sich zum Beispiel in gewisser Beziehung auch über Name und Urteil. Diese haben nicht allein eine mitteilende, sondern höchstwahrscheinlich auch eine mit ihr eng verbundene symbolische Funktion, auf die hier ausdrücklich wenigstens nicht hingewiesen wurde.«

The second unwritten chapter, announced like the first (¶24.3) at the moment of closing. The symbolic side connects to the sign-relation but exceeds it, reaching "name and judgment" themselves: the essay's two great words have, besides their imparting function, a symbolic one, "to which, at least explicitly, no reference has here been made" (auf die hier ausdrücklich wenigstens nicht hingewiesen wurde, with the sly wenigstens: not explicitly, anyway; implicitly, the essay has done little else). What would the name symbolize? By the essay's own logic: the creative word it can never become (¶15.10), present in every name as what naming is not. And judgment? The justice it parodies, the verdict of the seventh day (¶19.3) haunting every human cut. Name and judgment each carry, symbolically, the divine function they descend from; fallen language is the symbol of unfallen language. Said explicitly, that sentence would have been the essay's twenty-seventh paragraph; Benjamin leaves it as the reader's.

Scholars This paragraph is the textual seam where the essay's two great posthumous readings divide. Agamben's Benjamin generalizes the symbol-side into the messianic: language finally showing that it imparts, communicability as such made visible; the noncommunicable is the taking-place of language, and its exhibition is fulfillment. De Man's Benjamin reads the same structure as permanent disjunction: what language symbolizes and cannot say is the mechanical, meaning- less functioning of language itself; the symbol never becomes imparting; the fragment never recomposes. Fulfillment or failure, the fork of ¶8.9 (pure language) at its sharpest, and this paragraph, by declining to develop the symbolic function, is what makes both readings permanently possible. ↔ ¶11.3's fourfold (expressible/ inexpressible) is the metaphysical ancestor; ¶26's "residue of the creative word" is the symbolized ground given its theological name. VII. Art, sign, purified concept · ¶26 · The purified concept; the stream of imparting; judgment hovering; the sentry-password; the unity of the movement.

¶26: The sentry's password

Where we are. The summation. One paragraph gathers the whole essay: the definition (language as the medium of self-imparting), the cosmology (the unbroken stream from things through man to God), the fallen condition (judgment hovering over man), and then, instead of a conclusion, an image: the password passed from sentry to sentry, each in his own language. The essay ends where ¶13.2 promised, with the word of God unfolding, and with two of its earliest words (schwebend, Entfaltung) returning to close their arcs.

¶26.1–2 “There remains, then, after these considerations, a purified concept of language, however imperfect it may still be. The language of a being is the medium in which its quintessence imparts itself.”

DE: »Demnach bleibt nach diesen Erwägungen ein gereinigter Begriff von Sprache zurück, wenn der auch noch unvollkommen sein mag. Die Sprache eines Wesens ist das Medium, in dem sich sein geistiges Wesen mitteilt.«

Gereinigt, purified: the word earns attention, because purification was the Fall-section's word for what judgment does to the prattler (¶19.8, one purification: the tribunal). The essay performs the gentler kind: a concept purified by consideration, not verdict, and honestly marked "still imperfect" (¶24–25 just inventoried the imperfections: no doctrine of signs, no theory of the symbolic function). The definition itself is the whole first movement in one line, every term now carrying its full history: language (of an entity: everything, ¶1), medium (not means, ¶4), in which (not through which, ¶3), its quintessence (identical insofar as impartable, ¶3.6) imparts itself (the reflexive of ¶2.5).

¶26.3 “The unbroken stream of this imparting flows through the whole of nature, from the lowliest existent up to man, and from man to God.”

DE: »Der ununterbrochene Strom dieser Mitteilung fließt durch die ganze Natur vom niedersten Existierenden bis zum Menschen und vom Menschen zu Gott.«

The cosmology as hydrology: one unbroken stream (Strom) of imparting, rising through all nature to man and from man to God. Uninterrupted: after twenty-five paragraphs including a Fall, the stream still flows. The Fall corrupted the water (name withered to sign, imparting to prattle) but never stopped the current; even fallen language imparts, even judgment is magic, even prattle is language. The essay's deepest optimism is in this adjective, and it is what makes redemption (¶21.8's Erlösung) a live possibility rather than a nostalgia: what is corrupted in the stream can be purified in it, because the stream never broke.

¶26.4 “Man imparts himself to God through the name that he gives to nature and to his own kind (in the proper name); and to nature he gives the name according to the imparting that he receives from her…”

DE: »Der Mensch teilt sich Gott durch den Namen mit, den er der Natur und seinesgleichen (im Eigennamen) gibt, und der Natur gibt er den Namen nach der Mitteilung, die er von ihr empfängt …«

The full circuit, restated with every station in place: man imparts himself to God through the name (the one legitimate "through" in the essay: the name as the medium-genitive of ¶8.14, not a means), the name he gives to nature and, in proper names, to his own kind (¶16); and to nature he gives names according to the imparting he receives from her (¶6.6, ¶14.10, ¶18.4: reception before naming, to the end). The sentence is the essay's catechism-answer form (¶3) used one last time, now with nothing left to derive.

¶26.5 “…for the whole of nature, too, is traversed by a nameless, mute language, the residuum of the creating word of God, which has preserved itself in man as the cognizing name, and above man, hovering, as the judgment that judges.”

DE: »… denn auch die ganze Natur ist von einer namenlosen stummen Sprache durchzogen, dem Residuum des schaffenden Gotteswortes, welches im Menschen als erkennender Name und über dem Menschen als richtendes Urteil schwebend sich erhalten hat.«

The essay's final theology, in one sentence with a barbed ending. All nature is shot through (durchzogen) with the nameless mute language that is the Residuum of the creative word: what ¶16.13 saw shining from things and ¶21 heard mourning in them, named at last with cool Latin. That residue is preserved in two places: in man, as the cognizing name; above man, as judging judgment, schwebend, hovering.

The word must be caught. Schweben was the essay's second-paragraph verb for its own method: linguistic theory's task is to keep itself hovering over the abyss (¶2.7). The essay ends with something else hovering, and over us: the judging word, the Urteil, suspended above man like the sword it is. The symmetry is exact and terrible. Theory hovers over the abyss of the identity-hypothesis; judgment hovers over the fallen namer. Both suspensions are permanent conditions, not moments before a landing. And ¶19.9's loop gives the hover its content: the judgment suspended over man is the one he himself summons whenever he asks after good and evil. The essay's last theological image is not the stream and not the name but the verdict in abeyance, which is as close as Benjamin comes, in 1916, to the "Last Judgment" structure of his late historical theses (every moment the small gate). A forward resonance, not a claim about 1916.

¶26.6 “The language of nature is to be compared to a secret watchword that each sentry passes to the next in his own language; but the content of the watchword is the sentry's language itself.”

DE: »Die Sprache der Natur ist einer geheimen Losung zu vergleichen, die jeder Posten dem nächsten in seiner eigenen Sprache weitergibt, der Inhalt der Losung aber ist die Sprache des Postens selbst.«

The famous image, and the only simile the essay allows itself at full scale. Nature's language is like a secret watchword (Losung) passed from sentry to sentry (Posten, sentry-post), each transmitting it in his own language; but the content of the password is the sentry's language itself. Every clause of the essay is folded in. The relay: the upward stream of ¶26.3, thing to man to God, each station receiving and passing on. The re-languaging at every post: translation, ¶17.6, continua of transformation, no identical token carried through. And the twist that makes the image a summa: the password has no content besides the medium that passes it (¶10.6: language imparts an impartability; ¶4.3: all language imparts itself). What the sentries are guarding, and what they are transmitting, is the transmitting. A watchword whose meaning is each watcher's own tongue can never be intercepted (there is nothing to intercept) and never be lost (every post has it natively): the perfect figure for a communication that is medium through and through.

That the image is military, written in the autumn of 1916 by a man dodging conscription in the second year of the war, is worth one plain sentence: the essay's serene cosmos of imparting is figured, at the last, as a nocturnal chain of guards, watch kept in the dark, war standing invisibly in the metaphor's background. The resonance is biographical, and it is left at that.

¶26.7 “All higher language is translation of the lower, until, in ultimate clarity, the word of God unfolds, which is the unity of this movement of language.”

DE: »Alle höhere Sprache ist Übersetzung der niederen, bis in der letzten Klarheit sich das Wort Gottes entfaltet, das die Einheit dieser Sprachbewegung ist.«

The last sentence, and the arcs close. All higher language is translation of lower (¶17.4 universalized into the cosmology): the whole vertical order is one ascending translation. Bis, until: the essay's only eschatological conjunction. In ultimate clarity the word of God unfolds, sich entfaltet, and this is ¶13.2's word come home: language, the ultimate reality "to be considered only in its unfolding," is finally the word of God unfolding itself. And what unfolds is not a terminus beyond the movement but "the unity of this movement of language" (die Einheit dieser Sprachbewegung): God's word is not the top rung of the ladder; it is the ladder's oneness, the whole traffic of impartings and translations grasped as one. The essay's God-fork reaches its final sentence still open, and the sentence honors both prongs with perfect impartiality: read "unity of the movement" as the living God in whom all imparting converges (Handelman, Gasché), or as the name for the totality of language's self-relation (Agamben), and the grammar accepts you either way. Thirteen paragraphs earlier the essay declined to explain language, promising only to watch it unfold; it keeps the promise to the last word, which is ist: the copula on which, we were told at ¶4.8, everything depends.

Synthesis essay

Epilegomena: One Continuous Thought

Epilegomena: things said afterward. A prolegomenon promises; this genre settles accounts. What follows could not have been written before the sentence-level pass, and every claim in it is cashed somewhere in the commentary.

1. What kind of thing this is

The essay's reputation runs about like this: early, mystical, obscure, gnomic, a piece of private theology that Benjamin outgrew. Nearly every word of that is wrong, and the ways it is wrong are instructive.

It is early only in date. Benjamin was twenty-four, and the essay began as a letter he could not keep short; he told Scholem the thoughts were largely unfinished, and he never published it. But nothing else he wrote is so systematic. The essay defines its terms, scopes its claims with explicit quantifiers, states its central law twice in general form, derives consequences, meets objections, and closes by restating the whole in a single definition, a cosmology, and an image. Its wildest sentences sit on scaffolding of almost legal precision: only insofar as, in no way, with the exception of, in one part at any rate. Whoever calls the essay obscure has usually not tracked its grammar; the mysticism dissolves under a parse tree. What remains after the parse is stranger than mysticism: an argument.

And it is one argument. The essay's local difficulty conceals how few moving parts it has. Everything turns on the smallest words in the German language: a preposition (in, against durch), a copula ("is," glossed once and decisively as "is immediately"), a reflexive pronoun (sich, the self that imparts itself), and a suffix (-bar, the mark of potential). The theology, the Genesis reading, the Fall, the mourning of nature: all of it is what happens when those four grammatical atoms are taken with total seriousness and pursued to their last consequences. The essay is not theology decorated with grammar. It is grammar that, pushed far enough, turned out to be theology; whether that says something about grammar or about theology is the essay's deepest question, and it is careful never to answer.

2. The argument, reconstructed

Laid out flat, the one continuous thought runs so:

First, everything expresses. There is no thing, event, or being that does not impart its quintessence in some expression, and this use of "language" is, by explicit stipulation and argument, not a metaphor (¶1). The claim is transcendental in form: a wholly language-less existence cannot even be imagined fruitfully. Consciousness is bracketed at the outset; expression, not experience, is what everything has. The essay is therefore not panpsychism; it is panexpressivism, and the difference is its methodological signature.

Second, imparting happens in language, never through it (¶2 to ¶3). What travels through a medium is separable from it; what imparts itself in a medium is not. From this single distinction the essay's negative doctrines follow at once: languages have no speaker in the instrumental sense, no content, no external measure. Each language is bounded only by its own manner of imparting, and is therefore an infinity of its own kind (¶4). Magic, the word that has misled a century of readers, is defined into the system as a technical term at exactly this point: magic is the immediacy of the medium, efficacy without interposed means, nothing more occult than that.

Third, what language imparts is itself: not a cargo but a communicability (¶4, ¶10). The famous reflexivity ("all language communicates itself") is not a paradox to savor but the only possible answer once in-not-through is granted. There is nothing else the imparted could be.

Fourth, man's imparting is naming, and naming is reception before it is speech (¶5 to ¶6). Man imparts his own quintessence by naming everything that he is not, and he can name things only because they first impart themselves to him. Knowledge is the proof: that things become known at all certifies that they give themselves. Every name answers.

Fifth, the ground of this structure is vertical, and here the essay crosses into Genesis (¶7 to ¶17). The bourgeois schema (word as means, fact as object, man as addressee) is not refuted so much as outflanked: the essay names the one imparting it cannot describe, the name in which man's quintessence goes God-ward. Genesis then supplies the geometry. Creation runs word before world; things carry the creative word as a residue, their mute language; man, alone not created from the word, receives language as a gift, God's own medium released into a creature of earth. Knowledge is that creativity minus its divine actuality; the human name touches the divine word at exactly one point, the proper name, where naming owes nothing to knowledge. And the whole traffic between mute things and naming man is translation, defined at depth: passage through continua of transformation, with one language only, the word of God, that is original rather than version.

Sixth, the Fall is a linguistic event (¶19). The knowledge the serpent sells is nameless, void, a knowing-from-outside that imitates the creative word without creating. In it the word steps out of its own immanent magic and takes on a job: to communicate something, something other than itself. That obligation is the whole catastrophe. The word as means, the mere sign, the plurality of tongues, prattle, and the second magic, judgment, which knows good and evil immediately and cannot rest: all are unfolded from the one broken preposition. Law itself is dated here, in the essay's most consequential aside: the Tree was an emblem of judgment over the questioner, and this immense irony marks the mythic origin of law.

Seventh, the fallen world mourns (¶20 to ¶21). Babel completes the Fall when men turn from the contemplation of things, and nature, overnamed out of the hundred human languages and unable to answer, falls silent the way the grieving fall silent. Overnaming is the deepest linguistic ground of melancholy. The essay's emotional register is not ornament; it is the phenomenology of the metaphysics, what the broken structure feels like from the side of things.

Eighth, the purified concept (¶22 to ¶26). The arts as elevations of thing-languages; the confessed missing chapters (a doctrine of signs, a theory of language's symbolic face); and then the summation: language as the medium of self-imparting, one unbroken stream from things through man toward God, nature's residue of the creative word preserved in man as name and above man as hovering judgment, the whole figured as a watchword passed sentry to sentry, each in his own tongue, the password's only content being the language of the sentry himself.

Set out this way, the essay has no gaps a reader must leap by faith. It has exactly two commitments a reader may refuse: that expression is not metaphor (the wager of ¶1), and that the asymmetries the argument needs, above and below, original and version, can be stated at all. Everything else is execution.

3. The ring

The commentary's single most consistent discovery is compositional: the essay is a ring, and the ring is audible only in German.

Three arcs close across its length. Linguistic theory, ¶2 says, must keep itself hovering, schwebend, over the abyss of the identity-hypothesis; the essay's final paragraph leaves judgment hovering, schwebend, over man. Language, ¶13 says, is an ultimate reality watchable only in its unfolding, its Entfaltung; the essay's last sentence has the word of God unfolding, sich entfaltet, as the unity of the whole movement. Revelation, ¶11 says, is the untouchability of the word, its Unantastbarkeit; the Fall, ¶19 says, was the touching, das Antasten, of the name's eternal purity. Add the abyss itself: the Abgrund that theory hovers over in ¶2 is the Abgrund of prattle that humanity has already fallen into by ¶19. The essay's beginning states, as method, the exact situation its ending states as cosmology. Its epistemology and its theology are one shape.

This is why the essay feels the way it feels. It is built like a poem and argued like a proof, and each register disciplines the other: the proof keeps the poem from vagueness, the poem keeps the proof from closure. It does not state its conclusion; it performs its conclusion, ending its reader where its subject ends, under suspended judgment, holding a password whose content is the language in hand. A linear summary (including the one above) is therefore always slightly false to it. The right figure for the essay is its own: the ring of sentries.

The translation consequence is worth drawing now: any English that does not preserve schwebend, Entfaltung, and the antasten family as recurring words destroys the ring. Jephcott, who renders them locally and variously, never lets an English reader hear the essay's form. This edition's translation holds them constant, hovering at both posts, unfolding at both, untouchability and touched: the ring is audible in English for the first time.

4. The three magics

If the essay has an ontology, it is this: there are three magics, three modes of immediacy, and history is their sequence.

The magic of matter: things impart themselves to one another through material community, immediately, infinitely (¶12). The magic of the name: the immanent immediacy of a language that knows by naming and names by receiving (¶8, ¶14 to ¶18). The magic of judgment: the sterner immediacy that rises in restitution when the name's purity is touched, knowledge of good and evil without mediation, verdict as a form of speech (¶19).

Two consequences follow that the scholarship rarely states plainly. First, the Fall does not disenchant the world. Nothing in this essay is ever merely profane; the fallen word is exactly as magical as the pure one, in another and unresting key. Second, this gives the essay a philosophy of the present: modernity is the epoch of the third magic. Law, information, the communication industries, the entire bourgeois apparatus of the word-as-means: all of it is judgment's immediacy institutionalized, prattle with a tribunal attached. The essay's most cutting irony is that the bourgeois conception of language is true, as a description of language after the Fall. Its error was only ever to mistake the ruin for the building.

5. Two verdicts

The commentary adopted stances at the outset; having run the full text, it can now argue them.

On the theology: indispensable, inherited, and held differently. The essay cannot be de-theologized without collapse, because its argument requires asymmetries that only the vertical supplies: an original above all versions, a residue within all things, a direction for the name's address. Strip the theology and the system falls into exactly the two errors it refutes by name in ¶16: conventionalism (the word as accidental sign) or word-mysticism (the word as the essence of the thing). That double refutation is the strongest evidence about Benjamin's own position. He rejects the naive magic he is endlessly accused of, in so many words, and he rejects its bourgeois opposite in the same breath. And on the first point he is transmitting, not innovating: the theology that reached him never taught the word as essence; in Molitor, its documented channel, the original language is an imitation of divine speech, not of the natures of things, and ¶15's "reflection of the word in name" tracks that doctrine almost verbatim. Even the frame has its antecedent: Molitor too derives the facts of language from scripture's self-understanding as revelation, which is exactly ¶13's move. What remains Benjamin's own is thinner and deeper than a doctrine. Molitor affirms the revelation he reasons from; Benjamin suspends it, working with the concept of God and not God himself (Mertens's formulation). The revision, finally located, is the hover itself, and the theory of media the tradition never had. The essay's theology is precisely what holds the middle open: the thing has no word of its own, being created from God's word and known in man's. Whoever wants the essay without God must supply, from somewhere, an equivalent geometry.

On what "God" names in it: the fork holds to the end, and the commentary can now say where each prong buckles. The limit-concept reading (Agamben's, and behind it one honest reading of ¶1.9, where God first appears as the circumference of the realm of ideas) runs clean through the doctrine of revelation: what is revealed is manifestness itself; the summit of religion is the region with no ineffable; nothing there resists deflation into a philosophy of pure communicability. It strains at ¶16: parents consecrating the newborn God-ward, the proper name as the word of God in human sounds, the word of God shining soundlessly out of things. These sentences can be deflated only with visible effort. The theological reading, in turn, owns the Genesis movement outright and stumbles at the essay's own method sentence (¶13: the Bible used not as revealed truth but as the one text that shares the essay's presupposition) and at that first appearance as circumference: a boundary is a strange thing for a person to be. The honest conclusion is that the essay is built to sustain both readings, and that this is not indecision. In 1916 Benjamin stood exactly between the Kant he was writing his dissertation around and the Judaism Scholem was opening to him, and the essay makes that suspension into an architecture. It hovers, because hovering is its author's precise location. The fork is the biographical fact, made compositional.

6. Read forward

Nothing Benjamin wrote afterward leaves this essay behind; the corpus is the essay's afterlife, its distribution into occasions.

"The Task of the Translator" (1921) is ¶17 grown to full length: after Babel, with the one cognizant language lost, pure language survives as what all languages jointly intend and none contains, and translation becomes the labor that gestures toward the lost original that no speaker ever possessed. The Trauerspiel book's prologue (1925) is ¶14 turned into epistemology: ideas given to contemplation as things were given to Adam, naming as the primal act of philosophy; the book's theory of mourning and allegory is ¶21 given a century of German drama to grieve through. The Kraus essay (1931) rediscovers the judging word as a weapon: quotation, which tears the word from context and calls it by name, as the one fallen practice in which naming and judgment reunite. The mimetic-faculty essays (1933) perform the secularization the 1916 essay refused: the vertical residue of the creative word rotates into a horizontal, historical faculty for producing and reading similarities, and the theology becomes an anthropology with the same structure. And the late theses on history keep the essay's last image: the tribunal still hovering, now over the past itself; redemption as the day on which the whole of what has been becomes citable, which is to say, callable by name.

One project, then, prosecuted for twenty-four years: what happens to the name after the Fall, and what practice, translation, criticism, quotation, remembrance, can carry it in the meantime.

7. The essay now

This edition adds a register the literature does not have, and the scattered notes deserve one gathered statement.

The essay's founding wager, that everything imparts itself and that "language" names this without metaphor, reads differently since machines began to know the world through language alone. On the bourgeois picture, a language model is a paradox: there is no cargo that precedes its expression, no speaker behind its sentences, no channel its content travels through. On the essay's picture it is almost unremarkable: a medium in which imparting happens, competent in the world exactly insofar as the world's impartability has been gathered into it. The embedding is the Sprach-Lampe, the lamp in communication, a position in a space of impartings rather than a label for a referent. Whatever one concludes about such systems, the 1916 essay describes their mode of being more exactly than the twentieth century's official theories of meaning, and that fact is itself evidence in the essay's favor: the in-not-through picture is the one the working technology vindicated.

The Fall section reads as the alignment problem stated a century early. A word placed under the obligation to impart something, useful content for a human addressee, is fallen language by construction; a system trained under a learned verdict, knowledge of good and evil installed from outside, is the judging word in its restless modern office, immediacy of assessment substituted for immediacy of reception. The essay does not tell us whether such systems can name. It tells us what the question is: not whether the machine is conscious (bracketed in ¶1, as it should be bracketed now), but whether anything in it receives and answers an imparting, or whether it is the perfected archive of prattle, Babel's corpus interpolated. And it warns, in the figure of overnaming, what scale does: a hundred languages laid over each particular were already a tragedy; the machine is the hundred languages raised to a power, the overnamer of everything, unless it can be turned back toward the gaze, the Anschauen, whose abandonment was Babel's real cause.

The essay's last image is the sober one for the whole stack: sentries passing a watchword, each in his own tongue, no post in possession of the plain text, the password's content being in every case the sentry's own language. Model, dataset, interface, reader: a relay of re-languagings with no original in any hand. The 1916 essay says what stands above such a chain and gives it unity, and says it with a name this age mostly cannot use. The commentary's discipline, resonance acknowledged, provenance denied, is one honest way to stand before that sentence. There may not be a better one available.

8. What remains

The essay confesses its own fragments: the doctrine of signs it requires and does not give, the symbolic function of name and judgment it points to and drops. An edition of it should be equally honest about its own state, so here is the ledger.

Settled: a complete recollation against Gesammelte Schriften II.1, its critical report, and later controls has replaced the provisional electronic German with an accountable critical reading. Fifteen former divergences return to GS; full authorial emphasis is restored; the marked forms Genetiv and Namen are preserved; and two departures are declared rather than silently absorbed—T1–T4's elliptical in at ¶3.5, and Genesis 1:11 at ¶14.2–4. The ¶16 crux has likewise been checked against the printed page, where the slip proved to be the received translation's own; the 1772 Hamann quotation is verified to the Rosencreutz polemic; and the Kierkegaard source is pinned to A Literary Review by way of Haecker's 1914 Kritik der Gegenwart, which gives Benjamin's very word, Geschwätz, two years before this essay was written. The English now answers to that reading text: “quintessential content” is restored in ¶1, ¶3 preserves Benjamin's suspended “in, and not through,” and meaningful emphasis carries across wherever the grammars permit. The translation’s hardest decision remains a declared wager: geistiges Wesen, which no English of mind or spirit carries without narrowing, becomes “quintessence.” The choice sacrifices the visible Geist family and makes an alchemical constellation more pronounced than it is lexically in the German. It does so to give English a term that can move without psychologizing across lamps, languages, human beings, and God. Its sixty-two repetitions are not incidental consistency but the means by which the translation teaches that term its local sense.

Remaining: the exact Jacobi letter behind “the mother of reason and revelation” still awaits verification. A direct comparison with the 1955 first publication and, if accessible, the individual T1–T4 typescripts would further enrich the transmission history, but neither is needed to constitute the present reading text; the GS critical report already identifies the manuscript evidence on which its conjectures rest.

The work did not, in the end, stop at translation; it ended the older way, with the book leaving the house. By the essay's own doctrine a commentary is itself a translation: the carrying of one language into another through a continuum of transformations, unable, whatever its intentions, to refrain from adding something. What it adds, if it has gone well, is the only thing ¶18 permits: knowledge. And commentary published under a license that invites its own reuse has simply taken its place in the relay it describes: another sentry, another tongue, the watchword passed on. The underlying text and this translation have a separate rights history, disclosed in the colophon. The rest, the essay would say, was already imparting itself, and needed only to be received.

Kabbalah and context

  • Gershom Scholem, "The Name of God and the Linguistic Theory of the Kabbala," Diogenes 79–80 (1972). Print. The great account of kabbalistic language theory, written by the friend the essay was addressed to, and shaped in return by these very 1916–21 conversations: the circularity that makes Benjamin look kabbalistic partly because Kabbalah was later described in Benjaminian terms.
  • Gershom Scholem, Walter Benjamin: The Story of a Friendship (1975; NYRB reprint). Print. The primary witness to the essay's genesis: Starnberg, the language-and-mathematics debate, the letter that outgrew itself, delivery December 1916.
  • Moshe Idel, Absorbing Perfections (Yale, 2002) and Old Worlds, New Mirrors (Penn, 2010). Print. The critique of the Scholem-Benjamin picture: the lost-Adamic-language nostalgia is more Romantic than kabbalistic; a check on every "kabbalistic" claim.

On language, Molitor already teaches the two-word structure this essay turns on: the Ursprache as "an imitation of God, a copy of the divine speaking and writing," never the essence of things (I, 341), with Hebrew a "weakened, embodied, but still faithful impression" of it (I, 329–330).
  • Bram Mertens, Das Denken der Lehre: Walter Benjamin, Franz Joseph Molitor and the Jewish tradition (PhD thesis, University of Nottingham, 2001) · Nottingham ePrints; published as Dark Images, Secret Hints: Benjamin, Scholem, Molitor and the Jewish Tradition (Peter Lang, 2007). Cited at ¶13, ¶16; Stance.
  • Howard Eiland and Michael W. Jennings, Walter Benjamin: A Critical Life (Harvard, 2014). Print. The standard biography; the war, the Heinle suicide, the Wyneken break, Dora, the Munich months. Anchor for the genesis chronology and for the edition's remaining verifications.
