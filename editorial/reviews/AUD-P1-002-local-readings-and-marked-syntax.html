The question before the edition

With the German witness and governing lexicon now settled, the next audit item is sentence-level: which English peculiarities reproduce Benjamin’s own pressure on syntax, and which instead reverse agency, flatten a lexical distinction, import an unintended sense, or simply impede reading?

Published in Edition 2026.07.14. The complete approved package, including amended ¶15 option B, is now part of the public living edition.

Evidence and method

Each locus was checked against four layers, in this order:

  1. the maintained German reading text, now accountable to Gesammelte Schriften II.1 and its apparatus;
  2. the sentence’s local argument and recurring word-families;
  3. Edmund Jephcott’s canonical English as a control witness, using the private Carpe transcript at /Users/jesse/src/carpe/artifacts/benjamin/text/jephcott-translation-transcript.md;
  4. external lexical or primary-source evidence where the first three do not settle the question.

Jephcott is a control, not an authority over the German. At several loci he supplies the clean solution; at ¶12 and ¶21 he shares the present edition’s error; at ¶16–17 he clarifies reception but then adds a fertilization-and-birth sequence more explicit than Benjamin’s German.

1 · ¶1.9 and the circumference of ideas

German

im Bezirk der Ideen, deren Umkreis diejenige Gottes bezeichnet

Current

“within the precinct of ideas whose circumference traces the idea of God”

Jephcott

“within that realm of Ideas whose circumference defines the idea of God”

Both Umkreis and diejenige [Idee] Gottes can be nominative or accusative here. The audit initially treated the present agency as probably reversed, but the sentence does not permit that confidence. Unmarked German word order makes Umkreis the likelier subject, and Jephcott reads it that way. The current commentary, however, repeatedly assumes the opposite: God marks the boundary of the thinkable.

A · Preserve the grammatical surface

“within the precinct of ideas whose circumference designates the idea of God.”

Recommended. It retains default agency, translates bezeichnet more directly than “traces,” and makes the ambiguity explicit in commentary.

B · Reverse the agency

“within the precinct of ideas whose circumference the idea of God marks.”

Matches the current commentary’s geometry, but chooses the marked grammatical parse against Jephcott.

C · Spatially neutralize

“within the precinct of ideas, at whose circumference lies the idea of God.”

Readable and noncommittal about agency, but replaces Benjamin’s verb of designation with an added verb of position.

Recommendation: adopt A and revise the commentary from a categorical reverse-agency reading to a two-sided note. This is an ambiguity to disclose, not a definite error to “correct.”

2 · ¶12.1, figurative and sensory

German

nach der übertragenen und der sinnlichen Bedeutung

Current

“according to its figurative and its literal meaning”

Recommended

“This sentence has a double sense—figurative and sensory: the languages of things are imperfect, and they are mute.”

sinnlich means belonging to, conveyed by, or perceptible to the senses. The next sentence names sound as the sensory lack of things, and the current commentary already explains the phrase correctly. “Literal” therefore creates a distinction the German does not make. The lexical evidence is straightforward: see Duden, sinnlich.

Recommendation: this is a definite correction. The dash removes the awkward singular “meaning” after a two-member pair while retaining Benjamin’s compact taxonomy.

3 · ¶16, what the proper name vouches for

German

Mit ihm wird jedem Menschen seine Erschaffung durch Gott verbürgt

Current

“By it, each man is vouched his creation by God”

Recommended

“Through it, every person’s creation by God is vouched for.”

The grammatical subject is “his creation by God”; jedem Menschen is dative. The present English makes the person look like the direct object of “vouched,” while Jephcott’s “guaranteed his creation” remains unidiomatic. The proposed sentence preserves Benjamin’s nominal claim and the edition’s established “vouch” family without implying that a person is promised an act of creation in the future.

Recommendation: definite correction. “Through it” is slightly freer than mit ihm, but states the name’s mediating function cleanly.

4 · ¶16–17, reception and conception

This is the most consequential choice in the item. Benjamin writes empfangend, sprachempfangend, and then twice Empfängnis. The verb means receiving; the noun ordinarily means conception or becoming pregnant, but its historical range includes taking-in or reception. Benjamin then pairs it with Spontaneität, activating the philosophical contrast between receptivity and spontaneity.

The lexical doubleness is real. Duden’s empfangen begins with receiving or taking in and includes conceiving a child; Empfängnis gives conception as the modern sense while recording older “taking-in” and “instruction” in its history.

A · Reception-led

“it has become, in one part, receptive, though receptive to language. This reception is directed toward the language of things…”

“For reception and spontaneity together … this word applies also to that reception of the nameless in the name. It is translation…”

Recommended. Preserves one English family and the argument’s receive/respond structure; commentary retains conception as a secondary resonance.

B · Double signal

Use “receptive” for the adjectives but “reception/conception” or “conception” for the nouns.

Makes both senses visible in the reading text, but creates an editorial doublet Benjamin did not write and breaks the repeated noun.

C · Conception-led

Retain “conceiving,” “conception,” and the commentary’s fertilization-and-birth account.

Preserves the biological charge most strongly, but obscures ordinary receiving and makes the Kantian contrast depend on commentary to become visible.

Recommendation: A. The reading text should carry the grammatical action—things impart, the name receives, translation answers—while a concise note preserves the pregnancy resonance. The commentary should stop treating fertilization and birth as the sole or certain content of the German. Jephcott’s added “fertilized” and “give birth” are useful evidence for the resonance, not words to import.

5 · ¶19.9, arousing judgment

German

sie selbst haben es exzitiert … die Erweckung seiner selbst

Current

“they themselves excited it … the awakening of itself”

Recommended

“they themselves aroused it … its own awakening”

exzitieren means to stimulate or arouse; in present English, “excited it” strongly suggests enthusiasm or sexual excitement. Duden gives anregen; Jephcott’s “aroused” also prepares the following Erweckung.

Recommendation: definite correction to “aroused it.” The commentary already paraphrases the action as awakening, so its argument changes little.

6 · ¶21, the Maler Müller quotation in context

The audit was right that the line could not be responsibly recast in isolation. Müller repeats its syntax three times in “Huldigung der Tiere,” once for each created domain:

Land animals: und sah an den Adel, wie sie von mir wegsprangen, darum daß ihnen der Mann einen Namen gab.

Birds: und ich sah an den Adel, wie sie von mir wegflogen, darum daß ihnen der Mann einen Namen gab.

Sea creatures: und ich sahe an den Adel, wie sie von mir wegbrausten, darum daß ihnen der Mann einen Namen gab.

Friedrich Müller, “Huldigung der Tiere,” TextGrid Repository, stable object.

The varying verbs—sprang, flew, surged—show that Adel names the noble manner or dignity manifested in departure after naming. Benjamin quotes only the first member. The source also repeatedly says that each creature receives its own name, licensing “each” where an English plural pronoun would otherwise suggest one collective name.

Current

“and saw by the nobility with which they sprang away from me, because the man had given them a name.”

Jephcott

“And saw by the nobility with which they leaped away from me that the man had given them a name.”

Recommended

“and saw how nobly they sprang away from me, because the man had given each a name.”

Recommendation: recast the source’s archaic noun-and-inversion as an adverbial clause. This retains the strangeness that matters—the creatures springing away in nobility—without manufacturing English opacity. The commentary should cite the full threefold parallel and avoid claiming the syntax is transparent.

7 · ¶21.16, hundreds rather than one hundred

German

nicht aus der einen … sondern aus den hunderten Menschensprachen

Current and Jephcott

“not out of the one … but out of the hundred languages of man”

Recommended

“not out of the one … but out of the hundreds of human languages”

hunderten is plural: the contrast is one paradisal language against the hundreds of human languages. No symbolic exact hundred is present in the German.

Recommendation: definite correction. This has the largest downstream footprint: the ¶4 and ¶21 commentary, the epilogue’s summary of mourning, and its language-model coda all currently build rhetoric around an exact hundred and must change together.

8–9 · Two additional meaning-bearing findings

¶15 · Language is set free in man Approved

Full German

im Menschen entließ Gott die Sprache, die ihm als Medium der Schöpfung gedient hatte, frei aus sich

Published baseline

“in man, God released language … freely out of himself”

Superseded first recommendation

“in man, God set language, which had served him as the medium of creation, free.”

The current sentence does turn frei into the adverb “freely,” but “out of himself” is not an addition: it answers to aus sich. Jephcott’s clean “set language … free” suppresses that phrase rather than settling it. The edition therefore needs a fresh decision that preserves both im Menschen and aus sich without reproducing the current English friction.

A · Follow the canonical control

“rather, in man God set language, which had served him as the medium of creation, free.”

Most fluent and established in English; leaves aus sich without an explicit counterpart.

B · Preserve both vectors · approved

“rather, in man God let language, which had served him as the medium of creation, go free from himself.”

Approved amended reading. Keeps emancipation in man and release from God; the marked English answers to the marked German.

C · Retain the published reading

“rather, in man, God released language, which had served him as medium of creation, freely out of himself.”

Preserves both spatial terms, but makes frei adverbial and remains difficult to parse.

¶16.10 · The mystical theory is itself mistaken

German

Mißverständlich ist aber auch die Ablehnung … durch die mystische Sprachtheorie.

Current

“the rejection … by the mystical theory of language is likewise open to misunderstanding.”

Recommended

“But the mystical theory’s rejection of the bourgeois theory of language also rests on a misunderstanding.”

The next sentence states the mystical theory’s error: it identifies word and thing. The current English instead suggests that readers may misunderstand an otherwise viable rejection, weakening Benjamin’s deliberate demolition of both conventionalism and naive word-mysticism. Jephcott gets the relation right.

Decision · 2026-07-14: amended ¶15 option B was approved and implemented. The ¶16 correction was already part of the approved package.

The focused pass · a policy for marked syntax

Literalness is not the same as reproducing every German idiom with English words. The proposed policy is controlled estrangement:

Preserve marked English where the German itself is marked and the form performs conceptual work. Normalize where only the English is marked—especially ordinary German idioms, false friends, or calques that alter agency or relation. Explain the important retained estrangements in commentary.

LocusDispositionReason or proposed reading
¶3Retain: “What is impartable in a quintessence, that is its linguistic essence.”The German topicalizes the clause; the fronting makes what is impartable the theorem’s subject.
¶6Retain: “Why name?”Wozu benennt? is itself a verbless, jolting question. Smoothing it would erase Benjamin’s interruption.
¶10Retain: “A content of language there is not.”The fronted German object and categorical negation are meaning-bearing; commentary already teaches the form.
¶19Retain: “The word as outwardly imparting…”Benjamin’s German is itself an appositional fragment after naming the Fall.
¶6Normalize: “the answer runs” → “the answer is”lautet is ordinary German idiom; English “runs” contributes no conceptual pressure.
¶13Normalize: “these expositions follow it in principle in this: that in them…”Proposed: “these reflections follow it in principle by presupposing language as…” The present nesting is English-only friction.
¶16Normalize: “In a strict spirit” → “In the strict sense”im strengen Geist marks rigor, not a separate spiritual entity.
¶17Normalize: “this word holds also of” → “this word applies also to”Ordinary English relation; no German wordplay is lost.
¶17Normalize: “Its full meaning it gains” → “It gains its full meaning”The German order is unmarked; the English inversion is not.
¶19Normalize: “the question after good and evil” → “the question of good and evil”die Frage nach is ordinary government, not a spatial “after”; the present phrase also collides with “after creation.”
¶22Normalize: “very well thinkable” → “entirely conceivable”sehr wohl denkbar is idiomatic emphasis.
¶22Normalize: “infinitely much higher” → “infinitely higher”The degree remains; the English becomes grammatical.
¶24Normalize: “For the knowledge … it holds good to attempt”Proposed: “To understand the forms of art, one should attempt to conceive them all as languages…”
¶24Normalize: “An example that lies near” → “A ready example”naheliegt means an example that readily suggests itself.
¶25Normalize: “hangs together with … extends over” → “is connected with … extends to”Preserves both relations without German preposition calques.

Boundary of this pass: this is not a general modernization. The edition’s established conceptual families—impart, impartability, speaks itself out, name-language, mediality, creating/cognizing—remain intact. Nor does this review authorize unlisted smoothing during implementation; every reader-visible sentence change will be enumerated first.

Decision bundle

Four approvals would settle this item while keeping the genuinely different questions visible:

  1. Definite readings: approve the recommended corrections at ¶12, ¶15, both ¶16 syntax/meaning loci, ¶19, and both ¶21 loci.
  2. Reception policy: choose reception-led, double-signal, or conception-led English for ¶16–17. The recommendation is reception-led with the conception resonance in commentary.
  3. ¶1.9 ambiguity: choose default agency, reversed agency, or spatial neutralization. The recommendation is default agency with explicit disclosure.
  4. Register: approve or amend the controlled-estrangement policy and its exact representative list before implementation.
