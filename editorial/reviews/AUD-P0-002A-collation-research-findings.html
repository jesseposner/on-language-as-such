Answer in one paragraph

The commissioned research confirms that the current German descends from a defective electronic transcription, but it changes the initial audit in four important ways. There are sixteen—not fifteen—word-and-punctuation divergences from Gesammelte Schriften II.1; the unusual forms Namen and Genetiv are defensible readings rather than obvious errors; GS is itself a T1-based critical text containing three declared conjectures; and the current display has erased the German text’s entire emphasis layer. The synthesis recommends returning fifteen of the sixteen divergences to GS, retaining Genesis 1:11 as one declared, medium-confidence emendation, restoring the unanimous typescript reading in at one GS conjecture, and encoding all attested emphasis before the public text is revised.

Disposition: the recommendations were approved, implemented, verified, and published together as Edition 2026.07.12.5.

The three persisted research memoranda

  • Variants 1–7 and ¶¶1–11 sweep — page-image verification, Reclam and Jephcott controls, GS conjectures, Genetiv, and early-paragraph emphasis.
  • Variants 8–15 and ¶¶11–26 sweep — Genesis, der menschliche Namen, later variants, biblical controls, and the second-half sweep.
  • Adversarial full-text recollation — all 26 paragraphs, witness genealogy, quotation synchronization, emphasis inventory, and challenge to the first two recommendations.

The memoranda preserve disagreements and source limitations rather than manufacturing unanimity. This page is the synthesis and recommendation; the memoranda remain the full evidentiary record.

What the research changes

New The count is sixteen

The original audit missed ¶8: ED and textlog have modern Genitiv, while the GS page image and Reclam 2019 have dated but legitimate Genetiv. No further word-or-punctuation divergence survived high-resolution verification. A suspected Paradiesessprache difference was an OCR illusion; ED and GS agree.

New GS is not an authorial diplomatic witness

GS II.3 identifies T1—a typescript carrying Benjamin’s handwritten corrections—as its Druckvorlage, alongside T2–T4. It also discloses three editorial conjectures. The project may still use GS as its declared base, but german-gs.md must be described as a source-faithful transcription of the printed GS critical text, not as a diplomatic transcription of Benjamin’s typescript.

New All German emphasis is missing

Every one of the 26 displayed German paragraphs contains zero emphasis markup. GS visibly emphasizes propositions, contrasts, pronouns, and even the suffix -bar; GS II.3 reports one doctrinal sentence as double-underlined in all four typescripts. Reclam’s born-digital sample exposes 56 italic XML fragments before the sample ends. This is a systematic loss of textual information, not cosmetic styling.

New The web witnesses are one dependent branch

textlog and Projekt Gutenberg share a diagnostic cluster of rare corruptions and reproduce GS’s own conjectural readings. Their agreement is evidence of common electronic ancestry, not independent confirmation. The repository’s “99.7% agreement with a second online witness” claim must disappear when this issue is implemented.

Evidence hierarchy and remaining limits

  1. GS reading text: all eighteen essay pages, printed pp. 140–157, inspected as images. It remains the approved base.
  2. GS critical report: printed pp. 931–936, inspected for T1–T4, the copy-text declaration, conjectures, and transmitted emphasis.
  3. Reclam 2019: the publisher’s page-image/born-digital sample, used as a reputable later German reading edition where available.
  4. Jephcott: the user-supplied canonical English scan, visually checked as an editorial and translation control—not as authority over the German.
  5. Electronic transcriptions: textlog and Projekt Gutenberg, useful for provenance but given little independent weight.

Evidence still missing: the text pages of the 1955 Schriften II and facsimiles of T1–T4 were not obtained. Those comparisons would refine the genealogy, especially at Genesis and disputed punctuation, but they do not alter what the inspected GS pages and GS report say. The synthesis does not treat them as blockers to a reasoned decision now.

The two genuinely contested readings

¶3: in or in einer Sprache?

GS reading

in einer Sprache

Clearer modern syntax and the declared base reading, but explicitly a GS editorial expansion against all four typescripts.

Recommended critical reading

in

Unanimous T1–T4 reading; grammatically sharp ellipsis; followed by Reclam and closely tracked by Jephcott.

Recommendation: adopt T1–T4 in and report GS: in einer Sprache (conj.). Confidence: medium-high.

¶14: Genesis 1:11 or 1:14?

Probable authorial / base reading

Genesis 1:14

GS prints it and does not flag the locus; it contains Luther’s literal Es werden Lichter, and textlog preserves it. In the larger act, however, God then makes the lights at 1:16.

Recommended edition emendation

Genesis 1:11

Jephcott and Reclam both use it, and the creation act better fits Benjamin’s immediate claim that speech alone initiates it. Its Luther wording is Die Erde lasse …, not the literal Es werde.

Recommendation: retain 1:11, label it ED em., and replace the current categorical commentary with this two-sided account. Confidence: medium-high. Following GS 1:14 remains a viable conservative choice; the evidence does not justify calling either number an obvious correction.

Two forms that should no longer be “corrected”

der menschliche Namen

Modern standard German favors der Name, but GS, textlog, and Reclam all print Namen. More importantly, Benjamin uses the same nominative again at p. 155: in denen der Namen schon welkte. IDS grammis recognizes der Namen as a less-prevalent nominative formed by stem reanalysis. Restore the authorial markedness and explain it briefly; do not normalize it silently.

Genetiv

GS and Reclam retain this form; Duden marks it dated, not nonsensical. The approved policy follows GS spelling, so the critical reading should use Genetiv. If a later project-wide modernization policy is adopted, it must identify this as normalization rather than textual correction.

The missing emphasis layer

Restoration must be complete, not a hand-picked set of attractive italics. The maintained GS source should encode every visually confirmed emphasis boundary, including partial-word emphasis such as the -bar of mitteilbar. The reading source should preserve or explicitly depart from those boundaries, and exact German commentary quotations should be generated or mechanically checked against it.

Plain-text equality is insufficient. A transcription can contain every word and still erase Benjamin’s argumentative structure by dropping emphasis.

Implementation gate: the memos establish the omission and record representative emphases, but the source files must contain a page-by-page complete emphasis transcription before the public display changes. The final verification must compare inline boundaries, not merely text content.

Recommended public and internal record

The compact public apparatus should remain selective but fully disclose this edition’s departures from its declared base:

  • ¶3: in] in einer Sprache GS conj.; in T1–T4, Reclam.
  • ¶14: 1:11 ED em.] 1:14 GS; 1:11 Jephcott, Reclam, with a short explanation of the biblical ambiguity.
  • Textual note: describe GS as a T1-based critical text; explain the marked Namen and Genetiv forms; state that emphasis follows GS except where recorded.

The internal collation.md should be exhaustive: all sixteen ED/GS divergences, all three GS conjectures, the double-underlining report, later-witness conflicts, quote synchronization, and the electronic-witness genealogy.
