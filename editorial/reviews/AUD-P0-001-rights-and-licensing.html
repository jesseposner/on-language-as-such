Rights and licensing across jurisdictions

Published · interim Issue: AUD-P0-001 Priority: P0 · publication risk Opened: 2026-07-12 Affected editions: 2026.07.12–2026.07.12.3

The question before the edition

The site repeatedly states that Benjamin's German essay is in the public domain because he died in 1940. That is true in Germany and many life-plus-seventy jurisdictions, but copyright is territorial. The essay's 1955 first publication creates a serious possibility that it remains protected in the United States through 2050.

Option B was published in Edition 2026.07.12.3. The commissioned research and approved scope are synthesized in AUD-P0-001A: Rights research findings and recommendation. The complete edition remains online; no clearance outreach or counsel engagement has been authorized.

Facts presently established

  1. Walter Benjamin was a German author who died in 1940.
  2. The essay was composed 4–11 November 1916, circulated privately under restrictions, and remained unpublished in Benjamin's lifetime. Its first documented print publication was in West Germany in Schriften, vol. II (Suhrkamp, 1955), pp. 401–419. The set has been dated to mid-October; the exact day remains unverified.
  3. German copyright ordinarily runs for the author's life plus seventy years and expires at the end of the relevant calendar year. On that rule, Benjamin's authorial copyright expired there after 2010.
  4. The United States restored copyright in qualifying foreign works under the Uruguay Round Agreements Act when they remained protected in their source country on the applicable restoration date.
  5. Benjamin's work remained protected in Germany on 1996-01-01, the relevant restoration date for many eligible works.
  6. A restored 1955 publication would ordinarily receive the remainder of a ninety-five-year US publication term, ending after 2050.
  7. A new translation may carry copyright in the translator's original expression while still being a derivative use of the underlying protected work.

Working legal hypothesis: the German essay and a full new translation are likely still protected in the United States, through 2050, unless a fact about publication, eligibility, ownership, permission, or another doctrine changes the analysis.

What remains to be verified

  • The exact first-publication record, including whether publication occurred in the United States within thirty days of foreign publication.
  • The essay's historical US copyright status before restoration and which URAA eligibility basis applies.
  • The present US rights holder or administrator for the German essay and English translation rights.
  • Whether any existing license or permission covers this publication.
  • Whether the intended audience and distribution should be treated as US-facing, worldwide, or jurisdiction-limited.
  • Whether continued display during rights research is an acceptable risk.

These are questions for documentary research and, for a reliable publication decision, qualified legal advice. The audit itself cannot conclusively determine them.

Why the issue is P0

  • The site reproduces the complete German text rather than short quotations.
  • It publishes a complete new translation, the paradigmatic form of a derivative work.
  • The public-domain representation may invite downstream reuse by readers who rely on it.
  • The CC BY notice may appear to authorize uses beyond the rights the edition can grant.
  • GitHub Pages publication and the editor's US context make US status materially relevant even if the text is free in Germany.

Qualifying the notice would correct the representation but would not, by itself, cure any lack of permission to distribute protected material.

Resolution options

A. Temporarily withdraw the complete texts

Remove the full German and English translation from the public site while retaining a project description, limited quotations where justified, and the rights-research notice.

Tradeoff: lowest interim legal exposure, highest disruption to the edition and its readers.

B. Correct the notice immediately and conduct expedited clearance

Keep the edition online, replace every blanket public-domain claim with a territorial and under-review notice, stop representing the translation as unconditionally reusable, and promptly identify the rights holder or obtain advice.

Tradeoff: corrects misleading metadata and preserves access, but does not eliminate the risk that current distribution itself is unauthorized.

C. Seek permission for a durable worldwide edition

Identify the rights holder or administrator and request a license covering the German text, this translation, worldwide web publication, revision, archiving, and the intended downstream license.

Tradeoff: strongest long-term foundation, but may take time, cost money, impose terms, or be refused. An interim posture is still required.

D. Publish only where demonstrably public domain

Attempt to restrict the complete text by territory while presenting a reduced edition elsewhere.

Tradeoff: technically and legally complex, imperfect on static hosting, and not recommended without specialist guidance.

Provisional recommendation

Separate the immediate truthfulness problem from the underlying distribution decision:

  1. Approve an immediate correction of the blanket rights claims. No edition should tell readers that worldwide public-domain status follows simply from Benjamin's death date.
  2. Choose an interim publication posture: temporary withdrawal (A) or continued access with an explicit warning and expedited clearance (B).
  3. Pursue the durable answer: confirm the URAA analysis and identify or approach the rights administrator (C).

The legally safest interim posture is A. If continuity of public access is given greater weight, B is the minimally responsible alternative, with the explicit understanding that revised wording is not itself permission.

Decisions required

  1. For the interim, should the complete German and English texts be temporarily withdrawn, or should the edition remain online under corrected rights language while clearance proceeds?
  2. Should the project seek a formal license for worldwide publication and revision?
  3. Is CC BY reuse of the translation a non-negotiable goal, or may the eventual rights arrangement narrow downstream reuse?
  4. Should qualified counsel or a rights specialist confirm the URAA analysis before any final public statement is made?

Acceptance criteria

AUD-P0-001 can be marked resolved only when:

  • the interim publication posture is explicitly chosen;
  • the 1955 publication and URAA facts are documented;
  • the relevant rights holder or a defensible alternative conclusion is identified;
  • README, LICENSE, bibliography, colophon, and any metadata state the same accurate position;
  • the CC BY scope is stated without purporting to license rights the project does not control;
  • the public Revision History records the change;
  • the deployed site is checked against the approved language and publication posture.
