Separating Sache from Materie

Resolved Report: AUD-P1-001B Parent issue: AUD-P1-001 Prepared: 2026-07-12 Released edition: 2026.07.12.2

The question before the edition

The German distinguishes seven uses of Sache—a thing, object, affair, or subject matter—from three uses of Materie, material substance. Before this decision, the English rendered all ten as “matter.” The governing distinction was already approved in principle; this review determined the exact English at each of the seven Sache sites.

Option A was explicitly approved, verified, and released in Edition 2026.07.12.2. The seven reading-text replacements, repeated commentary quotations, translator’s note, and public records agree.

Why the distinction matters

7Sache sites: two in ¶7, five in ¶16.
3Materie sites: ¶12, ¶14, and ¶18.
10Former English “matter” renderings across both source words.

“Magic of matter” in ¶12 directly translates Magie der Materie. Extending “matter” backward into the bourgeois communication model of ¶7 and forward into the word–thing relation of ¶16 makes those passages sound more materially and alchemically charged than their German source vocabulary warrants.

The correction is not mechanically simple. In ¶7, Sache is detachable subject matter within a means–object–addressee model. In ¶16, it is the object or thing to which a word and a name relate. Meanwhile Benjamin also writes Gegenstand (“object”) in ¶7 and Ding (“thing”) throughout ¶16. No one English invariant preserves all three German distinctions.

The canonical English as a control witness

The review now collates every site against Edmund Jephcott’s standard English translation. The working reference is the private Carpe transcript at artifacts/benjamin/text/jephcott-translation-transcript.md, checked visually against Jesse’s PDF scan of the Selected Writings text at printed pp. 65 and 69. It is used here as a control witness, not as an authority that overrides the German.

Finding: the canonical English supports a contextual policy, but a more differentiated one than the first draft of this review. It points toward “factual subject matter” in ¶7, “object” for the relation in ¶16, and “thing” for essence, being-in-itself, knowledge, and naming—while its final “language” reading is precisely the slip this edition already identified and must not inherit.

Decision 1 · ¶7 as detachable subject matter

¶7.5 · what the bourgeois model can transmit

German

er teile eine Sache anderen Menschen mit

Former

“he imparts some matter to other men”

Jephcott

“communicating factual subject matter to other men”

Approved and implemented

“he imparts factual subject matter to other men”

¶7.7 · means, object, addressee

German

Das Mittel der Mitteilung ist das Wort, ihr Gegenstand die Sache, ihr Adressat ein Mensch.

Former

“the means of imparting is the word, its object the matter, its addressee a human being.”

Jephcott transcript

“the means of communication is the word, its object factual, and its addressee a human being.”

Approved and implemented

“the means of imparting is the word, its object the subject matter, its addressee a human being.”

Why “factual subject matter” first, then “subject matter”: Jephcott’s first phrase accurately names the detachable what-about which the bourgeois model supposes words carry. The shorter noun phrase in the triad is cleaner than the transcript’s elliptical “its object factual” while keeping the same semantic field.

Live alternatives: “some subject matter” stays closer to the German article; “something” is more idiomatic but too indefinite to recur in the triad; “the thing at issue” is precise in ¶7.7 but longer and less stable.

Decision 2 · ¶16 as object, then thing

¶16.9–11 · conventionalism and word-mysticism

German

das Wort zur Sache … das Wesen der Sache … die Sache an sich

Former

“the word relates to the matter accidentally… the essence of the matter… the matter in itself has no word”

Jephcott

“an accidental relation to its object… the essence of the thing… the thing in itself has no word”

Approved and implemented

“the word relates to its object accidentally… the essence of the thing… the thing in itself has no word”

¶16.12–13 · knowledge and naming

German

Diese Erkenntnis der Sache … der Name, den der Mensch der Sache gibt

Former

“This knowledge of the matter… the name that man gives to the matter”

Jephcott

“This knowledge of the thing… the name that man gives to language” (the received slip)

Approved and implemented

“This knowledge of the thing… the name that man gives to the thing”

Why “object” only at the relation: Jephcott’s contextual move is persuasive here. A word has a relation to its object; the same passage then asks whether the word is the essence of the thing. English gains clarity by varying with the argument instead of forcing one invariant.

Why “the thing in itself”: this now follows both the formal force of an sich and the received English. The phrase does carry Kantian resonance, but so does the German construction; “the thing itself” would reduce that formal pressure. The translator’s note should still disclose that English “thing” locally covers both Ding and Sache.

Why depart from Jephcott at the final site: the received “name that man gives to language” is a documented translation error. German says the name given to the thing rests on how it imparts itself. The standard English is evidence here chiefly because it shows what must be corrected.

Resolution options

A. Canonical-informed contextual rendering · approved

Use “factual subject matter” then “subject matter” in ¶7; use “object” at the word–referent relation in ¶16 and “thing” at the remaining four sites; retain “thing in itself”; correct Jephcott’s final “language” slip against the German.

Best idiomatic and argumentative fit, with the standard English used as a control rather than copied mechanically.

B. Simplified subject matter / thing split

Use “subject matter” twice in ¶7 and “thing” throughout ¶16, including “the thing itself.”

The cleanest two-term policy, but less sensitive than Jephcott to the changing function of Sache within ¶16 and less exact about an sich.

C. Source-distinguishing “object” policy

Use “subject matter” in ¶7 and “object” throughout ¶16, including “the object in itself.”

Keeps Sache visibly apart from Ding, but collides with Gegenstand and makes the creation passage sound like later semiotics.

D. Retain “matter” with a note

Leave the current edition’s English unchanged and explain that seven sites translate Sache, not Materie.

Avoids revision but preserves a materially misleading merger inside the reading text. Not recommended.

Approved decisions

  1. Use Jephcott’s “factual subject matter” at ¶7.5 and the cleaner “subject matter” at ¶7.7.
  2. Use the canonical-informed ¶16 variation—“object” at the relation, then “thing” at the remaining four sites.
  3. Render die Sache an sich as “the thing in itself.”
  4. Treat Jephcott’s final “language” reading as a received error to correct against the German, not wording to preserve.
  5. Adopt the compact translator’s-note disclosure and synchronized commentary changes in the same implementation.
  6. Approve and implement the seven-site matrix as a unit; no locus remains open.
