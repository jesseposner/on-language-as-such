# AUD-P0-001 — United States copyright research memorandum

**Status:** Internal research memorandum; not a legal opinion
**Prepared:** 2026-07-12
**Question:** What is the likely United States copyright status of Walter Benjamin's *Über Sprache überhaupt und über die Sprache des Menschen* if its first authorized publication was in Germany in 1955, and what does that imply for this project's full German text, new English translation, and public license?

> **Important:** This memorandum is legal research for editorial risk assessment, not legal advice. It does not create an attorney-client relationship and should not be used as a substitute for advice from qualified U.S. copyright counsel, including counsel competent to address the relevant German-law ownership history.

## Executive conclusion

The project's safest present U.S. assumption is that the underlying essay **remains protected in the United States through December 31, 2050**, and will enter the U.S. public domain on January 1, 2051, unless documentary research establishes a different first-publication history or another disqualifying fact.

That conclusion has two possible routes:

1. If the essay had already entered the U.S. public domain because of a missing notice or renewal, another historical formality, or lack of national eligibility, the Uruguay Round Agreements Act (URAA), now codified at [17 U.S.C. § 104A](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section104A&num=0&edition=prelim), likely restored its U.S. copyright automatically on January 1, 1996.
2. If the essay had obtained and retained an ordinary U.S. copyright, it did not need restoration and would ordinarily have the same 95-year-from-publication endpoint.

The principal unresolved restoration fact is whether an authorized U.S. publication occurred during the 30 days after the German first publication. Such a publication would defeat URAA restoration under § 104A(h)(6)(D), but it would **not by itself establish that the work is public domain**: a properly noticed and renewed ordinary U.S. copyright could still remain in force. The public-domain outcome would require the 30-day bar **and** the absence of a surviving ordinary U.S. copyright, or another failure of the assumed facts.

The new 2026 translation does not solve the underlying-rights problem. A translation is expressly a derivative work. The translator may contribute original expression, but that new expression does not confer permission to reproduce, distribute, display, or adapt a protected source work. Pending clearance, the CC BY 4.0 scope should exclude the translation as well as the German text and all third-party material.

The URAA's reliance-party rules do not plausibly protect a project begun in 2026. They are directed to specified pre-URAA uses, copies, business interests, and derivative works. Against a non-reliance party, § 104A(d)(1) makes ordinary infringement remedies available for infringement begun after restoration; a Notice of Intent to Enforce (NIE) is not a prerequisite. An unsuccessful search for an NIE therefore would not clear this project.

## Scope, assumptions, and record status

This memorandum analyzes federal copyright in the United States. It does not decide rights in Germany, the European Union, or other territories except where source-country protection is an input to § 104A.

It proceeds on these assumptions:

- Benjamin wrote the essay in 1916.
- Benjamin lived in Germany when it was created and died in 1940. The [Deutsche Biographie record](https://www.deutsche-biographie.de/dbo010176.html) places him in Berlin during the relevant period, gives his death date as September 26, 1940, and lists the posthumous two-volume *Schriften* in 1955.
- The essay's first authorized publication was in Germany in 1955 as part of *Schriften*.
- The project and this English translation were first created/published in 2026.
- No permission from a current U.S. rights owner has yet been documented.
- No evidence presently shows that this project acquired a pre-URAA derivative work or the significant assets of a qualifying reliance party.

The existing internal issue presentation is [`editorial/reviews/AUD-P0-001-rights-and-licensing.html`](../reviews/AUD-P0-001-rights-and-licensing.html). No separate publication-history memorandum was present when this memorandum was prepared. If one is later added, its evidence should be reconciled against the factual dependencies below before relying on this conclusion.

## Governing framework

### 1. Copyright is territorial

The fact that the German authorial term has expired does not determine the U.S. term. The U.S. Copyright Office likewise cautions that there is no single “international copyright” and that protection in a country depends on that country's law. See [Copyright Office Circular 38A, *International Copyright Relations of the United States*](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38a.pdf), pp. 1–2.

### 2. URAA restoration is automatic when the statutory elements are met

[17 U.S.C. § 104A(a)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section104A&num=0&edition=prelim) says that copyright in a qualifying restored work vests automatically on the restoration date and lasts for the remainder of the U.S. term the work would have received had it never entered the U.S. public domain. The Supreme Court upheld this restoration scheme in [*Golan v. Holder*, 565 U.S. 302, 314–17, 336 (2012)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/boundvolumes/565bv.pdf), explaining that it reached foreign works excluded from U.S. protection because of national eligibility, subject-matter rules, or failures such as notice, registration, and renewal.

The Copyright Office's current operational summary is [Circular 38B, *Copyright Restoration Under the URAA*](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38b.pdf).

## Application of § 104A to the assumed 1955 German publication

### 1. Germany is the likely source country

For a published work, § 104A(h)(8)(C) identifies the source country as the eligible country of first publication. If the work was published on the same day in two or more eligible countries, it uses the eligible country with the most significant contacts.

On the stated assumption—first authorized publication in Germany in 1955—Germany is the source country. That conclusion must be revisited if a publication-history search finds an earlier publication, a same-day first publication in another eligible country, or evidence that the 1955 appearance was not the legally operative first publication.

### 2. Germany was eligible; restoration date was January 1, 1996

Germany was a Berne Convention party long before the URAA. WIPO's official [Germany treaty table](https://www.wipo.int/wipolex/en/treaties/ShowResults?code=DE) lists Berne in force from December 5, 1887. Germany therefore counted as an eligible country on the URAA's December 8, 1994 enactment date under § 104A(h)(3)(B). Germany also became a WTO member on January 1, 1995, as reflected by the [WTO's Germany member page](https://www.wto.org/english/thewto_e/countries_e/germany_e.htm).

Section 104A(h)(2)(A) sets January 1, 1996 as the restoration date when the source country was a Berne or WTO country on that date. That is the operative date for this analysis.

### 3. The essay had not expired in Germany by the restoration date

Section 104A(h)(6)(B) excludes a work that was already in the source-country public domain through expiration of its term. Germany's official English translation of its Copyright Act states in [§ 64](https://www.gesetze-im-internet.de/englisch_urhg/englisch_urhg.html) that copyright expires 70 years after the author's death, and [§ 69](https://www.gesetze-im-internet.de/englisch_urhg/englisch_urhg.html) measures the term from the end of the relevant calendar year.

On a 1940 death date, the German authorial term ran through December 31, 2010. The essay therefore remained protected in Germany on January 1, 1996. Its later German expiration does not undo a U.S. restoration that already occurred; § 104A(a)(1)(B) supplies the remainder of the U.S. term.

Historical German transition law and any special status of the posthumous edition should still be confirmed by counsel. Nothing found in this research suggests that those questions would make the underlying essay public domain in Germany by January 1, 1996.

### 4. The U.S.-public-domain predicate has more than one plausible route

Section 104A(h)(6)(C) requires that the work have been in the U.S. public domain because of:

- noncompliance with U.S. formalities, expressly including renewal, notice, or manufacturing requirements;
- lack of subject-matter protection for certain sound recordings; or
- lack of national eligibility.

The sound-recording route is irrelevant. Either of the other two routes could apply to a 1955 German essay.

Under the [Copyright Act of 1909](https://www.copyright.gov/history/1909act.pdf), publication with the prescribed notice secured copyright (§ 9); §§ 18–19 prescribed the notice's contents and placement; and § 23 provided an initial 28-year term followed by a further term only after a timely renewal application. Current § 304's historical note confirms that works first copyrighted before 1964 remained subject to the old mandatory-renewal regime. See [17 U.S.C. § 304](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section304&num=0&edition=prelim).

The precise national-eligibility route is historically complicated. The Copyright Office's [Circular 38A](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38a.pdf), p. 8, lists German-American bilateral dates, the Universal Copyright Convention's September 16, 1955 effective date, later bilateral action in 1967, and Germany's Berne and WTO dates. The notes to [17 U.S.C. § 104](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section104&num=0&edition=prelim) reproduce the 1967 German proclamation and its account of earlier reciprocal rights and wartime interruption. The exact day of the 1955 publication, the claimant's status, the notice in the volume, registration, and renewal records are therefore needed to identify the historical route confidently.

For the present risk decision, the distinction is not decisive:

- If the work was U.S.-eligible but lost protection because of notice, renewal, or another formality, § 104A(h)(6)(C)(i) is designed to restore it.
- If it was excluded for lack of national eligibility, § 104A(h)(6)(C)(iii) is designed to restore it.
- If it obtained and never lost a valid U.S. copyright, it remains protected by the ordinary term rather than by restoration.

### 5. Author/national connection appears satisfied

Section 104A(h)(6)(D) requires at least one author or rightholder who was a national or domiciliary of an eligible country when the work was created. Benjamin's documented Berlin residence through 1916 strongly supports German domicile at creation. A final clearance file should nevertheless include documentary confirmation of the legally relevant nationality or domicile and address any state-succession nuance rather than relying only on biography.

### 6. The 30-day U.S.-publication condition is the key unresolved fact

For a published work, § 104A(h)(6)(D) also requires first publication in an eligible country **and no publication in the United States during the following 30 days**. An authorized U.S. publication on any of the following 30 days would therefore be fatal to restoration.

A same-day U.S. publication should also be treated conservatively as disqualifying unless counsel identifies controlling authority to the contrary. It would at minimum create a serious question whether the work was legally first published abroad. The Copyright Office's [historical 1967 Compendium](https://www.copyright.gov/comp3/1967version/Compendium-I-1967.pdf), § 8.1.1.II, generally treated same-day U.S. and foreign publication as first publication in the United States for 1909 Act administration, while expressly reserving context-specific exceptions. Section 104A itself uses “the 30-day period following” foreign publication, and this research did not locate a controlling decision squarely parsing a day-zero simultaneous publication under that clause. The factual search should therefore cover the German release day as well as the next 30 days.

“Publication” is a term of art, and the governing formulation must fit a 1955 event. [Circular 38B](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38b.pdf), p. 5, says that for pre-1978 works a general publication may be the unrestricted sale, placing on sale, or public distribution of one or more copies, while a limited publication to a limited audience, for a limited purpose, and with restrictions may not qualify. The modern definition in [17 U.S.C. § 101](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section101&num=0&edition=prelim) similarly focuses on public distribution of copies by sale, transfer, rental, lease, or lending and certain public offers to distribute; public performance or display alone is not publication. The inquiry is therefore not merely whether a copy crossed the Atlantic or was privately held in the United States. It is whether a legally sufficient, authorized public distribution or offer occurred here on the release day or in the statutory window.

This creates the following decision matrix:

| 1955/1983 facts | URAA consequence | Likely 2026 U.S. status |
|---|---|---|
| First German publication; no U.S. publication in following 30 days; work protected in Germany in 1996; U.S. public domain because of formalities or national eligibility | Restoration likely on 1996-01-01 | Protected through 2050 |
| First German publication; no 30-day U.S. publication; ordinary U.S. copyright valid and continuously maintained | Restoration unnecessary | Protected through 2050 |
| U.S. publication within 30 days; ordinary U.S. copyright valid and renewed | Restoration barred, but ordinary copyright continues | Protected through 2050 |
| U.S. publication within 30 days; no surviving ordinary U.S. copyright | Restoration barred and no alternative federal term shown | Potentially public domain; requires counsel-supported proof |
| First publication was not in 1955 Germany, or Germany was not the legal source country | Current model fails | Recalculate from established facts |
| Work had expired in its actual source country by its restoration date | Restoration unavailable under § 104A(h)(6)(B) | Status depends on any ordinary U.S. copyright and the corrected history |

The fourth row is the principal path to the site's desired U.S. public-domain conclusion, but no evidence presently establishes it.

### 7. The Alien Property Custodian exception should be checked, but appears remote

Section 104A(a)(2) excludes a work ever owned or administered by the Alien Property Custodian if the restored copyright would be government-owned. No such fact has been identified. Because Benjamin was a German author whose estate history crosses the Second World War, a final title search should record that this exception was checked rather than silently assume it away.

## U.S. duration if restored or continuously protected

Section 104A(a)(1)(B) grants the remainder of the term the work would have received had it never entered the U.S. public domain. For a work whose copyright was secured by publication in 1955, [17 U.S.C. § 304](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section304&num=0&edition=prelim) produces a total term of 95 years. [17 U.S.C. § 305](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section305&num=0&edition=prelim) carries the term to the end of the calendar year.

The resulting date is:

- **U.S. copyright through:** December 31, 2050
- **Expected U.S. public-domain date:** January 1, 2051

[Circular 38B](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38b.pdf), pp. 2–3, reaches the same general 95-year result for qualifying pre-1978 restored works. No additional term is awarded for the years during which the work may have been in the U.S. public domain. *Golan*, 565 U.S. at 315–16.

## Translation and derivative-work implications

### 1. A translation is expressly a derivative work

[17 U.S.C. § 101](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section101&num=0&edition=prelim) expressly includes a translation in the definition of “derivative work.” [17 U.S.C. § 106(2)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section106&num=0&edition=prelim) reserves to the copyright owner the exclusive right to prepare derivative works. Posting the full German source also implicates reproduction, distribution, and public-display rights under § 106(1), (3), and (5).

The fact that a translation contains substantial original choices does not authorize use of the source. [17 U.S.C. § 103(b)](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section103&num=0&edition=prelim) limits a derivative-work copyright to the new material and says it implies no exclusive right in the preexisting material.

Section 103(a) adds a separate risk: protection for a derivative work using protected preexisting material does not extend to any part in which that material was used unlawfully. The statutory revision notes use an unauthorized translation as the paradigm. The exact effect on which portions of this translator's expression would remain enforceable is not something this memorandum can determine; it is enough to reject the assumption that originality in the translation cures missing authorization.

### 2. CC BY cannot grant rights the project does not own

A public license can grant only rights held or authorized by the licensor. A CC BY notice on the translation cannot grant users permission in Benjamin's underlying protected expression. More seriously, if authorization to prepare the translation is absent, § 103(a) makes the project's ability to assert and sublicense even its new derivative expression legally uncertain.

Accordingly, the current scope should not list the translation as unconditionally CC BY while clearance remains unresolved. If permission is obtained, the permission must expressly cover not just creation and web publication but the intended downstream CC BY sublicensing, revision, archiving, repository distribution, and worldwide availability.

### 3. Fair use should not be treated as clearance on the present record

[17 U.S.C. § 107](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title17-section107&num=0&edition=prelim) requires a case-specific balance of purpose, nature, amount, and market effect. The project's scholarly commentary and criticism are favorable facts. But the site supplies the entire German essay and an entire English translation, and a full translation is the use expressly reserved by § 106(2). Potential substitution for authorized text and translation markets would also matter. This memorandum therefore does not conclude that fair use is unavailable, but it would be unsafe to use fair use as the publication-clearance theory without counsel's fact-specific analysis.

## Reliance parties, NIEs, and the 2026 project

### 1. The statutory reliance status is historical and narrow

Section 104A(h)(4) defines a reliance party by reference to pre-eligibility acts or copies, or qualifying succession to such a person's derivative work or significant assets. Because Germany was a Berne member when the URAA was enacted, it was treated as becoming eligible on December 8, 1994. See § 104A(h)(3) and its statutory notes.

The project began in 2026. On the known facts it did not itself undertake the relevant conduct or acquire copies before Germany became eligible. Merely using a copy later obtained from a person or website that may have made earlier uses does not establish statutory succession. No sale or disposition of a protected pre-URAA derivative work or of a qualifying person's significant assets has been identified.

**Conclusion:** absent presently unknown evidence of qualifying succession, this project is not a reliance party.

### 2. No NIE is needed to enforce against a non-reliance party

Section 104A(d)(1) makes Chapter 5 remedies available against a non-reliance party for infringement commenced on or after restoration. It does not condition that enforcement on an NIE. The filing/direct-notice and 12-month grace rules in § 104A(c) and (d)(2) address enforcement **against reliance parties**.

The Copyright Office states the same rule in [Circular 38B](https://www.copyright.gov/circs/circ38b.pdf), pp. 2, 4–5: restored copyright is automatic, and an owner may enforce against a non-reliance party without filing an NIE. The Office's [URAA/NIE page](https://www.copyright.gov/gatt.html) is useful for searching filed notices, but an absent title or author is not a safe harbor for a 2026 user.

### 3. The “existing derivative work” accommodation does not apply

Section 104A(d)(3) lets a reliance party continue exploiting a qualifying derivative work created before the URAA's enactment (or before later source-country eligibility), subject to reasonable compensation. This translation was created in 2026. It is not an “existing derivative work” within that paragraph.

*Golan* describes the same limited structure: pre-URAA reliance parties received notice/grace protections, and pre-URAA derivative works could continue only under the statutory compensation mechanism. 565 U.S. at 316–17. Nothing in that accommodation extends to a newly made 2026 translation.

## Ownership and enforcement questions not answered by term research

Section 104A(b) says initial ownership of a restored copyright is determined by source-country law. It does not identify today's owner. A reliable clearance requires the chain from the initial German rightholder through inheritance, publishing agreements, assignments, exclusive licenses, and any current administrator.

Neither of the following proves freedom to use:

- inability to identify an owner after a quick search; or
- absence of the essay from the Copyright Office's NIE index.

The United States has no general orphan-works exception that licenses public web distribution merely because an owner is difficult to find. *Golan* specifically acknowledged the orphan-works difficulty while treating its solution as a matter for Congress. 565 U.S. at 334–36.

## Alternative outcomes and what would change the conclusion

1. **Confirmed no U.S. publication within 30 days:** This materially strengthens the URAA conclusion. If all other assumed facts hold, treatment as protected through 2050 becomes the strongly preferred conclusion.
2. **Confirmed U.S. publication within 30 days:** Restoration is likely unavailable. Research must then establish whether the publication carried a valid notice and whether the 1983 renewal was timely. Protection through 2050 remains possible.
3. **Confirmed 30-day U.S. publication plus no surviving ordinary copyright:** This is the strongest route to a present U.S. public-domain conclusion, but should be documented and reviewed by counsel before publication relies on it.
4. **Earlier first publication:** Recalculate both source country and U.S. term. A publication year earlier than 1955 could materially advance the U.S. expiration date.
5. **A valid permission or license:** Permission may solve current publication risk even while term remains. It must cover the actual acts and territories and, if desired, downstream CC BY licensing.
6. **A demonstrable pre-URAA derivative or reliance-party succession:** This could affect remedies or continued exploitation of that historical derivative. It would not automatically authorize this independently created 2026 translation.
7. **Counsel-supported fair-use conclusion:** This could supply a separate defense for specified uses, but should be tied to the exact presentation, amount, audience, market evidence, and revision plan rather than stated generically.

## Factual dependencies and recommended clearance record

Before treating the U.S. status as finally determined, obtain and preserve:

1. **1955 first-edition evidence**
   - title page, verso/copyright page, imprint, table of contents, and the essay's opening/closing pages;
   - exact publication date, not only year;
   - publisher, editors, claimant/proprietor, printing location, and every copyright notice;
   - evidence that the publication was authorized by the estate or initial rightholder.
2. **Publication-history evidence**
   - searches of the German National Library, WorldCat, Library of Congress, publisher archives, periodical indexes, and Benjamin archival catalogues for any pre-1955 appearance;
   - evidence of same-day publication in another country;
   - U.S. catalogues, advertisements, distributor records, reviews, offers, or editions during the first 30 days, analyzed under the legal publication definition rather than mere physical importation.
3. **Historical U.S. records**
   - Copyright Office registration and renewal searches under the German title, English title variants, Benjamin, the editors, the estate, and the publisher;
   - inspection of relevant Catalog of Copyright Entries volumes and renewal records;
   - a record of the 1955 U.S.–Germany eligibility analysis and any formalities that were or were not satisfied;
   - a search for Alien Property Custodian administration.
4. **URAA records**
   - search the Copyright Office NIE lists by all title, author, editor, estate, and publisher variants;
   - record explicitly that an absent NIE is not dispositive for a non-reliance party.
5. **Ownership/permission evidence**
   - German-law initial ownership and inheritance analysis;
   - publishing contracts, assignments, and current administration;
   - written permission covering the source text, translation right, worldwide web distribution, repository copies, archives, revision, and the intended downstream license.
6. **Project provenance**
   - dates on which the German text and translation were created and first published;
   - the source copies used;
   - any claimed acquisition or succession from an older project, with contracts and asset records.

## What can and cannot responsibly be concluded without counsel

### Can be concluded for editorial risk management

- The worldwide public-domain representation should be removed.
- The German authorial term and U.S. term are different questions.
- On the assumed 1955 German first publication, § 104A presents a substantial and likely route to U.S. protection through 2050.
- The missing 30-day publication fact prevents a definitive restoration conclusion.
- Even proof of 30-day U.S. publication would not alone prove public-domain status.
- A 2026 full translation is a derivative work and does not fit § 104A(d)(3)'s historical derivative exception.
- On known facts, the project is not a reliance party and cannot rely on the absence of an NIE.
- A disclosure or reservation corrects the misleading license claim but does not itself authorize continued publication.

### Cannot be concluded reliably without additional evidence and counsel

- that every § 104A element is conclusively met or conclusively fails;
- that the 1955 volume created, lost, renewed, or continuously retained an ordinary U.S. copyright;
- that no qualifying U.S. publication occurred within the statutory 30-day window;
- who owns or administers the present U.S. rights;
- whether any estate/publisher agreement authorizes this translation or downstream CC licensing;
- whether the complete present site is fair use;
- whether continued publication during research presents an acceptable legal risk; or
- that any proposed public wording itself cures unauthorized reproduction, distribution, display, or adaptation.

## Recommended public wording pending clearance

These are scope corrections, not permission and not a recommendation to keep the full texts online. The legally safest interim option remains temporary withdrawal of the complete German and English texts until status or permission is confirmed.

If the edition remains online while clearance proceeds, use language at least this precise and remove every inconsistent “public domain” or blanket CC BY statement.

### Short rights notice for the site, README, and colophon

> **Interim rights notice (U.S. status under review):** The authorial copyright in Walter Benjamin's underlying German essay is understood to have expired in Germany and in some other jurisdictions. The essay may remain protected in the United States through December 31, 2050. No permission to reproduce, distribute, translate, adapt, or otherwise use any protected underlying or third-party material is granted by this notice.

### Conservative repository-license scope

> **License scope pending clearance:** CC BY 4.0 applies only to Jesse Posner's independently created commentary, annotations, epilegomena, and page design, and only to the extent he owns or is authorized to license those rights. It does not apply to Walter Benjamin's German essay; to this English translation while authorization of the underlying work remains unresolved; to quotations from third-party translations; or to any other third-party material. No rights in excluded material are granted.

Do not say that CC BY applies to the translation merely “to the extent authorized.” That reservation may prevent an overbroad promise, but it still leaves readers unable to determine what they may reuse and understates the § 103(a) problem. Excluding the translation pending clearance is clearer and more conservative.

### Wording after permission, only if the grant actually supports CC BY

> The English translation is © 2026 Jesse Posner and licensed under CC BY 4.0. Walter Benjamin's underlying German essay is used by permission and is excluded from the CC BY license except to the extent the rights holder expressly states otherwise. Third-party quotations and other identified third-party material are also excluded.

That post-clearance wording should be used only if the signed grant permits preparation of this translation, worldwide publication and revision, and sublicensing to the public on CC BY 4.0 terms. If the grant is narrower, the public notice must mirror the grant rather than the project's preferred license.

## Recommended present decision

For AUD-P0-001, adopt the following research-backed posture unless counsel advises otherwise:

1. Treat the underlying essay as U.S.-protected through December 31, 2050.
2. Correct all blanket public-domain and blanket CC BY statements immediately.
3. Exclude both the underlying German text and the 2026 translation from CC BY pending clearance.
4. Choose explicitly between temporary withdrawal and continued online display with awareness that corrected wording does not cure missing permission.
5. Commission the documentary publication/renewal/title search above and have U.S. copyright counsel review the resulting file.
6. If durable worldwide publication and CC BY reuse are project goals, seek written permission broad enough to support both.
