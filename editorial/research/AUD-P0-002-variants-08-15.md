# AUD-P0-002 research memo: variants 08–15 (¶¶11–26)

**Status:** research complete; no textual changes authorized or implemented
**Prepared:** 2026-07-12
**Scope:** proposed variants 08–15, plus an independent sweep of the German in ¶¶11–26
**Base under the approved framework:** Walter Benjamin, *Gesammelte Schriften* (GS) II.1, pp. 140–157

## Executive conclusion

The page images support six straightforward restorations to GS: variants 08 and 11–15. The two difficult cases require changes to the initial audit recommendation:

1. **Genesis should follow GS `1,14`, but disclose the genuine `1,11` editorial tradition.** GS visually prints `(1,3; 1,14)`, as do textlog and the Project Gutenberg transcription. Edmund Jephcott's 1978 English and Fred Lönker's 2019 Reclam German edition instead give 1:11. Neither number resolves every part of Benjamin's sentence: 1:14 contains the literal Luther formula `Es werden Lichter`, but its larger creation act includes `machte` at 1:16; 1:11 is a creation act without `machte` or `nannte`, but begins `Es lasse die Erde ...`, not `Es werde`. Because GS is the declared base, 1:14 is not meaningless, and no manuscript evidence for 1:11 was found, the reading text should retain 1:14 and report 1:11 in the apparatus. The current commentary's claim that 1:11 “alone fits the argument” is too strong and should be replaced by a note explaining the ambiguity.
2. **Do not emend `der menschliche Namen` to `Name`.** `Namen` is not safely dismissible as malformed German. GS prints it; the GS editorial report does not flag the locus; Lönker's 2019 Reclam edition retains it; and, most decisively, Benjamin uses the same nominative form again at GS p. 155: `in denen der Namen schon welkte`. The Leibniz Institute for the German Language describes `der Namen` as a less-prevalent nominative variant produced by stem reanalysis, while Duden gives `der Name` as the current standard form. The appropriate critical action is to restore GS `Namen` and gloss the marked morphology, not silently normalize it.

The independent word-level and visual sweep found **no additional ED/GS divergence in ¶¶11–26** beyond variants 08–15 (and variant 07, the comma in `Geist, und`, which is assigned to the preceding pass). It did find the second nominative `der Namen` noted above, which is not a divergence because the current edition already preserves it.

## Variant-by-variant findings

### V08 · ¶11 · GS p. 146

**ED/TL:** `der sprachlich existentester. d. h. fixierteste Ausdruck`
**GS:** `der sprachlich existenteste, d. h. fixierteste Ausdruck`

**Evidence.** The GS page image clearly has `existenteste` and a comma before `d. h.`. RJ printed p. 17 independently presents the same morphology and punctuation. EJ pp. 66–67 renders the coordinated superlatives as “linguistically most existent” and “most fixed,” confirming the syntax, though not serving as a German punctuation witness. TL and PG both preserve the malformed `existentester.` sequence.

**Recommendation:** adopt GS exactly.
**Confidence:** **High.** This is electronic corruption, not a plausible substantive variant.

### V09 · ¶14 · GS p. 148 · Genesis 1:14 / 1:11

**ED/EJ/RJ:** Genesis 1:3; 1:11
**GS/TL/PG:** 1:3; 1:14

**Visual and transmission evidence.** GS p. 148 unambiguously prints `(1,3; 1,14)`. GS-App identifies T1 as copy text and does not record a conjecture at this locus. TL and PG also read 1:14. EJ p. 68 and RJ p. 20 read 1:11. The 1955 reading is unknown.

**Biblical control.** Benjamin's cited source was the 1888 Luther Bible; GS-App pp. 935–936 identifies it and separately indexes the Genesis verbs. The present [Deutsche Bibelgesellschaft Luther text](https://www.die-bibel.de/bibel/LU17/GEN.1.1) preserves the relevant structure:

- 1:3: `Es werde Licht`.
- 1:11: `Die Erde lasse ...`; the act produces vegetation without a subsequent `machte` or `nannte`.
- 1:14: `Es werden Lichter`; the act later says `Gott machte` at 1:16 and contains no naming.

This explains the split rather than eliminating it. **1:14 best matches Benjamin's quoted verbal formula** `Es werde`; **1:11 best matches his claim that only the fiat, and not making or naming, occurs in the creation act**. Jephcott and Lönker evidently prioritize the latter; GS preserves the former and is the only reading presently tied to the manuscript-based edition.

**Recommendation:** in a GS-based critical text, display normalized `(Genesis 1:3; 1:14)` and use 1:14 in the English. Add an apparatus note `EJ, RJ: 1:11` and replace the current commentary's categorical defense of 1:11 with the two-sided explanation above. Do not call either reading a simple biblical “correction.”
**Confidence:** **Medium-high** for retaining 1:14 under the approved base-text policy; **high** that the conflict must be disclosed.

### V10 · ¶16 · GS p. 149 · `der menschliche Namen`

**ED:** `der menschliche Name`
**GS/TL/PG/RJ:** `der menschliche Namen`

**Primary and edition evidence.** The GS page image clearly prints `Namen`. GS-App uses T1 as copy text and lists no conjecture at this locus. RJ printed p. 21 deliberately retains `der menschliche Namen` despite otherwise modernizing orthography (`dass`, `muss`, `Schluss`). TL and PG agree. EJ p. 69 has the grammatically neutral English `the human name`.

**Internal authorial control.** At GS p. 155, Benjamin again writes a nominative singular on `-n`: `in denen der Namen schon welkte`. ED correctly preserves that second occurrence at ¶21, and EJ p. 73 translates it as singular “name.” Two semantically ordinary nominatives in one short essay make a one-off typographical explanation improbable.

**Grammatical and historical status.** Current Duden gives the prevailing paradigm `der Name / den Namen / dem Namen / des Namens` ([entry](https://www.duden.de/rechtschreibung/Name_Ruf_Benennung_Bezeichnung), [declension table](https://www.duden.de/deklination/substantive/Name_Ruf_Benennung_Bezeichnung)). The Leibniz Institute's grammis account, however, explicitly lists `der Name (der Namen)` among mixed-declension nouns whose `-n` has been reanalyzed as part of the stem; it says the `-e` form is standard-language predominant, not that the `-n` nominative is impossible ([Kasusflexion, lines 131–151](https://grammis.ids-mannheim.de/kontrastive-grammatik/3635)). LEO likewise labels `Namen` as a rare nominative beside `Name` ([dictionary entry](https://dict.leo.org/german-english/der%20Namen)).

**Recommendation:** restore GS `der menschliche Namen`; do not list it as an emendation. Add a compact linguistic note that `der Namen` is a marked, less-prevalent nominative variant and point to the confirming p. 155 occurrence. The English `the human name` remains unchanged.
**Confidence:** **High.** The initial proposal to retain ED `Name` as a declared emendation should be withdrawn.

### V11 · ¶16 · GS p. 150

**ED/TL/PG/RJ:** `Auf die Sprache der Dinge selbst; aus denen ...`
**GS:** `Auf die Sprache der Dinge selbst, aus denen ...`

**Evidence.** The GS image clearly has a comma. Its syntax is continuous: the relative clause `aus denen ... das Wort Gottes hervorstrahlt` modifies `Dinge`, and the main predicate follows: `ist diese Empfängnis gerichtet`. The semicolon in ED/TL/PG separates the preposed complement from its predicate and is syntactically disruptive. RJ printed p. 23 nevertheless retains the semicolon, a real later-witness conflict that may reflect an unavailable earlier edition. EJ p. 69 recasts the sentence and cannot decide the German mark.

**Recommendation:** adopt the GS comma. Record RJ's semicolon in the private collation, but no public apparatus entry is necessary unless the edition exposes later-witness punctuation variants generally.
**Confidence:** **High** for a GS-based text; the GS syntax outweighs the unexplained RJ conflict.

### V12 · ¶18 · GS p. 151

**ED/TL/PG:** `in den Namen Das ist also`
**GS/RJ:** `in den Namen. Das ist also`

**Evidence.** The GS image clearly has a full stop. RJ printed p. 24 independently supplies it. EJ p. 70 likewise begins a new English sentence after “name.” ED's own commentary quotation already supplies the stop.

**Recommendation:** restore the full stop in the German display and synchronize every duplicate quotation.
**Confidence:** **High.** The missing mark is a shared electronic transcription defect.

### V13 · ¶19 · GS p. 154

**ED/TL/PG:** `Diese Unmittelbarkeit »in der Mitteilung`
**GS:** `Diese Unmittelbarkeit in der Mitteilung`

**Evidence.** The GS page image has no opening guillemet. There is no corresponding closing mark, and the phrase is ordinary syntax rather than a quotation. EJ p. 72 has an unquoted continuous sentence. A later scholarly quotation also reproduces the GS reading without the guillemet ([Zimmermann 2021, quoted passage](https://archiv.ub.uni-heidelberg.de/artdok/8849/1/Zimmermann_Das_Brautphoto_Carl_Albert_Dauthendeys_2021.pdf)). RJ's public sample ends before this locus.

**Recommendation:** remove the guillemet.
**Confidence:** **High.** It is an orphaned electronic character.

### V14 · ¶21 · GS p. 155

**ED/TL/PG:** `sie hervor-, In der Sprache`
**GS:** `sie hervor. In der Sprache`

**Evidence.** The GS image unambiguously has a full stop followed by capital `In`. EJ p. 73 likewise makes two English sentences: God calls things into being by their proper names; in human language they are overnamed. Reputable later scholarship quoting this passage follows the GS boundary ([example, pp. 197–198 of the linked study](https://files01.core.ac.uk/download/pdf/131214499.pdf)). RJ's sample does not reach this locus.

**Recommendation:** replace the stray hyphen/comma with a full stop.
**Confidence:** **High.** The electronic reading is not defensible punctuation.

### V15 · ¶22 · GS p. 156

**ED/TL/PG:** `eine Obersetzung der Sprache der Dinge`
**GS:** `eine Übersetzung der Sprache der Dinge`

**Evidence.** The GS image clearly has `Übersetzung`. The entire surrounding argument is about translation, and EJ p. 73 renders “a translation of the language of things.” A later academic quotation of GS p. 156 also has `Übersetzung` ([quotation and locator](https://core.ac.uk/download/pdf/11595803.pdf)). RJ's sample does not reach this locus.

**Recommendation:** restore `Übersetzung`.
**Confidence:** **High.** `Obersetzung` is an OCR/transcription substitution.

## Independent ¶¶11–26 sweep

### Procedure

1. Extracted the German for ED ¶¶11–26 from `index.html`.
2. Compared it token by token with the GS OCR for printed pp. 146–157 after removing line-end hyphenation and running heads.
3. Inspected every reported difference against the GS page image, rejecting OCR artifacts such as `identisdl`, `sdlOn`, `Word`, and false word breaks.
4. Re-read the complete GS page images from p. 146 through the end of p. 157 rather than checking only the eight target strings.
5. Checked duplicated German quotations in the current commentary where searches exposed them.

### Result

No additional wording or punctuation divergence was found beyond:

- V07, the spurious comma in `Geist, und` at the beginning of ¶11;
- V08–V15 as discussed above; and
- display normalizations already inherent in the edition, such as adding the label `Genesis` and using colon rather than comma between chapter and verse.

The sweep did reveal two synchronization consequences:

- The ¶16 commentary blockquote currently repeats ED `der menschliche Name`; it must change with the adopted German reading or explicitly label itself as normalized.
- The ¶14 commentary currently argues categorically for 1:11 and calls 1:14 an inherited electronic error. The GS image proves that account wrong: 1:14 is the manuscript-based critical-edition reading, even if one ultimately prefers the Jephcott/Lönker emendation.
