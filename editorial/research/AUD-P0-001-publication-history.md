# AUD-P0-001 publication-history memorandum

**Research date:** 2026-07-12
**Scope:** Publication history of Walter Benjamin's *Über Sprache überhaupt und über die Sprache des Menschen* insofar as it bears on the factual predicates of a United States copyright analysis.
**Status:** Documentary research memorandum; not legal advice. No conclusion here substitutes for a qualified analysis of 17 U.S.C. § 104A or a rights clearance.

## Executive conclusion

The best available documentary record supports the following account:

1. Benjamin composed the essay between 4 and 11 November 1916, began a fair copy on 11 November, and delivered the work to Gershom Scholem in December 1916. Benjamin then allowed a small, named circle to read, borrow, copy, or hear it under restrictions. This was controlled manuscript circulation, not an ordinary offer of copies to the public. The editors of *Gesammelte Schriften* II.3 and Harvard's *Selected Writings* both state that the essay remained unpublished during Benjamin's lifetime.
2. The first documented publisher-issued printing is Walter Benjamin, “Über Sprache überhaupt und über die Sprache des Menschen,” in *Schriften*, vol. II, edited by Theodor W. Adorno and Gretel Adorno with the collaboration of Friedrich Podszus (Frankfurt am Main: Suhrkamp, 1955), pp. 401–419. The German National Library's scan of the original table of contents is headed “Inhalt des zweiten Bandes,” places the abbreviated entry “Über die Sprache” at p. 401, and places the next work at p. 420. This resolves the volume as **II**, not I.
3. The German National Library establishes the year, publisher, place, volume, and extent, but gives no day or month. A scholarly reconstruction based on Adorno–Scholem and publisher correspondence says the two-volume set appeared in **mid-October 1955**. No exact calendar day was established in this research.
4. No evidence of an earlier print publication of this essay was found. The positive editorial statements and the negative catalog searches make 1955 highly probable as the first printing. That conclusion is not the same as proving that no obscure excerpt, proof, or privately printed issue existed between 1940 and 1955.
5. No evidence was found of a United States edition, co-imprint, or publication within thirty days of the German publication. US-held catalog records describe only the Frankfurt Suhrkamp edition; their fixed fields identify Germany as the country of publication. Exact searches of both halves of the official 1955 *Catalog of Copyright Entries* found no entry combining Benjamin with Suhrkamp or *Schriften*. These are meaningful negative results, but **they do not prove absence of US publication**. Registration was not the same thing as publication, imported copies may not produce a separate catalog record, the exact German publication day is unknown, and no Suhrkamp distribution ledger or contemporaneous US trade record was obtained.
6. The 1955 edition was plainly a regular commercial publication. Secondary archival scholarship reports that Adorno had been designated by Benjamin to administer the literary estate, that the edition was planned with Scholem, that Benjamin's widow and son were in cooperative contact, and that Suhrkamp ultimately issued it. This strongly supports calling it the first documented publisher-issued edition. The underlying estate agreement, publishing contract, and chain of title were not inspected, so this memorandum does **not** independently prove “authorization” in the legal sense or identify the current rights holder.

For the URAA inquiry, the established publication facts are consistent with first publication in Germany in 1955 and with no presently documented US publication in the following thirty days. The latter remains an open fact requiring a professional records search, publisher/archive evidence, or both.

## Detailed findings

### 1. Composition and manuscript circulation

GS II.3 reconstructs the following chronology from Benjamin's letters and Scholem's account:

- Benjamin began an extended letter to Scholem on approximately 4 November 1916, broke it off, and reworked it into the essay.
- By 11 November the essay was complete enough for Benjamin to have begun the fair copy.
- He delivered it to Scholem when he returned to Berlin in December 1916.
- Benjamin later contemplated continuation, calling this the beginning of a larger language project, but the contemplated further parts were not completed as such.

The circulation evidence is concrete and restrictive:

- Scholem held a copy and attempted a partial Hebrew translation for private oral reading.
- Benjamin asked that Scholem lend his copy to Werner Kraft.
- Ludwig Strauß could hear or receive it only under stated conditions.
- Benjamin discussed a fixed, small number of copies and who should receive each one.
- Ernst Schoen and Herbert Belmore were individually offered or allowed access.
- In 1928 Benjamin said he had only one copy available and considered having it copied for a specific university-related purpose.

GS characterizes these copies as carefully controlled “Zirkulare” and expressly states that the early and later language essays remained unpublished during Benjamin's lifetime. The Copyright Office's general description of a pre-1978 limited publication—a limited audience, a limited purpose, and restrictions on future use—resembles this fact pattern. Whether the circulation constitutes legal “publication” under the law applicable to a particular copyright question is a legal conclusion this memorandum does not make.

### 2. First documented print publication

The first located print publication is:

> Walter Benjamin, “Über Sprache überhaupt und über die Sprache des Menschen,” in *Schriften*, vol. II, ed. Theodor W. Adorno and Gretel Adorno, with the collaboration of Friedrich Podszus (Frankfurt am Main: Suhrkamp, 1955), 401–419.

### 3. Was there an earlier publication?

#### Proven

- The essay was not published during Benjamin's lifetime, according to both GS II.3 and the Harvard editorial note.
- The 1955 *Schriften* volume contains it at pp. 401–419.

#### Strong inference

- 1955 was the first print publication. Modern scholarship consistently describes the essay as first published in that edition, and no 1940–1954 publication was located in the DNB exact-title results, WorldCat, HathiTrust, Google Books results, Internet Archive metadata, or the publication histories consulted.

#### Limits of the negative search

- DNB and WorldCat do not guarantee analytic records for essays inside collections or periodicals.
- Search did not include a page-by-page review of every German-language periodical from 1940–1954.
- No Suhrkamp/Adorno production file was inspected for advance proofs or private issues.
- The private manuscript copies and Scholem's partial Hebrew exercise were not shown to have been offered to the public.

The defensible formulation is therefore “first documented print publication, and highly probably the first publication, in 1955,” not “proved beyond possibility that no earlier publication existed.”

### 4. Was there US publication within thirty days?

#### Positive evidence located

- The only 1955 edition found has a Frankfurt/Suhrkamp imprint and German publication-country coding.
- The HathiTrust/Library of Congress-derived record has LCCN 56001669, but an LCCN and US library holding show cataloging/acquisition, not US publication.
- Jaumann dates the German set to mid-October 1955. This would place the relevant thirty-day inquiry approximately from mid-October to mid-November, but the precise interval cannot be calculated without a day.
- The later Schocken copyright page says the source collection was “Originally published in Germany by Suhrkamp Verlag, Frankfurt a. M.”

#### Negative search results

- No 1955 US publisher, co-publisher, place of publication, or distinct US edition was found in WorldCat/HathiTrust records or general exact-title searches.
- No Benjamin/Suhrkamp/*Schriften* entry was found in either half of the official 1955 *Catalog of Copyright Entries* under the searched forms.
- The publication history reports that Schocken in New York was considered as an alternative when Suhrkamp temporarily withdrew, but that Suhrkamp resumed and issued the set. No evidence of a 1955 Schocken coedition was found.

#### Why the fact remains unresolved

- A Copyright Catalog entry is not required to establish publication, and URAA restoration itself is automatic if its conditions are met.
- Imported German sheets or copies could potentially raise distribution facts without creating a separate title record; their legal significance would require advice.
- No contemporaneous US distributor catalog, invoice, advertisement, accession-date file, or publisher shipping ledger was obtained.
- The exact German release day remains unknown.

The present record supports “no US publication within thirty days has been found,” but not “no such publication occurred.”

## URAA-relevant implications

These are factual implications for further analysis, not legal conclusions:

1. The evidence points to a published literary work first issued in Germany in 1955.
2. The pre-1955 circulation was private, named, purpose-limited, and restricted. That is materially different from the unrestricted commercial distribution documented in 1955.
3. No US publication in the following thirty days is presently documented, so the thirty-day exclusion has not been triggered by any located evidence. It has also not been conclusively eliminated as a factual possibility.
4. The absence of a 1955 Copyright Catalog entry is not dispositive. Circular 38B says eligible restoration is automatic and does not depend on registration.
5. If counsel concludes that all § 104A eligibility conditions were met, Circular 38B says a pre-1978 restored work ordinarily receives the remainder of the term it would have had—generally ninety-five years from first publication. On a 1955 first-publication year, the existing audit's “through 2050” working hypothesis is arithmetically consistent. This memorandum does not determine eligibility, ownership, enforceability, or exceptions.

## Unresolved questions and recommended next evidence

1. **Exact German release date.** Obtain Suhrkamp's production ledger, publication announcement, or dated delivery record for *Schriften* in the Siegfried Unseld/Suhrkamp archive or a contemporary *Börsenblatt* listing. The present best evidence is only “mid-October 1955.”
2. **US publication/distribution in the following thirty days.** Search contemporaneous US trade catalogs, *Publishers Weekly*, Books in Print records, importer/distributor files, and Library of Congress acquisition records. A Copyright Office search report may also document registrations and NIEs, although registration silence cannot alone settle publication.
3. **Authority and chain of title.** Inspect the 1955 publishing agreement, estate correspondence, and any assignments or exclusive licenses. The publication history supports a regular estate-associated publisher edition, but does not identify the present owner or administrator.
4. **Post-1940, pre-1955 obscure printing.** Check the Suhrkamp, Adorno, Scholem, and Benjamin archival files for proofs, memorial collections, offprints, or proposed excerpts. No such publication was found in the accessible catalogs.
5. **Legal characterization of the 1916–1930s circulation.** Give the GS II.3 facts and the original correspondence to counsel; do not convert the editorial term “Zirkulare” into a legal conclusion without analysis.

### Public-domain and license claims

Nothing in this memorandum supports the unqualified statements “the German text is in the public domain” or “the original is out of copyright.” The 1955 publication record instead strengthens the need for territorial qualification and a completed US analysis. Any approved public notice should continue to distinguish:

- jurisdictions in which Benjamin's authorial term has expired;
- unresolved or potentially restored US rights in the underlying German work;
- copyright in the project's new translation and commentary; and
- the limited scope of any CC BY grant to rights actually controlled by the licensor.
