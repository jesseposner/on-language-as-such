# AUD-P0-002 research memo: variants 1–7 and ¶¶1–11 sweep

**Status:** research complete; no edition text changed
**Date:** 2026-07-12
**Scope:** the seven proposed actions numbered 1–7 in
`AUD-P0-002-german-witness-and-apparatus.html`, plus an independent sweep of
the current edition’s German ¶¶1–11
**Base-policy already approved:** *Gesammelte Schriften* II.1 (GS) as the
declared base witness; critical reading text; public apparatus; accountable
source files before display changes

## Executive finding

All seven proposed GS readings are confirmed by direct inspection of the GS
page images. Six are also supported without conflict by Fred Lönker’s 2019
Reclam critical edition. The one conflict is variant 6: GS prints the colon
after `benennt`, while Reclam and Jephcott present a full stop. Because the GS
colon is clear, is not identified as an editorial conjecture in the GS
apparatus, and the edition has already selected GS punctuation as its default,
the recommendation remains to adopt the colon.

The fresh sweep found three matters not captured by the initial fifteen-item
list:

1. **A substantive hidden GS conjecture at ¶3.** All four typescripts read
   only `in`; GS silently expands this to `in einer Sprache`. Reclam restores
   the typescript reading, and Jephcott’s English tracks it. The critical
   reading text should strongly consider departing from GS here and restoring
   the unanimous typescript reading as a declared emendation.
2. **A silent spelling modernization at ¶8.** ED and the online sources read
   `Genitiv`; GS and Reclam read `Genetiv`. The latter is a legitimate, now
   dated spelling, not malformed German. Under the approved policy of following
   GS spelling, adopt `Genetiv` or explicitly classify `Genitiv` as display
   normalization.
3. **Authorial emphasis is absent from the German display.** GS uses italics
   extensively in ¶¶1–11, including for doctrinal propositions and contrasts;
   none of the edition’s twenty-six German paragraph containers contains an
   `<em>` or `<i>` element. The accountable German sources and public display
   should encode this emphasis. This is a textual-presentation issue, not a
   word-level emendation.

The 1955 *Schriften* printing was identified bibliographically but not obtained
as page images. No conclusion below is represented as a 1955 collation.

## Confidence scale

- **High:** direct page-image evidence is clear, with no material conflict
  among the strongest accessible witnesses.
- **Medium-high:** the primary evidence is clear, but a reputable later edition
  differs or the recommendation requires departing from the declared base.
- **Medium:** the evidence is indirect, incomplete, or materially divided.

## Witnesses and method

### 1. GS II.1: page images and critical apparatus

The working scan is Walter Benjamin, “Über Sprache überhaupt und über die
Sprache des Menschen,” in *Gesammelte Schriften* II.1, ed. Rolf Tiedemann and
Hermann Schweppenhäuser (Suhrkamp, 1977), pp. 140–157. It is available through
the [Internet Archive item](https://archive.org/details/GesammelteSchriftenBd.2)
and its [direct PDF](https://archive.org/download/GesammelteSchriftenBd.2/BenjaminGs2.pdf).
The same edition is linked by the parent review through
[Kritiknetz](https://www.kritiknetz.de/images/stories/texte/walter-benjamin-gesammelte-schriften-ii.pdf).

Method:

- The PDF text layer was used only to locate passages.
- Every reading reported for GS below was checked visually in rendered images
  of printed pp. 141–146.
- The transmission and conjecture claims were checked visually on printed
  p. 935, not accepted from OCR.
- A full ¶¶1–11 word/punctuation comparison was made between ED and the GS
  searchable layer, after which every discrepancy was returned to the image.

The GS apparatus materially changes the evaluation. Printed p. 935 records:

- T1: a typescript with Benjamin’s handwritten corrections, used as the GS
  printer’s copy;
- T2–T4: three typescript copies;
- GS p. 142, line 8: `in einer Sprache` is explicitly **conjectured for `in` in
  T1–T4**;
- GS p. 143, line 21: `Einsichten. –` is explicitly conjectured for the malformed
  `Einsichten.!` in T1–T4.

Thus GS is a critical editorial text, not a neutral facsimile of one surviving
authorial document. Its own declared conjectures should enter the project’s
collation record even when the project ultimately follows them.

## Variant-by-variant findings

### Variant 1 ¶¶1: omitted `geistigen` (GS p. 141)

**ED/TL:** `seinen Inhalt mitzuteilen`
**GS/RL:** `seinen geistigen Inhalt mitzuteilen`

**Evidence**

- GS printed p. 141 clearly shows `geistigen` in the page image.
- RL sample p. 8 retains the adjective.
- J p. 62 reads “mental contents,” confirming that the adjective was present in
  the German source used by Jephcott.
- TL, Project Gutenberg, and Signaturen all omit it; this shared omission is
  evidence of a defective online transmission, not three independent readings.

**Assessment**

This is substantive, not cosmetic. The adjective returns the sentence to the
essay’s governing `geistig` vocabulary and distinguishes the content at issue
from undifferentiated content.

**Recommendation:** adopt the GS/RL reading. Revise the corresponding English
under the already approved `geistiges Wesen` policy; the minimal project-policy
rendering is “its quintessential content.”
**Confidence:** **High**.

### Variant 2 ¶1: `Idee; aber` (GS p. 141)

**ED/TL:** colon
**GS/RL:** semicolon

**Evidence**

- The GS page image unmistakably has a semicolon.
- RL sample p. 9 also has a semicolon.
- J pp. 62–63 preserves the same relation as “an idea; but this idea…”

**Assessment**

The semicolon coordinates the negative hypothetical existence with the
qualification that follows. The ED colon incorrectly presents the second
clause as an expansion or explanation of the first.

**Recommendation:** adopt the GS semicolon. No English wording change is
required because the English already has the appropriate relation.
**Confidence:** **High**.

### Variant 3 ¶2: suspended `über` construction (GS p. 141)

**ED:** supplies a first `ihm` and adds a comma after `erhalten`
**GS/TL/RL:** retains the suspended construction without those additions

**Evidence**

- The GS image reads `und über, gerade über ihm ... ist ihre Aufgabe`.
- RL sample p. 9 reproduces that wording.
- TL also has it, despite its surrounding defects.
- J p. 63 renders the thought as surviving “suspended precisely over this
  abyss,” confirming the semantic force of the suspension, though not German
  punctuation.

**Assessment**

ED’s change makes the German more ordinary but erases an intentionally poised,
self-interrupting construction at the moment Benjamin describes theory’s
hovering over an abyss. It is a silent stylistic emendation without witness
support among the sources checked.

**Recommendation:** restore GS exactly: remove the supplied first `ihm` and
the comma after `erhalten`. The clarified English may remain syntactically
smoother.
**Confidence:** **High**.

### Variant 4 ¶3: full stop after `sprachlichen Wesen` (GS p. 142)

**ED/TL:** colon
**GS/RL:** full stop

**Evidence**

- The GS page image clearly ends the sentence after `sprachlichen Wesen`.
- RL sample p. 10 starts the next sentence with `Das geistige Wesen`.
- J p. 63 likewise closes “linguistic being” and begins a new sentence.

**Assessment**

The colon in ED makes the identity claim read as the explanation of the
preceding negative definition. GS gives it the stronger status of a new
proposition.

**Recommendation:** adopt the GS full stop. Mirror the boundary in every
German quotation. The English already uses a full stop.
**Confidence:** **High**.

### Variant 5 ¶4: missing reflexive in `in sich selbst` (GS p. 142)

**ED/TL:** `in selbst`
**GS/RL:** `in sich selbst`

**Evidence**

- GS printed p. 142 clearly contains the second `sich`.
- RL sample p. 11 agrees.
- J p. 64 reads “in itself,” which requires the GS relation.
- Several scholarly quotations found in university-hosted publications also
  give `in sich selbst`; some introduce a different extra `selbst`, showing why
  quotation snippets cannot replace a witness collation.

**Assessment**

ED is ungrammatical. The first `sich` belongs to `teilt sich mit`; the second
belongs to `in sich selbst`.

**Recommendation:** restore the missing `sich` from GS. The English already
expresses the intended reflexive relation.
**Confidence:** **High**.

### Variant 6 ¶5: colon after `benennt` (GS p. 143)

**ED/TL/RL:** full stop
**GS:** colon

**Evidence**

- The GS page image clearly prints a colon before the next paragraph’s
  `Wozu benennt?`
- The GS apparatus does not identify that colon as a conjecture or variant;
  unlike its nearby conjecture at `Einsichten. –`, it is presented as the base
  text.
- RL sample p. 12 instead prints a full stop and starts the question as a new
  paragraph.
- J p. 64 also uses a full stop and paragraph break. Because J is a translation,
  that does not prove a different German punctuation mark.

**Assessment**

The evidence is genuinely divided at the editorial-practice level. The GS
colon explicitly opens the questions that follow and is coherent. RL and J
prefer a more ordinary modern paragraph boundary. No accessible 1955 image was
available to arbitrate the reception history.

**Recommendation:** adopt the GS colon under the approved base-text policy.
Record RL’s full stop in the collation file, but it does not justify an
emendation of a coherent GS reading.
**Confidence in the GS transcription:** **High**.
**Confidence in the recommendation:** **Medium-high** because RL differs.

### Variant 7 ¶11: remove comma in `Geist und Sprache` (GS p. 146)

**ED/TL:** comma after `Geist`
**GS/RL:** no comma

**Evidence**

- The GS page image clearly reads the coordinated pair without a comma.
- RL sample p. 16 agrees.
- Signaturen has also removed the comma, while TL and Project Gutenberg retain
  it. This mixed web state reinforces that the web texts are being silently
  repaired rather than independently transmitted.
- The comma has no grammatical function inside the coordinated object of
  `zwischen`.

**Assessment**

This is an electronic punctuation intrusion.

**Recommendation:** remove the comma.
**Confidence:** **High**.

## Additional findings from the ¶¶1–11 sweep

### A. ¶3: GS conjectures against all four typescripts

**ED/GS:** `Das geistige Wesen teilt sich in einer Sprache und nicht durch eine Sprache mit`
**T1–T4/RL:** elliptical `in und nicht durch eine Sprache`

GS printed p. 935 explicitly states that its words `in einer Sprache` are a
conjecture for `in` in T1–T4. This is stronger evidence than an inference from
OCR or a later quotation:

- all four recorded typescripts agree on `in`;
- T1 contains Benjamin’s handwritten corrections and is the GS printer’s copy;
- RL sample p. 10 restores the typescript reading;
- J p. 63 reads “communicates itself in, not through, a language,” closely
  tracking the elliptical German;
- the ellipsis is grammatical and sharpens the essay’s `in`/`durch` opposition.

The GS editors’ expansion is intelligible as clarification, but the present
project has chosen a *critical* reading text, not automatic reproduction of
every GS conjecture. This is exactly the kind of case that should be decided
openly.

**Recommendation:** adopt T1–T4/RL `in` as a declared departure from GS. An
English consequence would be to consider “imparts itself in, and not through,
a language,” but that translation change remains separately gated.
**Confidence:** **Medium-high**. The typescript evidence is unanimous and the
reading is coherent; the missing 1955 page and lack of direct access to T1
prevent the highest rating.

Suggested apparatus entry:

```yaml
- id: de-p03-in
  paragraph: 3
  base_witness: "GS II.1, 142"
  base_reading: "in einer Sprache"
  adopted_reading: "in"
  type: emendation
  authority: "T1–T4; RL 2019"
  rationale: "Restores the unanimous typescript reading; GS p. 935 identifies its expansion as a conjecture."
```

### B. ¶8: `Genetiv`, not silent `Genitiv`

**ED/TL/Project Gutenberg/Signaturen:** `Genitiv`
**GS/RL:** `Genetiv`

The GS page image at printed p. 144 clearly has `Genetiv`. RL sample p. 14
retains it even while modernizing general orthography. The
[Duden entry for `Genetiv`](https://www.duden.de/rechtschreibung/Genetiv)
classifies the form as dated and defines it as `Genitiv`; it is therefore an
older lexical form, not a nonsensical typo.

Under the approved policy that GS governs spelling, ED’s `Genitiv` is a silent
normalization. It should not remain invisible.

**Recommendation:** use GS/RL `Genetiv` in both the diplomatic source and the
critical reading text. If the editor prefers modern `Genitiv` for readers,
classify that choice as a documented display normalization rather than an
unmarked correction.
**Confidence:** **High**.

### C. ¶5 boundary: keep GS’s repair of malformed typescript punctuation

GS p. 935 states that its `Einsichten. –` is conjectured for `Einsichten.!` in
T1–T4. ED currently follows the sensible GS repair. This is not an ED/GS
divergence, but it belongs in the collation record because it reveals a second
place in ¶¶1–11 where the declared base already contains editorial intervention.

**Recommendation:** keep the GS repair. Record the typescript punctuation in
the internal collation; no public apparatus entry is required unless the
edition decides to expose GS’s own conjectures as well as departures from GS.
**Confidence:** **High**.

### D. Missing authorial emphasis throughout the German display

The GS page images show frequent italic emphasis in this span, including:

- the recurring contrasts `durch` / `in`;
- the thesis that every language imparts itself;
- the thesis that the linguistic essence of things is their language;
- the thesis that the linguistic essence of man is that he names things;
- the proposition about man imparting himself to God in the name;
- the Hamann quotation at the end of ¶11.

GS p. 935 adds that the passage beginning with the thesis about the linguistic
essence of things was **double-underlined in T1–T4**, confirming that at least
this emphasis is part of the transmitted authorial document rather than merely
GS house styling. RL also preserves marked emphasis in the sampled text.

ED’s German is plain text: all twenty-six `.de` paragraph containers contain
zero `<em>` or `<i>` tags. This flattens semantic contrasts that matter directly
to the essay’s argument. It is also broader than the seven local variants, so a
full-text emphasis collation should be completed by the adversarial pass.

**Recommendation:** transcribe emphasis into `german-gs.md`, carry it into
`german-reading.md`, and render it accessibly with semantic `<em>` markup. The
collation record should distinguish authorial emphasis reported by GS from any
purely editorial display styling.
**Confidence:** **High** that emphasis is missing and must be encoded;
individual full-essay ranges still require the commissioned complete sweep.

## Paragraph-sweep conclusion

The word-and-punctuation sweep of ED ¶¶1–11 against GS found:

- the seven assigned differences;
- the already-listed variant 8 in ¶11 (`existenteste, d. h.`), which belongs to
  the companion variants 8–15 pass and is not re-decided here;
- the additional `Genitiv` / `Genetiv` difference at ¶8;
- no other unlisted lexical or punctuation divergence from GS in ¶¶1–11;
- two GS conjectures disclosed by its own apparatus, one of which deserves a
  new decision;
- the systematic loss of emphasis, which a word-only collation had missed.

This conclusion is limited to the witnesses obtained. It does not claim
collation of the physical typescripts, the 1955 printing, or the forthcoming
*Werke und Nachlaß* volume 2. The official
[Walter Benjamin Online series overview](https://www.walter-benjamin-online.de/)
shows volume 2, *Philosophische und ästhetische Schriften*, without a linked
published volume; the newer critical-series treatment of this essay therefore
does not appear to be available yet.
